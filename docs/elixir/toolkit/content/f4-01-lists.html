F4 · Foundations · module 1

Lists, recursion & complexity

The list is the BEAM's fundamental sequence, and it is a linked list of cons cells, not an array. That one fact explains everything that follows: why prepending is instant, why finding the length is not, why every list function is written by recursion, and how to read the cost of an operation straight from the shape of the data.

This module builds the list from three angles — the cons-cell shape, the recursion that walks it, and the big-O cost that shape implies. The portal's lesson minutes are the running example.

A list is cons cells Three deep dives

A list is cons cells

Each cons cell is a pair: a head (one value) and a tail (a pointer to the rest of the list). [12, 8, 30] is three cells chained to the empty list []. Select an angle to see what that shape means.

The list points one way, from head to tail. Operations that work at the head are cheap; operations that need the far end are not. Everything in this module follows from that asymmetry.

Three deep dives

In order: the cons-cell shape and its O(1) prepend; the recursion that walks a list; then the big-O cost model that shape gives you, and the operations to avoid in a hot path.

F4.01.1
Cons cells & the shape of a list →
A list is [head | tail]. Prepending is O(1); hd/1 and tl/1 read the cell; appending must copy.
F4.01.2
Recursion over lists →
Match [h | t], act on the head, recurse on the tail, stop at [] — with a tail-recursive accumulator.
F4.01.3
Complexity & big-O on the BEAM →
O(1) prepend versus O(n) length, ++, and last — reading cost from how many cells an operation must touch.
