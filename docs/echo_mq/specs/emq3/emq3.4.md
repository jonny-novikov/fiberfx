## 1 · The trim contract — `trim/4` over `XTRIM`, the two window forms, the blast radius bounded by the declared window

emq3.4 builds a dedicated public `EchoMQ.Stream.trim/4` verb. The contract, stated as precondition / postcondition / invariant:

| | The `MAXLEN` window (keep the N newest) | The `MINID` window (drop below a mint instant) |
|---|---|---|
| **The form** | `XTRIM <key> MAXLEN [~|=] <count>` — keep the `count` newest entries, remove the older. `~` approximate (whole macro-nodes, cheaper) is the DEFAULT; `=` exact is the OPT-IN. | `XTRIM <key> MINID [~|=] <id>` — remove every entry with id strictly below `<id>`. The floor `<id>` is DERIVED from a `DateTime` via `Snowflake.min_for/1` (§2). |
| **Precondition** | `count` a non-negative integer; the queue + stream names well-formed (the shipped `Keyspace.queue_key/2` gate). | a `DateTime` (the retention horizon — "keep entries minted at/after this instant"); the floor derived, never a raw `min_for/1` integer handed to the wire (§2). |
| **Postcondition** | post-trim, entries OLDER than the N newest are gone; the N newest (or, under `~`, at-least-N — approx keeps a few extra, never fewer) remain; a read over the retained span == the truth (INV1, INV2). | post-trim, every entry minted strictly BEFORE the instant is gone; every entry minted AT/AFTER the instant remains (INV1, INV6); a read inside the window never misses (INV2); a read of a trimmed range answers what survives, never a lie (INV3). |
| **The blast radius (INV4, load-bearing)** | bounded by `count`: a `MAXLEN ~ N` can NEVER remove one of the N newest. The approximate `~` may keep MORE than N (it never removes more), so it can never over-delete inside the window. `=` removes EXACTLY to N. | bounded by the floor: a `MINID <floor>` can NEVER remove an entry at/above the floor. The floor IS the window edge; over-deletion (removing at/above the floor) is structurally impossible under correct `XTRIM` and is the LOUD failure the mutation battery proves caught. |

**The approximate-vs-exact safety asymmetry (why `~` is the default).** `MAXLEN ~` and `MINID ~` are the SAFE-by-construction defaults: approximate trimming removes in whole macro-nodes, so it always keeps AT LEAST the requested window and may keep slightly more — it can **under-trim** (leave a few extra old entries) but can NEVER **over-trim** (remove inside the window). This is exactly the property INV4 needs: the destructive op's error direction is toward KEEPING data, never losing it. The exact form (`=`) is the explicit opt-in for the caller who needs the count/floor honored to the entry (a hard compliance cap) and accepts the higher cost; even `=` cannot remove inside the window (it removes exactly to the edge). **A trim can never delete inside the declared window (INV4) — the conformance scenario PROVES this positively (it appends entries inside the window and asserts they survive a trim), never a no-op.**

`trim/4` returns `{:ok, removed_count}` (the integer `XTRIM` answers — how many entries were removed) or `{:error, term}` (any connector/server fault, surfaced verbatim — the shipped `command/3` convention; a `WRONGTYPE` against a non-stream key is surfaced, not swallowed). The removed-count is the receipt the driver logs/meters.

## 2 · The `MINID` floor — derived from `Snowflake.min_for/1`, never the raw integer (INV6, the rung's one piece of real id-math)

The `MINID` window is "keep entries minted at/after a `DateTime` instant." The floor handed to `XTRIM MINID` must be a STREAM ID in the writer's A1 form (`"<ms>-<tail>"`), because that is the form `append/4` stores (`stream.ex:18-20`, `Stream.Id.xadd_id/1`). The derivation, stated exactly so Mars builds it right and Apollo verifies it positively:

1. `Snowflake.min_for(dt)` (`snowflake.ex:116`) returns the **smallest snowflake integer** mintable at-or-after `dt`: `(DateTime.to_unix(dt, :millisecond) - @epoch_ms) <<< 22` — the millisecond shifted into the timestamp field, the node|seq tail all-zero.
2. The Unix-ms of that floor snowflake is `Snowflake.unix_ms(min_for(dt))` == `DateTime.to_unix(dt, :millisecond)` (the inverse, `snowflake.ex:107`) — i.e. the instant's own Unix-ms.
3. The `MINID` floor id is `"<ms>-0"` where `ms = DateTime.to_unix(dt, :millisecond)` (equivalently `unix_ms(min_for(dt))`) — the SMALLEST entry id at-or-after the instant (tail `-0` is the lowest sequence at that ms). `XTRIM MINID "<ms>-0"` removes every entry whose `ms-seq` id is strictly below `<ms>-0`, i.e. every entry minted in an EARLIER millisecond — exactly the entries minted strictly before the instant.

**INV6 — the MINID floor == `Snowflake.min_for(dt)` exactly.** The floor is DERIVED FROM `min_for/1` (its ms component is `unix_ms(min_for(dt))`), never a hand-rolled epoch arithmetic and never `min_for/1`'s raw integer passed to the wire (the wire wants `ms-seq`, not a 63-bit snowflake int). *Check:* a unit assertion that the floor id's ms component equals `Snowflake.unix_ms(Snowflake.min_for(dt))`; a positive integration assertion that an entry minted at `dt - 1ms` is trimmed by `MINID(dt)` and an entry minted at `dt` survives (the floor is the exact mint-instant edge — the half-open `[dt, ∞)` retention, mirror of the emq3.6 half-open `[dt1, dt2)` read the synthesis fixes for F3.6-A). The floor derivation is the ONE place emq3.4 does real id-math; it reuses the SHIPPED `min_for/1` (the F3.6-A / F3.4-B convergence both cite it — [`../../kb/streams-tier/streams.synthesis.md`](../../kb/streams-tier/streams.synthesis.md) §2) rather than re-deriving the bit layout.

## 3 · The declared per-stream policy + the named/opt-in driver (D-2, D-3)

**The policy is BEAM-side, declared, re-applied at start (D-3).** A per-stream retention policy is a declared value (`{:maxlen, count, approx?}` or `{:minid, horizon_fun_or_duration, approx?}`) held BEAM-side — an ETS map or the driver's own config keyword list. It is NOT a per-call default (a caller may always call `trim/4` directly with explicit args) and NOT a keyspace subkey (D-3 — no `emq:{q}:stream:<name>:policy` key, no at-rest cleanup obligation; the gate needs no reader-visible policy because a polyglot reader reads ENTRIES, it does not enforce retention). The policy is the driver's instruction: "for this stream, keep this window." Keyspace visibility (a mirror a cross-runtime reader could introspect) is a later ADDITIVE upgrade reachable WITHOUT a wire break, surfaced not built (D-3).

**The driver is a named, opt-in, owner-started child — the `EchoMQ.Pump` shape (D-2).** emq3.4 builds a named trim driver (forward-tense — `EchoMQ.StreamRetention` or the equivalent the build settles, a `:transient` opt-in OTP child mirroring `EchoMQ.Pump`/`EchoMQ.Pump.Core`: a pure tick/decision core + a thin process router). On its tick it re-applies each declared stream's policy via the public `trim/4`. It is **opt-in** (owner-started, `no mod:` — never a default-on background sweep) and **decoupled from consumer liveness** (D-2 — it is NOT the `StreamConsumer` beat; a stream with no consumer still trims if its driver is configured; the `StreamConsumer` loop is byte-frozen this rung). A manual operator call to `trim/4` is the other supported cadence (the driver is sugar over the verb, never the only path). **The tick DECISION is a pure, doctested core** (the `Pump.Core` precedent + the architect-craft rule "a verdict/shaping surface is specced as a pure module, not a process `defp`"): given the declared policy + the clock, the core answers WHICH `trim/4` call to make (or `:noop`), exhaustive + disjoint over the policy forms — so the cadence is testable WITHOUT a live process and the destructive call is never buried in an IO `defp`.

**Why a driver at all, and why opt-in.** D-2's "retention is a property of the stream" needs SOMETHING to re-apply the window over time (a one-shot `trim/4` bounds memory once, not continuously). The driver is that something — but opt-in, because forcing a default-on destructive sweep on every stream is the coupling Lens B refused (a stream the operator wants UNBOUNDED, e.g. a short-lived run replayed in full, must not be silently trimmed). The opt-in driver + the always-available manual `trim/4` give both: continuous bounded memory where declared, no surprise deletion where not.

## Invariants (the runnable checks emq3.4 carries)

- **EMQ3.4-INV1 — trim honors the window (post-trim, entries below the window are gone, at/above remain).** A `trim/4` with a `MAXLEN N` keeps the N newest (under `~`, at-least-N) and removes the older; a `trim/4` with a `MINID <floor>` removes every entry minted strictly below the floor and keeps every entry at/above it. *Check:* the `stream_retention` conformance scenario appends K entries, calls `trim/4` with a window that should retain the newest M (< K), and asserts a subsequent `read/3..6` returns EXACTLY the retained span (the M newest under `=`, at-least-M under `~`) — the removed entries gone, the retained entries present. The `XTRIM` removed-count == K − (retained) under `=` (exact accounting).
- **EMQ3.4-INV2 — a read inside the window never misses (`XRANGE` over the retained span == the truth).** Every entry that the policy KEEPS is readable after the trim; the trim never removes an entry inside the declared window. *Check:* the scenario appends entries spanning the window edge, trims, and `read/3..6`s the IN-WINDOW span — every in-window entry's branded receipt is present (none missing); a property/integration assertion appends N entries inside the window, trims to a wider window, and asserts all N survive (the safe-direction proof).
- **EMQ3.4-INV3 — outside the window the read answers truthfully (a trimmed range returns what survives, never a lie).** A `read/3..6` of a range that the trim has emptied returns what genuinely survives (possibly nothing), never a phantom and never an error masking a deletion. *Check:* after a `MINID` trim, a `read/3..6` of the trimmed (below-floor) range returns ONLY the surviving (at/above-floor) entries that fall in the requested range — a read of a fully-trimmed below-floor range returns `[]` (honest empty), not an error and not stale entries.
