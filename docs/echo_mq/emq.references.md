# Echo — References

## The Branded Component System — Consolidated Bibliography

The complete, deduplicated list of every external source cited by a References block anywhere in the manuscript ([`bcs.toc.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs.toc.md) is the reading order). Each entry carries the full citation, an abstract of what the source says, and the list of chapters that cite it — with what each chapter takes from it. A reverse index (chapter → references) closes the file. The `*.llms.md` reading lists point only at internal files (the canon, the specs, the rung transcripts) and are catalogued in the "Internal canon" note, not here.

Sources are grouped in four families: the foundational papers and theses the historical case stands on, the industry essays and engineering posts that carry the practice, the runtime-platform documentation (Erlang/OTP, Go), and the Valkey engine documentation and releases that ground Parts I and III.

## I. Foundational papers and theses

### 1. Ross, D. T. — *A Generalized Technique for Symbol Manipulation and Numerical Calculation*

Communications of the ACM 4(3), March 1961, pp. 147–150.
[dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/366199.366256](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/366199.366256)

**Abstract.** Ross's note from MIT's Computer Applications Group describes the *plex* technique: modeling a problem as n-component elements — blocks of contiguous storage whose components hold properties and pointers to other elements — manipulated through index registers. It is one of the earliest published articulations of structured, pointer-linked records addressed by identity rather than by position, written before the vocabulary of records and structs existed. The series' historical case opens here: the idea that a *thing* in a program is a bundle of named properties reachable through one identifying handle.

**Cited by:** Preface ([`bcs.preface.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs.preface.md)) — the first link in the sixty-year chain from plexes to branded identities.

### 2. Sutherland, I. E. — *Sketchpad: A Man-Machine Graphical Communication System*

MIT PhD thesis, January 1963; University of Cambridge Computer Laboratory reissue UCAM-CL-TR-574 (2003), with preface by Blackwell and Rodden.
[cl.cam.ac.uk/techreports/UCAM-CL-TR-574.html](https://www.cl.cam.ac.uk/techreports/UCAM-CL-TR-574.html)

**Abstract.** Sutherland's thesis describes the first interactive graphical system: drawings built from ring-structured data, a master/instance mechanism where one definition stamps out many occurrences, and constraint satisfaction over the structure. The master/instance split — shared definition, per-instance state — is the direct ancestor of both object-oriented instantiation and the archetype/override pattern the manuscript builds in Part II. The Cambridge reissue is the canonical freely-available edition.

**Cited by:** Preface ([`bcs.preface.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs.preface.md)) — masters and instances as the second step of the historical arc.

### 3. Hoare, C. A. R. — *Record Handling*

ALGOL Bulletin 21 (AB21.3.6), 1965; archival copy in the Computer History Museum's collection.
[archive.computerhistory.org/resources/text/Knuth_Don_X4100/PDF_index/k-9-pdf/k-9-u2293-Record-Handling-Hoare.pdf](https://archive.computerhistory.org/resources/text/Knuth_Don_X4100/PDF_index/k-9-pdf/k-9-u2293-Record-Handling-Hoare.pdf)

**Abstract.** Hoare's proposal to extend ALGOL with *records* — heap-allocated bundles of named fields — and *references*: typed handles that may only point at records of a declared class, checked by the compiler. The proposal seeded Simula's class concept and, through it, object orientation; for this manuscript its load-bearing idea is the *typed reference* — a handle that carries, in its type, which kind of thing it may name. The branded id's namespace discriminant is this idea moved from compile-time into the value itself.

**Cited by:** Preface ([`bcs.preface.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs.preface.md)) — typed references as the third step of the historical arc.

### 4. Lamport, L. — *Time, Clocks, and the Ordering of Events in a Distributed System*

Communications of the ACM 21(7), July 1978, pp. 558–565.
[dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/359545.359563](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/359545.359563)

**Abstract.** The foundational distributed-systems paper: simultaneity is not observable across machines, so order must be *constructed*. Lamport defines the happened-before relation as a partial order generated by process-local sequence and message passing, gives the logical-clock condition that any clock must satisfy to respect it, and shows how to extend the partial order to an arbitrary total order. The manuscript's law of two clocks stands on this: identity time (the mint instant inside the id) is a constructed order, not a wall-clock reading, and event time is data.

**Cited by (continued):** 4.2 Coherence by Mint Time ([`bcs4.2.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs4.2.md)) — the total order from timestamps, carried in the name: newer-wins resolves without coordination.

### 44. Thompson, M., Farley, D., Barker, M., Gee, P., Stewart, A. — *Disruptor: High performance alternative to bounded queues for exchanging data between concurrent threads*

[lmax-exchange.github.io/disruptor/disruptor.html](https://lmax-exchange.github.io/disruptor/disruptor.html)

**Abstract.** The LMAX technical paper: a bounded ring of preallocated entries addressed by sequence counters, single-writer consumption eliminating contention, and the batching effect — a consumer that falls behind drains everything available in one pass, so throughput degrades gracefully under load instead of collapsing. The manuscript takes the sequences-slots-batching shape whole and swaps the wait strategy for the BEAM's mailbox.

**Cited by:** 4.3 The Single Writer and the Ring ([`bcs4.3.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs4.3.md)) — sequences over slots; batching as catch-up; the full-policy contrast.

**Cited by:** 1.5 The Time Inside the Name ([`bcs1.5.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs1.5.md)) — happened-before as the foundation of the order theorem's claim.

### 5. Codd, E. F. — *A Relational Model of Data for Large Shared Data Banks*

Communications of the ACM 13(6), June 1970, pp. 377–387.
[dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/362384.362685](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/362384.362685)

**Abstract.** Codd's paper introduces the relational model: data as relations over domains, independent of storage order and access paths, with a normalization procedure that removes nonsimple domains — in particular, lifting embedded repeating groups out into relations of their own keyed by the parent's identifier. The manuscript's edges store is this normalization performed deliberately: a relation between two identities is a first-class row in a relation-owning system, never a list embedded in either endpoint.

**Cited by:** 2.5 Relations Are Systems ([`bcs2.5.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs2.5.md)) — relations as first-class; the normalization that gate E3 performs.

### 6. Steindorfer, M. J. & Vinju, J. J. — *Optimizing Hash-Array Mapped Tries for Fast and Lean Immutable JVM Collections*

OOPSLA 2015, pp. 783–800.
[dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2814270.2814312](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2814270.2814312)

**Abstract.** The CHAMP paper. Steindorfer and Vinju redesign Bagwell's hash-array mapped trie for immutable collections: each node carries two bitmaps separating inline payload from sub-node pointers, popcount indexing keeps nodes densely packed, and a canonical (always-compressed) representation makes structural equality cheap and improves cache locality over classic HAMTs. The manuscript's persistent property database is a CHAMP, with the contract's hash32 as the trie's placement function and structural sharing as the snapshot mechanism.

**Cited by:** 2.3 The CHAMP Property Database ([`bcs2.3.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs2.3.md)) — the two-bitmap node, popcount indexing, cache locality, and the canonical representation the implementation carries.

### 7. Nagle, J. — *On Packet Switches With Infinite Storage*

RFC 970, IETF, December 1985.
[datatracker.ietf.org/doc/rfc970](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/rfc970/)

**Abstract.** Nagle's thought experiment: give a packet switch infinite buffering and congestion still collapses it, because FIFO service lets one misbehaving source consume the switch. The remedy is structural, not punitive — queue per source and serve the queues round-robin, so fairness is a property of the service discipline and a flooding source only delays itself. The manuscript takes this as the fairness construction for queue groups: ring rotation over per-group parked sets, so one hot tenant cannot starve the lane next door.

**Cited by:** 3.4 Fair Lanes ([`bcs3.4.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.4.md)) — source-sorted round-robin as the fairness construction behind group rotation.


### 31. Shreedhar, M. & Varghese, G. — *Efficient Fair Queuing Using Deficit Round-Robin*

IEEE/ACM Transactions on Networking 4(3), June 1996, pp. 375–385; SIGCOMM '95 open-access edition at Washington University Open Scholarship.
[openscholarship.wustl.edu](https://openscholarship.wustl.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1339&context=cse_research)

**Abstract.** The deficit round-robin paper: fair queuing approximated at O(1) per packet by giving each flow a quantum and carrying the unspent remainder as a deficit, achieving near-perfect throughput fairness without sorted scheduling — and stated by its authors as applicable to scheduling problems beyond packets, wherever service cannot be broken into smaller units. The manuscript cites it as the refinement of Nagle's per-source round-robin for variable service costs: the future knob Chapter 3.4's boundary names, since per-job rotation equalizes counts, not costs.

**Cited by:** 3.4 Fair Lanes ([`bcs3.4.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.4.md)) — the O(1) fair-share refinement for variable service sizes; the weighted and deficit future knob.

## II. Industry essays and engineering posts

### 8. Leonard, T. — *Postmortem: Thief: The Dark Project*

Game Developer Magazine, July 1999.
[gamedeveloper.com/design/postmortem-i-thief-the-dark-project-i-](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/postmortem-i-thief-the-dark-project-i-)

**Abstract.** Tom Leonard's postmortem of Looking Glass Studios' *Thief* describes the Dark Engine's object system: game objects as ids into a property database, archetypes as property bundles that concrete objects inherit and override per-instance, and behavior composed from data rather than class hierarchies. It is the earliest shipping articulation of the property-database-plus-archetypes design the manuscript calls the Looking Glass move — the proto-ECS that Part II's archetype chapter performs in Elixir.

**Cited by:** Preface ([`bcs.preface.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs.preface.md)) — the property database entering shipping practice, the fourth step of the historical arc.

### 9. West, M. — *Evolve Your Hierarchy: Refactoring Game Entities with Components*

Cowboy Programming / Game Developer Magazine, 2007.
[cowboyprogramming.com/2007/01/05/evolve-your-heirachy](https://cowboyprogramming.com/2007/01/05/evolve-your-heirachy/)

**Abstract.** The classic entity-component essay. West walks through refactoring a deep game-object inheritance tree — where every new entity kind forces a worse ancestor — into a flat composition: an entity is an id, and its capabilities are components attached to that id, owned and updated by their subsystems. The article fixed the ECS vocabulary for a generation of engines. The manuscript reads it twice: once for the entity id whose weakness Part I diagnoses, once for composition-as-data that Part II's archetypes generalize.

**Cited by:** 1.4 From ECS to BCS ([`bcs1.4.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs1.4.md)) — the game-engine entity id whose contract gaps the chapter traces. · 2.4 Archetypes and Composition ([`bcs2.4.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs2.4.md)) — composition over inheritance as the component pattern's core move.

### 10. Weissflog, A. — *Handles are the better pointers*

floooh.github.io, June 2018 (with the November 2018 per-slot generation-counter update).
[floooh.github.io/2018/06/17/handles-vs-pointers.html](https://floooh.github.io/2018/06/17/handles-vs-pointers.html)

**Abstract.** Weissflog's widely-cited systems-programming essay argues for index handles over raw pointers: central systems own their object arrays, hand out small integer handles, and tag each slot with a generation counter so a stale handle is *detected* rather than dereferenced into garbage. It is the strongest in-process form of the entity id — and still process-local: the generation trick protects a handle inside one run of one program. The manuscript cites it as the high-water mark the branded id must surpass the moment identity crosses a save file, a socket, or a foreign store.

**Cited by:** 1.4 From ECS to BCS ([`bcs1.4.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs1.4.md)) — the generation-counter handle as the best process-local identity, and where it stops.

### 11. King, R. — *Announcing Snowflake*

Twitter Engineering blog, June 2010.
[blog.twitter.com/engineering/en_us/a/2010/announcing-snowflake](https://blog.twitter.com/engineering/en_us/a/2010/announcing-snowflake)

**Abstract.** Twitter's announcement of Snowflake: the database could no longer mint ids fast enough, so ids became 64-bit values minted *without coordination* — a millisecond timestamp in the high bits, a worker id, and a per-worker sequence — yielding ids that are unique across a fleet and *roughly* time-sortable. The manuscript's identity contract adopts the layout and hardens the "roughly": with a monotonic mint floor per node, order within a node becomes exact, which is the order theorem.

**Cited by:** 1.2 The Identity Contract, Read as Architecture ([`bcs1.2.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs1.2.md)) — the layout the contract hardens into exact per-node order. · The identity canon ([`contract.md`](../../contract/contract.md)) — the time/worker/sequence layout and its uncoordinated, roughly-sortable design goals.

### 12. Appleby, A. — *SMHasher and MurmurHash3*

Public-domain repository: the hash function family and its test suite.
[github.com/aappleby/smhasher](https://github.com/aappleby/smhasher)

**Abstract.** Appleby's repository carries MurmurHash3 and the SMHasher quality suite that validated it. The piece the contract borrows is the `fmix64` finalizer — a short sequence of xor-shifts and multiplies whose stated purpose is to "force all bits of a hash block to avalanche": flip any input bit and every output bit flips with probability one half. Being public domain and exactly specified, it can be re-implemented identically in every runtime of the canon — which is why hash32, the contract's placement function, is the same number in Elixir, Go, Node, Rust, and C.

**Cited by:** 1.2 The Identity Contract, Read as Architecture ([`bcs1.2.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs1.2.md)) — the finalizer family the placement hash draws its round and constant from. · The identity canon ([`contract.md`](../../contract/contract.md)) — the public-domain `fmix64` reference.

### 13. Chassaing, J. — *Functional Event Sourcing Decider*

thinkbeforecoding.com, December 2021.
[thinkbeforecoding.com/post/2021/12/17/functional-event-sourcing-decider](https://thinkbeforecoding.com/post/2021/12/17/functional-event-sourcing-decider)

**Abstract.** Chassaing's essay defines the decider: a pure functional core for event sourcing made of `decide: Command -> State -> Event list`, `evolve: State -> Event -> State`, an initial state, and a terminal predicate — small enough to test exhaustively and composable into larger deciders. The manuscript's Part VIII rethinks the pattern on BCS ground: commands and events become messages about branded identities, the decider's state a system-owned table, and strategies compose as `STR`-keyed deciders supervised like any other system.

**Cited by:** 1.1 The System Substrate ([`bcs1.1.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs1.1.md)) — the decide/evolve pattern Part VIII rethinks on BCS.

### 14. Venners, B. — *Design Principles from Design Patterns: a conversation with Erich Gamma*

Artima Developer, 2005.
[artima.com/articles/design-principles-from-design-patterns](https://www.artima.com/articles/design-principles-from-design-patterns)

**Abstract.** Venners interviews Erich Gamma a decade after *Design Patterns*, and Gamma defends the book's two load-bearing principles — program to an interface, not an implementation; favor object composition over class inheritance — as the part that aged best, with inheritance demoted to an implementation detail rather than a modeling tool. The manuscript cites it where archetypes replace class hierarchies: property inheritance done as data composition, the diamond problem dissolved rather than solved.

**Cited by:** 2.4 Archetypes and Composition ([`bcs2.4.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs2.4.md)) — composition-over-inheritance from the Gang of Four, defended a decade on.

### 15. Kleppmann, M. — *How to do distributed locking*

martin.kleppmann.com, February 2016.
[martin.kleppmann.com/2016/02/08/how-to-do-distributed-locking.html](https://martin.kleppmann.com/2016/02/08/how-to-do-distributed-locking.html)

**Abstract.** Kleppmann's critique of the Redlock algorithm, whose constructive half matters here: a lock alone cannot protect a resource, because a paused or partitioned client can wake and write after its lease expired — correctness requires a *fencing token*, a monotonically increasing number checked by the resource itself, so a stale claimant's write is refused. The manuscript's job state machine satisfies the argument structurally: the `attempts` counter rises on every claim, and the single-script transition checks it, so a zombie worker's late completion cannot land.

**Cited by:** 3.3 The State Machine in Lua ([`bcs3.3.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.3.md)) — the fencing-token argument the `attempts` counter satisfies.


### 32. Helland, P. — *Life beyond Distributed Transactions: an Apostate's Opinion*

CIDR 2007, Third Biennial Conference on Innovative Data Systems Research, pp. 132–141.
[cidrdb.org/cidr2007/papers/cidr07p15.pdf](http://cidrdb.org/cidr2007/papers/cidr07p15.pdf)

**Abstract.** Helland's position paper on almost-infinite scale without distributed transactions: applications decompose into entities — keyed collections of data atomically updatable within one entity and never across — connected by at-least-once messaging, with idempotence achieved by the recipient remembering which messages it has processed. The manuscript makes the remembering concrete with the identity contract: the job's branded name is the memory, the row itself is the table, and Chapter 3.5's per-row guard turns at-least-once delivery into exactly-once effect.

**Cited by:** 3.5 The Bus Meets the Stores ([`bcs3.5.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.5.md)) — entities atomic within, never across; at-least-once messaging; the recipient-remembers requirement the provenance guard satisfies.


### 43. Fowler, M. — *The LMAX Architecture*

[martinfowler.com/articles/lmax.html](https://martinfowler.com/articles/lmax.html)

**Abstract.** The expositional account of LMAX's retail trading platform: a business logic processor running single-threaded and in-memory — six million orders per second on one thread — fed and drained by Disruptors, with event sourcing carrying recovery and the team's conclusion that queue-based concurrency fights modern CPUs. The manuscript reads it as the prior art for one-owner-applies-the-stream and translates its shape onto the BEAM.

**Cited by:** 4.3 The Single Writer and the Ring ([`bcs4.3.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs4.3.md)) — the single-threaded processor and the ring that feeds it.

### 46. Richardson, C. — *Pattern: Transactional outbox*

[microservices.io/patterns/data/transactional-outbox.html](https://microservices.io/patterns/data/transactional-outbox.html)

**Abstract.** The pattern-catalog entry for reliable messaging beside a database: record the outgoing message in an outbox table within the service's own storage, relay it to the broker afterward, and accept that the relay may deliver more than once — so consumers must be idempotent, typically by tracking processed identities. The manuscript implements the shape per group on SQLite and satisfies the idempotence demand with machinery already shipped: job-id dedup at bus admission and newer-wins at application.

**Cited by:** 4.4 The Lane That Remembers ([`bcs4.4.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs4.4.md)) — the outbox's contract and its crash seams.

## III. Runtime-platform documentation

### 16. Erlang/OTP — `ets` module, stdlib

[erlang.org/doc/apps/stdlib/ets.html](https://www.erlang.org/doc/apps/stdlib/ets.html)

**Abstract.** The reference for Erlang Term Storage: in-memory tables owned by a process, with table types (`set`, `ordered_set` in Erlang term order), protection levels (`public`, `protected`, `private` — a `private` table readable and writable only by its owner), table lifetime bound to the owning process, ordered traversal (`first`/`next`, `prev`/`last`), and `select/2,3` with match specifications. The manuscript leans on three facts: `private` makes table ownership enforceable, not aspirational; `ordered_set` over branded keys makes chronology a property of the keyspace; the table dies with its owner, which is what makes restart semantics an architectural statement.

**Cited by:** 1.1 The System Substrate ([`bcs1.1.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs1.1.md)) — protection levels; `ordered_set` term order; `prev`/`last` traversal. · 2.1 A System Is an OTP Application ([`bcs2.1.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs2.1.md)) — `private`; table lifetime bound to the owner. · 2.2 Property Stores on ETS ([`bcs2.2.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs2.2.md)) — ordered traversal; `select/2` and match specifications; protection levels. · 2.5 Relations Are Systems ([`bcs2.5.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs2.5.md)) — `ordered_set` traversal over tuple keys; `select/2,3`. · 2.6 The Boundary Audited and Priced ([`bcs2.6.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs2.6.md)) — protection levels and the `read_concurrency` tuning the chapter prices. · 4.1 Cache-Aside at ETS Speed ([`bcs4.1.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs4.1.md)) — public `read_concurrency` tables for caller-side hits; `select_delete` as the sweeper's one-pass reclaim.

### 17. Erlang/OTP — `supervisor` behaviour, stdlib

[erlang.org/doc/apps/stdlib/supervisor.html](https://www.erlang.org/doc/apps/stdlib/supervisor.html)

**Abstract.** The reference for OTP supervisors: restart strategies (`one_for_one` as the default, restarting only the failed child — the single-child blast radius), children started in list order and shut down in reverse, and restart intensity (max restarts in a sliding window, beyond which the supervisor itself fails upward). The manuscript reads supervision as architecture: which children share a fate, in what order stores come up, and what a restart erases are design decisions the supervision tree states explicitly.

**Cited by:** 2.1 A System Is an OTP Application ([`bcs2.1.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs2.1.md)) — strategies, blast radius, start/shutdown order, restart intensity. · 3.5 The Bus Meets the Stores ([`bcs3.5.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.5.md)) — one_for_one restart isolation and tree-order start and shutdown, the owner semantics the consumer joins.

### 18. The Go Project — *Codewalk: Share Memory By Communicating*

[go.dev/doc/codewalk/sharemem](https://go.dev/doc/codewalk/sharemem/)

**Abstract.** The official Go codewalk illustrating the proverb "don't communicate by sharing memory; share memory by communicating": a URL poller where each piece of state is owned by exactly one goroutine at a time, handed between stages over channels, so sequential access is guaranteed *by construction* and no mutex is needed. This is the owner-goroutine pattern the manuscript designates as the Go counterpart of the BEAM's process-owned ETS table: the same ownership law, enforced by a different runtime.

**Cited by:** 1.1 The System Substrate ([`bcs1.1.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs1.1.md)) — the owner-goroutine pattern; sequential access by construction.


### 33. Erlang/OTP — `erl_nif`, erts

[erlang.org/doc/apps/erts/erl_nif.html](https://www.erlang.org/doc/apps/erts/erl_nif.html)

**Abstract.** The reference for native implemented functions: NIFs execute on the scheduler thread that calls them, with the documented expectation that a call returns within a millisecond and the dirty-scheduler classification for work that cannot — the discipline that keeps native code from stalling the BEAM's preemptive scheduling. The manuscript leans on it where the boundary audit prices native crossings and classifies which contract work may ride a NIF.

**Cited by:** 2.6 The Boundary Audited and Priced ([`bcs2.6.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs2.6.md)) — the millisecond discipline and the dirty-scheduler classification for native work.


### 35. Oban documentation — `Oban` module

[hexdocs.pm/oban/Oban.html](https://hexdocs.pm/oban/Oban.html)

**Abstract.** The API reference for Oban, the Elixir job processor on relational storage: insert and insert_all for enqueueing, their `Ecto.Multi` forms that compose a job insert into the caller's own transaction — the documented enqueue-atomic-with-data pattern — and the queue pause and resume controls. The manuscript stages the Multi pattern as the rival's advantage row: one transaction carries the business write and its job, rollback erases both, commit lands both — the sentence the bus cannot say.

**Cited by:** 3.6 Conformance and the Rival's Numbers ([`bcs3.6.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.6.md)) — the transactional enqueue-with-data pattern; pause and resume around the drain.

### 36. PostgreSQL documentation — Asynchronous Commit

[postgresql.org/docs/current/wal-async-commit.html](https://www.postgresql.org/docs/current/wal-async-commit.html)

**Abstract.** The reliability chapter on commit modes: transaction commit is normally synchronous — the server waits for the transaction's WAL records to reach permanent storage before reporting success — and for short transactions that wait is the major component of total time; asynchronous commit trades a bounded data-loss window for throughput. The manuscript derives the rival's sequential and drain bands from the synchronous flush, and names the asynchronous knob as the tuning that would close the gap by trading away the guarantee being measured.

**Cited by:** 3.6 Conformance and the Rival's Numbers ([`bcs3.6.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.6.md)) — the WAL-flush wait behind the sequential and drain derivations.

### 37. PostgreSQL documentation — NOTIFY

[postgresql.org/docs/current/sql-notify.html](https://www.postgresql.org/docs/current/sql-notify.html)

**Abstract.** The command reference for NOTIFY: events raised inside a transaction are delivered only if and when the transaction commits, and notifications from different transactions arrive in commit order — the wake itself is transactional. The manuscript reads this as the structural floor under the rival's end-to-end latency: a job's wake cannot outrun its commit, where the bus's wake is a list push answered by a parked blocking pop.

**Cited by:** 3.6 Conformance and the Rival's Numbers ([`bcs3.6.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.6.md)) — delivery at commit, in commit order: the floor under the rival's wake.


### 40. The Go Project — `x/sync/singleflight`

[pkg.go.dev/golang.org/x/sync/singleflight](https://pkg.go.dev/golang.org/x/sync/singleflight)

**Abstract.** The Go module providing "a duplicate function call suppression mechanism": concurrent calls keyed alike coalesce onto one execution, duplicates wait and receive the shared result. The manuscript cites it as the named prior art for one-fill-per-herd and as the Go port's idiom for the single-flight fill.

**Cited by:** 4.1 Cache-Aside at ETS Speed ([`bcs4.1.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs4.1.md)) — fill coalescing's prior art and the port's library.

### 45. Erlang/OTP — `atomics` module, erts

[erlang.org/doc/apps/erts/atomics.html](https://www.erlang.org/doc/apps/erts/atomics.html)

**Abstract.** Mutable 64-bit atomic variables in arrays, implemented on hardware atomic instructions with no software locking: get, put, add, exchange, compare-exchange, and the ordering guarantee the manuscript leans on — all atomic operations are mutually ordered, so a reader that observes a later update observes every earlier one. The ring's publish-then-advance correctness is this sentence.

**Cited by:** 4.3 The Single Writer and the Ring ([`bcs4.3.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs4.3.md)) — the sequences' visibility guarantee.

### 47. SQLite documentation — Write-Ahead Logging

[sqlite.org/wal.html](https://sqlite.org/wal.html)

**Abstract.** The WAL journal mode: commits append to a log file and checkpoints fold pages back into the database; at `synchronous=NORMAL` the checkpoint is the only operation issuing a sync barrier, so commit latency is an append and durability against power loss rides the checkpoint cadence. The manuscript runs the per-group journal in this mode and states the trade in its boundaries.

**Cited by:** 4.4 The Lane That Remembers ([`bcs4.4.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs4.4.md)) — the journal's commit path and the NORMAL trade.

### 48. Litestream documentation — How it works

[litestream.io/how-it-works](https://litestream.io/how-it-works/)

**Abstract.** Streaming SQLite replication from a separate background process: Litestream holds a long-running read transaction to take over checkpointing, copies WAL pages into a shadow sequence, ships them to replicas, and restores by snapshot plus WAL replay. The manuscript names it as the off-box layer above the journal — deliberately a separate process, deliberately not implemented in the codebase.

## IV. Valkey engine documentation and releases

### 19. Söderqvist, V. — *A new hash table*

Valkey project, technical deep dive, 2025-03-28.
[valkey.io/blog/new-hash-table](https://valkey.io/blog/new-hash-table/)

**Abstract.** Söderqvist's deep dive into the hash table that replaced the chained dict in Valkey 8.1: open addressing over cache-line-sized buckets (one bucket per 64 bytes, metadata and hash bits packed so most lookups touch one cache line), incremental rehash, and — decisive for the manuscript — a single allocation per entry whose size walks the allocator's size classes with the key's length. That size-class step is the primary source behind Part I's storage cost model: why a 14-byte branded key and a 16-byte binary UUID land in the same class, and where each longer rendering tips into the next one.

**Cited by:** 1.3 Choosing the ID System ([`bcs1.3.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs1.3.md)) — the primary source the cost model is derived from. · Appendix 1.1 Branding Beats Its Own Integer ([`bcs1.a1.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs1.a1.md)) — the single-allocation entry layout behind the size-class step. · The ID system record (`bcs.id-system.md`, outside this archive) — same derivation, decision-record form.

### 21. Valkey documentation — Streams introduction

[valkey.io/topics/streams-intro](https://valkey.io/topics/streams-intro/)

**Abstract.** The streams data-type guide: entries keyed by ids of the form `ms-seq` (millisecond timestamp dash sequence number), ids strictly increasing within a stream, and range queries (`XRANGE`) that address *time* directly through the id because the timestamp is embedded in it. The manuscript reads this as the engine independently arriving at the contract's own theorem — time inside the name — and Chapter 1.3's streams experiment extends the branded id into entry ids at zero cost per entry.

**Cited by:** 1.3 Choosing the ID System ([`bcs1.3.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs1.3.md)) — entry-id structure; time ranges from the id. · 1.5 The Time Inside the Name ([`bcs1.5.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs1.5.md)) — ids carry the millisecond; ranges address time through the id. · The ID system record (`bcs.id-system.md`, outside this archive).

### 22. Valkey command reference — XADD

[valkey.io/commands/xadd](https://valkey.io/commands/xadd/)

**Abstract.** The command reference for appending a stream entry: auto-generated ids from the server clock, or *explicit* ids supplied by the caller — accepted only if strictly greater than the stream's current top id, which makes the stream a totally ordered log with a monotonic floor enforced at the engine. The explicit-id form is what lets the manuscript's branded snowflakes serve as stream entry ids directly; the monotonic top-id rule is the engine-side mirror of the contract's mint floor.

**Cited by:** 1.3 Choosing the ID System ([`bcs1.3.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs1.3.md)) — explicit ids, total order, the monotonic top-id rule. · The ID system record (`bcs.id-system.md`, outside this archive).

### 23. Valkey documentation — Protocol specification (RESP)

[valkey.io/topics/protocol](https://valkey.io/topics/protocol/)

**Abstract.** The serialization protocol specification: clients send commands as arrays of bulk strings; every frame is CRLF-terminated and the first byte identifies the type; bulk strings are binary-safe because their length is prefixed, never delimiter-scanned. This is the wire law beneath Part III and both connector appendices — the one-pass RESP codec, the pipelining-as-primitive design, and gate F4's binary-safety exercise all derive from the prefixed-length rule.

**Cited by:** 3.1 The Fence and the Keyspace ([`bcs3.1.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.1.md)) — length-prefixed bulk strings; the binary safety F4 exercises. · Appendix A. The Connector ([`bcsA.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcsA.md)) — the command/reply framing the codec implements. · Appendix B. The Production Connector ([`bcsB.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcsB.md)) — the RESP2 framing under the rewritten codec.

### 24. Valkey documentation — Cluster specification

[valkey.io/topics/cluster-spec](https://valkey.io/topics/cluster-spec/)

**Abstract.** The cluster specification: the keyspace divided into 16384 hash slots, a key's slot computed as CRC16 of the key modulo 16384, and *hash tags* — when a key contains `{...}`, only the braced substring is hashed — as the sanctioned mechanism for forcing related keys into the same slot. The manuscript's `emq:{q}:*` keyspace is designed against this: every key of a queue shares the queue's hash tag, so every multi-key Lua script stays single-slot by construction, and the slot function sits in the connector in reserve.

**Cited by:** 3.1 The Fence and the Keyspace ([`bcs3.1.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.1.md)) — slots, CRC16 mod 16384, hash tags as the same-slot mechanism behind the co-location law.

### 25. Valkey documentation — Sorted sets

[valkey.io/topics/sorted-sets](https://valkey.io/topics/sorted-sets/)

**Abstract.** The sorted-set data-type guide: members ordered by score, with ties broken lexicographically by member — and the documented pattern of giving *every* member the same score so the set becomes a purely lexicographic index, valid only while scores stay equal (the different-scores caveat). The manuscript's job indexes ride this: branded ids at score zero sort lexicographically, and because the branded encoding is order-preserving, lexicographic order *is* mint order.

**Cited by:** 3.2 Jobs Are Entities ([`bcs3.2.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.2.md)) — the same-score lexicographic family as a generic index; the caveat behind the score-zero decision.

### 26. Valkey documentation — ZRANGE

[valkey.io/commands/zrange](https://valkey.io/commands/zrange/)

**Abstract.** The command reference for range queries over sorted sets: `BYSCORE`, `BYLEX`, and the `REV` modifier, with equal-score elements ordered lexicographically by member. The `REV` + `BYLEX` combination is the operation behind the manuscript's newest-first browsing — walk the score-zero index backwards and, by the order theorem, the newest mint comes out first without any timestamp column existing.

**Cited by:** 3.2 Jobs Are Entities ([`bcs3.2.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.2.md)) — the REV and BYLEX forms behind newest-first browse.

### 27. Valkey documentation — Programmability

[valkey.io/topics/programmability](https://valkey.io/topics/programmability/)

**Abstract.** The scripting guide: Lua scripts execute atomically — "all of the script's effects either have yet to happen or had already happened" — no other command interleaves, and scripts must declare their keys. This atomicity is the load-bearing fact of Part III's state machine: each lifecycle transition (claim, complete, retry, dead-letter) is one script, so a job is never observable mid-transition, and the declared-keys rule is what the fork's every-key-declared law makes total.

**Cited by:** 3.3 The State Machine in Lua ([`bcs3.3.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.3.md)) — atomic script execution as the single-script-transition guarantee. · Appendix B. The Production Connector ([`bcsB.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcsB.md)) — atomic scripts blocking all clients, the fact that sent blocking verbs to their own lane.

### 28. Valkey documentation — Replication

[valkey.io/topics/replication](https://valkey.io/topics/replication/)

**Abstract.** The replication guide, cited for its script semantics: scripts replicate by *effects* (the writes they performed), not by re-execution, and time is frozen during script execution — so a script that reads `TIME` and writes a value derived from it replicates deterministically. This is what makes the manuscript's use of `TIME` inside write scripts (TTL stamps, checkpoint timestamps) sound rather than a nondeterminism bug.

**Cited by:** 3.3 The State Machine in Lua ([`bcs3.3.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.3.md)) — the frozen-time rule that makes `TIME` inside a write script sound.

### 29. Valkey command reference — BLPOP

[valkey.io/commands/blpop](https://valkey.io/commands/blpop/)

**Abstract.** The blocking list pop: removes and returns the head of the first non-empty list among the given keys, or *blocks* the calling client until an element arrives or the timeout expires (returning nil). This is the park primitive of park-don't-poll: a worker with no eligible work parks on a blocking pop instead of spinning a poll loop, and is woken by the arrival itself.

**Cited by:** 3.4 Fair Lanes ([`bcs3.4.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.4.md)) — blocking head pop; nil on timeout; the park primitive.

### 30. Valkey documentation — Modules and blocking commands

[valkey.io/topics/modules-blocking-ops](https://valkey.io/topics/modules-blocking-ops/)

**Abstract.** The modules guide to blocking operations, cited for the engine-side law it states: a blocking command blocks the *calling client*, never the server — the event loop continues serving every other connection while the blocked client waits. This is the engine's half of Appendix B's lane law: blocking verbs get their own connection lane, because the block ties up a connection, not the engine.

**Cited by:** 3.4 Fair Lanes ([`bcs3.4.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.4.md)) — blocking applies to the calling client, not the server.


### 34. Valkey command reference — LMOVE

[valkey.io/commands/lmove](https://valkey.io/commands/lmove/)

**Abstract.** The command reference for atomically moving an element between lists — and, with the same key as source and destination, the documented list-rotation primitive: one step of a circular walk, safe under multiple rotating clients and under concurrent pushes. The manuscript's ring of serviceable lanes turns on it: every claim is one LMOVE rotation before service, which is what makes round-robin fairness a single O(1) engine command.

**Cited by:** 3.4 Fair Lanes ([`bcs3.4.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs3.4.md)) — same-key rotation as the ring's engine primitive.


### 38. Valkey command reference — SET

[valkey.io/commands/set](https://valkey.io/commands/set/)

**Abstract.** The string-set command with its expiry options — `EX`, `PX`, `EXAT`, `PXAT`, `KEEPTTL` — and conditional flags. The manuscript uses `SET ... PX` to land the L2 cache row and its TTL in one command, the second layer expiring itself on the server's own clock.

### 39. Valkey command reference — EXPIRE

[valkey.io/commands/expire](https://valkey.io/commands/expire/)

**Abstract.** Expiration semantics: timeouts stored in milliseconds against the server's clock, and the two reclamation paths — on-access expiry and the active expire cycles that reap volatile keys in the background. **Cited by:** 4.1 Cache-Aside at ETS Speed ([`bcs4.1.md`](../echo/bcs/content/bcs4.1.md)) — server-side reclamation beside the sweeper.

### 41. Valkey documentation — Pub/Sub

[valkey.io/topics/pubsub](https://valkey.io/topics/pubsub/)

**Abstract.** The publish/subscribe contract: at-most-once delivery — a message is delivered once if at all, lost if the subscriber is absent or disconnected — channels unpersisted, messages ordered per publisher, subscription replies delivered as messages, and under RESP3 any command permitted while subscribed. The manuscript takes the contract whole as the broadcast coherence lane's semantics and prices it beside the job lane.

[valkey.io/topics/client-side-caching](https://valkey.io/topics/client-side-caching/)

The invalidation is deletion-shaped — no version, no order, no replay. The manuscript names this as the comparison set's nearest prior art and the gap the versioned message closes.
