## The arc and the thesis

The course argues one thing, restated on every page: a Claude agent is a fast, tireless implementer of *well-specified*
thin slices, and the human is the source of decomposition, judgement, and acceptance. Neither no-plan (vibe coding) nor
all-plan (the big-bang spec) ships reliable software; the unit that does is a thin slice of value, specified only
enough, proven before the next begins. A3 lands that thesis on the *delivery* layer.

The chapter sits at a known place in the course. A0 framed the method in three questions (why, what, who). A1 made the
case for thin, provable slices and named the Author/Operator loop and "correct by definition". A2 turned a vision into a
dependency-ordered value ladder of INVEST stories with Given/When/Then — a backlog. **All of A0–A2 is built and
accepted.** A backlog, though, is not a plan: left alone it ships in the wrong order, or all at once. A3 is the
**roadmap layer** — where *how we deliver* the A2 backlog is decided, separately from *what we build and prove*, which
is the **spec layer** (A4, next). The roadmap orders work and points each rung at a spec; it never restates behaviour.
A4 then defines and proves each rung; A5 briefs the agent and builds it; A6 makes the increment production-grade; A7
runs the whole loop on the Portal end to end. A3 depends only downward on A0–A2 and feeds forward into A4.

The two-layer separation A3 teaches is the same one A1.04 introduced: the **roadmap** is the coarse, re-orderable
delivery plan that points at the spec and defines no behaviour; the **spec** is the single source of truth, edited only
by feedback. Re-ordering rungs edits the roadmap, never a spec — that invariant is the spine of the whole chapter, and
several A3 interactives prove it by holding a "spec edits" counter at zero while the rungs move.

## The locked F6 grounding facts — reuse these verbatim, invent nothing

The worked exemplar for the whole chapter is the Portal's real web chapter, **F6 (Phoenix)**, and its real
`phoenix.roadmap.md` (`docs/elixir/specs/phoenix/phoenix.roadmap.md`). The program-level facts come from
`portal.roadmap.md` (`docs/elixir/specs/portal.roadmap.md`). Quote these exactly; do not paraphrase a fact into a new
one, and do not invent a rung, a milestone, a property, or a Portal surface.

**The master invariant (quote it verbatim):**

> The web layer calls only the `Portal` facade and renders only the closed `%Portal.Error{}` set. No controller,
> LiveView, plug, or template names `Portal.Engine`, a repo, or `GenServer.call`.

In the file's own words, "This single rule is what makes the ladder cheap." This is the one place A3 may name
`Portal.Engine` and `%Portal.Error{}` — only as they appear in this quote.

**The three milestones (the delivery arc):**

1. **Ship the catalog** — rungs **F6.1–F6.5.5** — browse a persistent catalog and add courses, server-rendered in the
   jonnify design system, with inline errors.
2. **Make it live** — rungs **F6.6–F6.7** — search and create without reloads; every client updates live with a viewer
   count.
3. **Ship to users** — rungs **F6.8–F6.9** — sign in, run behind auth on a deployed clustered release, watch an
   operations dashboard.

**The nine-rung ladder** `f6.1 … f6.9`, each a vertical slice that ships a capability a real role can use and leaves the
platform live, all over the unchanged facade. **The per-iteration table columns are: Rung | Ships (the slice) | Demo |
Harness | Feedback asked** — each row a PR-sized increment (a spec triad, the slice, a green harness, a demo, a feedback
note). The per-rung loop is `sharpen → build → ship → demo → review → feedback → adapt`, and feedback edits the spec,
the single source of truth, never the shipped code.

**F6.1 is the tracer bullet:** "the engine served as a web app (endpoint, request → facade → render)" — the first thread
through every layer, shipped *before* persistence (F6.3) or the rendered catalog (F6.5). Its demo is "hit the root, see
a page"; its harness is a `ConnTest` GET smoke. It is a real thin feature, kept and grown — not a prototype to discard.

**The seven "thin but robust for the web" properties** (from the F6 roadmap's section of that name) — each names a
concrete move a rung makes:

- **Over the facade** — every controller, LiveView, and template calls only `Portal` and renders only the closed
  `%Portal.Error{}` set; the web invents no domain logic and no new error vocabulary.
- **Harnessed** — controllers via `ConnTest`, LiveViews via `LiveViewTest`, enrollment against the in-memory adapter,
  so the suite is fast and needs no live browser.
- **Verified and safe** — `~p` verified routes (a path typo fails to compile), HEEx-escaped interpolation, declared
  component `attr`s.
- **Rendered in the system** — dynamic pages emitted through a shared root layout + a single token stylesheet, the
  tokens declared once.
- **Honest real-time** — broadcasts fire only after a successful write, so clients only ever learn of facts.
- **Supervised** — new runtime pieces (endpoint, PubSub, Presence) are supervised children; the engine's crash
  isolation is untouched.
- **Always live** — every rung leaves the dev node booting clean and serving (`GET /health` answers `200` and the
  rung's route renders) — the liveness criterion.

**The program view** (from `portal.roadmap.md`): the Portal is one domain core behind one facade, surfaced through
several adapters. **The web (F6) and the bot (F10) are parallel surfaces over the same `Portal` facade** — neither
depends on the other for its core loop, so they are sequenced by product priority, not a hard dependency, and they ship
on **independent cadences**. The master invariant is the seam that decouples them: because every surface calls only the
facade and renders only the closed error set, adding a surface adds a roadmap of its own and never changes the core.
F7–F9 (multi-runtime) are reserved, not designed.

## The plan for A3.4–A3.8 — per module

Each module is a hub + three deep-dive subpages, two interactives per lesson, grounded on the facts above. The
acceptance for each is its Given/When/Then in [`a3.stories.md`](a3.stories.md) — read it as the definition of done.

- **A3.4 · Thin but robust** — `/roadmap/thin-but-robust`. Dives: `thin-not-shallow`, `what-robust-adds`, `always-live`.
  The line between a thin-but-robust slice and a flimsy prototype: a slice is thin-but-robust only when it is a vertical
  thread built over the facade — contract-guarded, harnessed, gated, and leaving the system live; a shallow or untested
  one is flimsy. Ground "what robust adds" on the seven F6 production properties above. **Bridge:** robustness → the
  Portal **master invariant** (the web calls only the facade and renders only the closed error set).

- **A3.5 · Milestones and iterations** — `/roadmap/milestones`. Dives: `shippable-milestones`, `the-iteration-loop`,
  `sequencing-the-ladder`. Group the nine rungs into the three F6 milestones (ship the catalog · make it live · ship to
  users), each a usable capability boundary; run each rung through one iteration over the columns **Ships | Demo |
  Harness | Feedback**; sequence by dependency *and* product priority. **Bridge:** the iteration → a PR-sized rung with
  a demo and a harness, accepted before the next begins. Re-grouping rungs edits the roadmap, never a spec.

- **A3.6 · The program roadmap** — `/roadmap/program-roadmap`. Dives: `roadmap-of-roadmaps`, `one-core-many-surfaces`,
  `independent-cadence`. Sequencing whole chapters into a program; the web (F6) and bot (F10) as parallel surfaces over
  the one `Portal` facade, each a roadmap of its own, shipping on independent cadences. **Bridge:** "call only the
  facade" → the rule that keeps each surface's roadmap independent of the others — the master invariant as the
  decoupling seam.

- **A3.7 · Tracer bullets and walking skeletons** — `/roadmap/tracer-bullets`. Dives: `end-to-end-first`,
  `tracer-vs-prototype`, `defer-breadth`. Ship a thin end-to-end thread (F6.1: request → facade → render) before depth,
  to de-risk integration early; distinguish a **tracer** (a real thin feature, kept and grown) from a **walking
  skeleton** (end-to-end structure, no feature yet) from a **prototype** (built to learn, then discarded); defer breadth
  deliberately. **Bridge:** the tracer contrasted with the prototype — F6.1 is the tracer, kept and grown into the live
  platform.

- **A3.8 · Workshop — roadmapping Portal** — `/roadmap/workshop`. Dives: `ladder-to-roadmap`, `choose-the-tracer`,
  `the-program-view`. Run the whole A3 sequence on the Portal: turn A2's value ladder into Portal's chapter roadmap
  (milestones, the iteration table, the tracer, the deferred seams) and its program view (web + bot over one facade).
  The worked output **reproduces the real F6 `phoenix.roadmap.md`** — invented nowhere; re-ordering rungs edits the
  roadmap, never a spec. This is the delivery plan Part VII (A7) executes.

`%Portal.Error{}` appear only inside the master-invariant quote. The audience is all levels: a learner who clicks, a
developer who roadmaps their own chapter, and the Claude Author who builds the pages.
