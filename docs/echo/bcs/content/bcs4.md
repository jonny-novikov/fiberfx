# BCS · Part IV — EchoCache: the near-cache the comparison set does not ship

<show-structure depth="2"/>

Part II gave the names a home and Part III put them in motion; Part IV makes reading them cheap without making them wrong. EchoCache is a near-cache — an L1 of declared ETS tables in front of the L2 Valkey the systems already share — and its one-line case is the part's title: branded keys, local speed, bus-driven coherence. The comparison set is real and named. Valkey itself ships server-assisted client-side caching, where the server tracks what a connection has read and sends an invalidation when a key changes. Nebulex documents a near-cache topology — a multilevel cache with a local L1 over a partitioned or Redis-backed L2 — with Cachex as the in-memory engine beneath it. Every member of that set, measured where it runs in the part's closing chapter, shares one shape: coherence is deletion. The message says *forget this key*; it carries no version, promises no order, leaves no record, and offers no path with a delivery guarantee. EchoCache's coherence message says something stronger — *a newer row exists for this name, minted at this time* — and because the version is a branded identity, the receiver decides newer-wins by comparing two names. Coherence without coordination is the identity theorem cashed a third time: the same bytes that gave Part II ordered browsing and Part III queue order now give caches a total order on writes.

This part inherits a measured floor, not a hope. The bus that will carry coherence has a committed end-to-end profile — `end-to-end median 0.3 ms bus vs 8.8 ms rival` — and a parked consumer costs `0 commands` while it waits, so an invalidation lane is cheap to keep open and fast when it fires. The contract under those messages is itself a committed artifact: `fourteen of fourteen contracts hold` in the conformance record, so a coherence message enqueued is a message whose delivery semantics are gated, not assumed. And the stores being cached already guard themselves — Part III's torn-write drill landed `qty 12 once, never 17 -- and the order completes` — which means a cache layered above them can be wrong only in the benign direction: stale for a bounded moment, never a fabricated row. The part's work is to make that bounded moment small, declared, and survivable.

## The laws of the part

**The cache is declared, not discovered.** Every L1 table is declared per kind with its TTL, size bound, and coherence mode; the operator can enumerate every cache in the node the way they enumerate stores. A cache that appears wherever a developer was impatient is a leak with a hit rate.

**One fill per herd.** A miss does not stampede the L2 or the system of record: concurrent misses on a key coalesce onto a single in-flight fill, and the herd reads the one answer. The thundering-herd drill is a gate, not an anecdote.

**Coherence is a message about a name.** Invalidation carries the branded key and the writer's mint-time version, nothing else. Newer wins by comparing identities; there is no coordinator, no lock, and no clock but the one already inside every id.

**Two coherence lanes, named per surface.** The broadcast lane rides the bus's wake fabric for latency and accepts fire-and-forget delivery; the job-backed lane pays queue overhead to buy at-least-once when a stale read costs money. Each cached surface names which lane it rides, under the same delivery-semantics discipline Part III demanded of the queue.

**The single writer applies the stream.** Per cache, coherence application is one owner draining an ordered ring — batched, allocation-light, and race-free by construction, because a stream applied by one writer needs no locks to stay ordered.

**Recovery is replay beside the bus, never WAL inside it.** Decision D-2 stands: the bus remains volatile. The memory that must survive a node — provenance, checkpoints, last-applied versions — lives in per-group journals beside the consumers, and recovery replays them. The rescue sits next to the bus, never inside it.

## The essentials, examined

**The ring and the single writer.** The fastest published trading systems put their business logic on one thread and feed it an ordered stream — the LMAX architecture's Business Logic Processor, fed by Disruptor ring buffers, is the canonical report, and the research appendix ([`bcs.research.3.md`](bcs.research.3.md)) traces that genealogy with its reported figures and their hardware caveats intact. EchoCache borrows the shape, not the scale: each cache's coherence applier is a single owner draining a ring of invalidation events. The ring gives three things at once. Order — events apply in arrival order, so newer-wins degenerates to a comparison, never a merge. Batching — a draining owner takes everything present in one pass, amortizing wakeups exactly the way Part III's lanes amortize claims. Backpressure as a number — ring occupancy is the health gauge, and a full ring is a typed, visible condition rather than an unbounded mailbox quietly growing. The Disruptor is prior art standing beside the bus's own park-don't-poll: both replace discovery with arrival, a parked consumer and a waiting applier each costing nothing until the moment there is work.

**Lanes and groups with isolated persistence.** Part III gave every group a fair lane; Part IV asks what a group is allowed to remember when its node dies. The answer is deliberately narrow. Job rows are not remembered — they belong to the bus and the bus is volatile by D-2. What a group journals is the small set recovery can be held accountable to: the provenance names its rows have absorbed, the checkpoints of long work, and the last coherence version it applied. Each group gets its own journal — a single SQLite file owned by its consumer, one writer per file, which is the workload SQLite is built for — and the isolation is structural: one group's journal can lag, grow, or corrupt without touching a neighbor's, the same per-lane independence the rotation ring already enforces on fairness. Recovery is a replay, in order: open the journal, rebuild the provenance and checkpoint state, warm the L1 from the last-applied version forward, resume the lanes. A consumer that comes back is current before it claims its first job.

**Litestream as the rescue.** A journal beside the consumer survives a process crash; it does not survive the machine. Litestream closes that gap without bending D-2: it is an open-source tool that streams SQLite changes continuously to cloud or local object storage, runs as a separate process with no code changes in the application, and restores a database to the point of failure. The fit is exact — per-group journals are small single-writer SQLite files, the kind of thing object storage replicates for pennies — and the boundary is stated rather than hidden: Litestream's replication is asynchronous, so a restored journal may be missing its final moments. That tail is why the two-lanes law exists. A group whose memory matters re-derives the gap through the job-backed coherence lane — at-least-once delivery replays what the journal's tail lost, and the provenance it journaled earlier makes the replay idempotent. The division of labor is clean: the bus stays a volatile, fast fabric with no write-ahead log of its own; the journals beside it remember; Litestream carries the journals off-box; and replay stitches the three together into recovery.

**Chapter 4.5. The Cache Referee** — Nebulex's near-cache topology, Cachex, and Valkey's server-assisted tracking measured where they run: hit-path latency, the herd drill, and coherence-lag distributions, with the rows the comparison set cannot print — typed keys, mint-ordered newer-wins, a job-backed lane — beside the rows it wins.

## Boundaries of the part

The bus does not grow persistence in this part: D-2 stands, and D-3 keeps Streams on the EMQ 3.0 horizon — coherence rides the wake fabric and job lanes that already exist. Persistence here means journals beside consumers, nothing wider; the system of record remains the stores, and a cache is never consulted as truth. The comparison set will be measured at its defaults, with its advantages printed in their own rows, under the same referee discipline Part III applied to its rival — a near-cache that wins only against a hobbled baseline has not won anything.
