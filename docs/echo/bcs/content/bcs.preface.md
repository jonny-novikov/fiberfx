# The Branded Component System — Preface: boundaries around systems, not objects

<show-structure depth="2"/>

Every architecture series owes its reader one sentence that survives all the details. Here is this one's: **encapsulation boundaries are drawn around systems, not objects — and the only thing an object keeps, once its state and behavior have moved out, is its name.** The Branded Component System (BCS) is what follows when that observation is treated as a first-class architectural law and its single remainder — identity — is given a contract strong enough to carry the load. The law is older than it looks. It was discovered, in working code, by a game studio in Cambridge, Massachusetts in 1998, and the ideas it assembled are older still: an MIT memo from 1961, a thesis from 1963, an ALGOL Bulletin proposal from 1965. This preface walks that lineage, because the architecture the series builds is easier to trust once it is clear how little of it is new — and exactly which part is.

The part that is new is the identity. BCS takes the component-system inversion as settled and asks the question the lineage left open: if the object dissolves into an identifier, what must the identifier be? The series' answer is the branded snowflake contract — a 14-byte name carrying its domain type, its mint time, and its storage placement, specified once and conformant across the BEAM, Node, Go, PostgreSQL, and WebAssembly. The result is a compile-time hierarchy of encapsulation that matches the domain model, not the boundaries around the objects.

## Scope and sources

This is a historical essay, not a measured article; the series that follows returns to benchmarks and committed outputs. Every foundational claim here is tied to a primary source listed in the references — Ross's 1961 paper, Sutherland's thesis in its Cambridge reissue, Hoare's Record Handling, and Tom Leonard's published postmortem of Thief: The Dark Project. Where the text uses industry vernacular with no citable origin — "fat struct" above all — it is labeled as folklore. Direct quotation is kept to a few short phrases; the reading and the connections drawn between sources are this series' own.

## The model the industry inherited

By the mid-1990s the common entity model in C game code was a single structure with every field any entity might ever need — the fat struct, in the folklore name:

```c
typedef struct Entity {
    int        kind;        /* ENT_PLAYER, ENT_GUARD, ENT_BARREL, ...        */
    int        flags;       /* FLAMMABLE | CONTAINER | UNDEAD | LOCKED ...   */
    Vec3       pos, vel;
    int        hp;
    AIState    ai;          /* meaningful for guards                         */
    Inventory *inv;         /* meaningful for players and containers         */
    RenderRef  render;
    SoundRef   sound;
} Entity;                   /* every field, every entity, forever            */
```

The structure fails along two independent axes. First, every system touches every entity: the renderer walks an array in which most fields are noise to it, the AI walks the same array, and a change to any field's meaning ripples into every loop in the program. Second — subtler and more consequential — the `kind` and `flags` fields are a discriminant the compiler never sees. Nothing prevents reading `ai` on a barrel or `inv` on a guard; the program's correctness rests on a thousand hand-written `if` statements agreeing about what the integers mean.

Object orientation, ascendant in the same decade, offered the era's respectable alternative: draw the boundary around the object, hide the fields behind methods, and arrange the kinds in an inheritance tree. The boundary moved; the problem changed shape rather than size. Domains are not trees. The barrel that must burn *and* hold loot sits at a diamond between `Flammable` and `Container`, and every deep hierarchy in a shipping game accumulated such diamonds until behavior migrated into base classes that knew everything — the fat struct rebuilt out of virtual functions. The era's two models disagreed about where to draw the boundary around the object and never questioned that the object was the thing to draw it around.

## The genesis: Ross and the n-component element, 1961

The component is a decade older than that argument. In March 1961, Douglas T. Ross of MIT's Electronic Systems Laboratory published "A Generalized Technique for Symbol Manipulation and Numerical Calculation" in the Communications of the ACM [1], positioning his proposal against the list systems of the day — IPL, McCarthy's LISP, Perlis's threaded lists — on the grounds that pure list structure handled symbols well and numbers poorly. His alternative was the **n-component element**: a contiguous bundle of named components, each component free to hold symbolic links or numeric values, with algorithms written against the components they need. Ross's larger program — he called the discipline *plex*, and it grew through the AED project — treated data, structure, and algorithm as one design problem. Read from this distance, the 1961 element is the record before the record, the struct before C, and — the reading this series builds on — the *component* before the object: the unit of design is the named slice of an entity, not the entity whole. The fat struct is the n-component element's degenerate descendant: every component fused into one block, mandatory for everyone, with the discriminant decayed into unchecked flags.

## The parallel path: Sketchpad, 1963

Two years later and a few buildings away, Ivan Sutherland's January 1963 thesis described Sketchpad, the drawing system on Lincoln Laboratory's TX-2 [2]. The acknowledgments thank Douglas T. Ross of the Electronic Systems Laboratory by name, and the thesis builds its storage on n-component elements explicitly — the lineage is documented in the document itself, not reconstructed by historians. Sketchpad's elements were threaded on **ring structures**, one ring per type of thing, designed for "rapid processing of topological information with no searching at all" [2]: a procedure visits every line segment, or every constraint, by walking its ring.

Sketchpad is famous as an ancestor of object orientation — its master drawings and instances prefigure classes and objects, and that is the reading Alan Kay's tradition carried forward. The reading that matters here is the quieter one. Sketchpad's constraints were not behavior attached to objects; they were *data* — rows of a typed table, threaded on their own ring — and satisfaction was performed by a relaxation procedure that swept the ring and adjusted the drawing until the constraints held. A homogeneous table of typed components, iterated by an engine that owns the rule: that is a system, in the component-system sense, running in 1963. Two futures were latent in one thesis. The loud one became Simula, Smalltalk, and the boundary around the object. The quiet one — tables of typed things plus engines that sweep them — waited thirty-five years for a game studio to need it badly enough.

## Deeper origins: Hoare and the discriminant, 1965

The third foundation is the discipline of the tag. C.A.R. Hoare's "Record Handling," circulated in the ALGOL Bulletin in 1965 under an epigraph borrowed from Occam — *entia non sunt multiplicanda praeter necessitatem* — proposed dynamically created records, grouped into declared **record classes**, reachable only through **references** that are themselves typed by class [3]. The 1966 development of the proposal added subclasses and the discrimination of cases: a value belongs to exactly one variant, the variant tag is part of the value, and access under the wrong tag is a checked error rather than a quiet misreading. This is the discriminated union, and it is the exact discipline the fat struct lacked — `kind` was a discriminant living outside the type system, enforced by convention and broken by every refactor. Hoare's proposal flowed into ALGOL W and, through Dahl and Nygaard's reading, into Simula 67's classes; the mainstream took the records and largely set the discrimination aside for thirty years, until ML's descendants and the gradual typing movement carried it back. Hold the idea: *the tag belongs inside the value, and the type system guards access by it.* BCS will compile that 1965 sentence into the first three bytes of every identifier.

## Looking Glass, 1998: the boundary moves

Thief: The Dark Project shipped in December 1998 from Looking Glass Studios, nineteen developers and two near-cancellations into a two-and-a-half-year project [4]. Tom Leonard's postmortem in Game Developer names the design goal — "an object-rich world governed by high-quality, self-consistent simulation systems" [4] — and names the architecture that delivered it: the Dark Object System, designed primarily by Marc LeBlanc. The postmortem describes it as a general database over the simulation's objects, built on a generic notion of *properties* an object may possess and *relations* that may hold between two objects, mature enough that designers specified most of the game's behavior without scripting or programmer intervention [4].

Read structurally, every load-bearing piece of the inherited model has moved. State left the entity and lives in per-property tables — a position table, a flammability table, an AI-alertness table — each owned by the one system that understands it. Behavior left the methods and lives in systems that sweep their tables, Sketchpad's relaxation loop grown up. Kind left the inheritance tree and became composition of data: an archetype is a bundle of property values, a concrete object inherits them by reference and overrides what it must, and the burning lootable barrel is two properties attached to one object rather than a diamond in a class graph. Encapsulation did not weaken in the move — it *relocated*. Nobody but the AI system touches alertness; nobody but the physics system touches velocity. The boundary that OO drew around each object, the Dark Engine drew around each system. And the object itself? Strip away properties held in other people's tables and relations held in other people's graphs, and what remains of an object is its identifier. The entity became a join key.

## The remainder is identity

That remainder is where the lineage stopped, and where every weakness of the component-system tradition since 1998 has lived. The identifier the industry settled on was a process-local integer — an index into an array, sometimes dressed with a generation counter to detect reuse. Leonard's own postmortem touches the symptom from the era's edge: the resource layer's integer IDs needed a special compilation step and bound assets to a management scheme rather than a name [4]. The pattern generalizes. An integer entity ID means nothing after the process exits, so saving a game is a swizzling pass over every table. It means nothing on another machine, so replication invents a second identity scheme and a mapping. It means nothing to a database, so persistence translates at every boundary between the runtime's keys and the store's keys. It carries no type, so the wrong-table join — the AI system asked about a particle effect — fails at runtime if it is lucky. The component-system inversion gave systems a clean boundary and then handed them, as the only thing permitted to cross it, the weakest value in the program.

BCS begins where that stops: if identity is the one thing that crosses every boundary, identity deserves the strongest contract in the system, not the weakest.

## What the branded id supplies

The branded snowflake contract — specified normatively in this project's `contract/contract.md`, with vectors every runtime must pass — is the completed remainder, and each of its properties answers one chapter of the history.

The **namespace** is Hoare's discriminant, carried in the value and in the type. The first three bytes of `CRS0KHTOWnGLuC` declare its domain class on the wire, and the compile-time brand declares it to the type system: a `BrandedId<'USR'>` does not pass where a `BrandedId<'CRS'>` is required in TypeScript, an Ecto parameterized type rejects the foreign namespace at the changeset, a PostgreSQL domain rejects it at the column, a Fastify schema rejects it before the handler runs. The set of namespaces is the domain vocabulary, and each system declares — in types and schemas, checked before the program runs — which parts of that vocabulary its boundary admits. This is the compile-time hierarchy of encapsulation matching the domain model: the hierarchy lives in what systems accept, not in what objects extend.

The **order theorem** makes every property table a timeline. Because the text form sorts as the mint instant sorts, a system's table keyed by branded id is chronological by construction — the feed, the cursor, and the time-window scan come from the key itself, with no timestamp column and no sort. The **placement hash** makes storage addressable from identity alone: the same 32-bit function routes a CHAMP trie slot, a cache shard, and a hash partition, so any system can compute where another system's row physically lives without asking. And the **canon** — one Rust source, one C reference, one vector file, five conformant implementations — makes "system" a deployment decision rather than a language commitment: the boundary may enclose an OTP application, a Fastify worker, a Go service, or a set of PostgreSQL partitions, and the identifier means the same thing on every side.

## The law, stated

The Branded Component System is three clauses. Systems own their state and their behavior, encapsulated behind a boundary no other system reaches through. The only values that cross boundaries are identities and messages about identities. And identity is a contract — typed by namespace, ordered by mint time, placed by hash, conformant across runtimes — so that the type system can verify, before anything runs, that the flow of identities between systems matches the domain model. The shift from the inherited models is a relocation of one line: the boundary that thirty years of practice drew around the object, BCS draws around the system, and it spends everything saved on making the object's last possession — its name — strong enough to be the architecture's foundation.

## The series ahead

The substrate already exists and is measured: the contract specification and its vector file, the conformant implementations in Elixir, Rust, TypeScript, Go, and SQL, and five published articles of benchmarks behind them. The series builds the system layer on top. The opening parts read the identity contract as BCS's foundation stone and the per-property storage question on the BEAM — ETS tables and CHAMP forests as property databases owned by supervised systems. The middle of the series crosses process boundaries: EchoMQ as the bus on which identities and messages about identities travel between BEAM, Node, and Go systems, with the namespace gates standing at every consumer. The later parts treat relations as their own systems, queries and feeds as direct consequences of the order theorem, and PostgreSQL — the custom type, the domains, the contract-hash partitions — as a system whose boundary happens to be a database. The image-generation pipeline built earlier in this project will be re-read, by then, as what it quietly already is: a first Branded Component System, with Fastify minting identities, BullMQ carrying messages about them, and every table along the way keyed by a name that means the same thing everywhere.
