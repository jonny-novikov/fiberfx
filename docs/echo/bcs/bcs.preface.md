# The Branded Component System — Preface: boundaries around systems, not objects, on a floor that remembers

<show-structure depth="2"/>

Every architecture series owes its reader one sentence that survives all the details. Here is this one's: **encapsulation boundaries are drawn around systems, not objects — and the only thing an object keeps, once its state and behavior have moved out, is its name.** The Branded Component System (BCS) is what follows when that observation is treated as a first-class architectural law and its single remainder — identity — is given a contract strong enough to carry the load. The law is older than it looks: it was discovered in working code by a game studio in 1998, and the ideas it assembled reach back to an MIT memo of 1961, a thesis of 1963, and an ALGOL Bulletin proposal of 1965. This preface walks that lineage, because the architecture is easier to trust once it is clear how little of it is new and exactly which part is — and then it adds the movement this edition of the preface exists to add: the durable floor the whole stack now stands on.

## Scope and sources

The lineage below is a historical essay tied to primary sources — Ross's 1961 paper, Sutherland's thesis, Hoare's *Record Handling*, and Tom Leonard's postmortem of Thief: The Dark Project — and the connections drawn between them are this series' own. The closing movement on persistence is not history but as-built architecture: it stands on shipped source in this repository (the `echo_store` engines, the `echo_cache` near-cache, the EchoMQ bus) and on the storage engines those modules embed. Direct quotation is kept to a few short phrases; where the text uses vernacular with no citable origin — "fat struct" above all — it is labelled folklore.

## The model the industry inherited

By the mid-1990s the common entity model in C game code was a single structure with every field any entity might ever need — the fat struct, in the folklore name:

```c
typedef struct Entity {
    int        kind;        /* ENT_PLAYER, ENT_GUARD, ENT_BARREL, ...        */
    int        flags;       /* FLAMMABLE | CONTAINER | UNDEAD | LOCKED ...   */
    Vec3       pos, vel;
    int        hp;
    AIState    ai;          /* meaningful for guards                         */
    Inventory *inv;         /* meaningful for players and containers         */
    RenderRef  render;
    SoundRef   sound;
} Entity;                   /* every field, every entity, forever            */
```

It fails along two axes. Every system touches every entity — the renderer walks an array in which most fields are noise to it, the AI walks the same array, and a change to one field's meaning ripples into every loop. And the `kind`/`flags` fields are a discriminant the compiler never sees: nothing stops a read of `ai` on a barrel, and correctness rests on a thousand hand-written `if` statements agreeing about what the integers mean. Object orientation offered the era's respectable alternative — draw the boundary around the object, hide the fields behind methods, arrange the kinds in a tree — but domains are not trees. The barrel that must burn *and* hold loot sits at a diamond, and every deep hierarchy accumulated such diamonds until behavior migrated into base classes that knew everything: the fat struct rebuilt out of virtual functions. The two models disagreed about where to draw the boundary around the object and never questioned that the object was the thing to draw it around.

## The lineage: component, table, and tag

The component is older than that argument. In March 1961 Douglas T. Ross of MIT published the **n-component element** [1] — a contiguous bundle of named components, each free to hold symbolic links or numeric values, with algorithms written against the components they need. Read from this distance it is the component before the object: the unit of design is the named slice of an entity, not the entity whole, and the fat struct is its degenerate descendant — every component fused into one block, the discriminant decayed into unchecked flags.

Two years later Ivan Sutherland's Sketchpad thesis [2] built its storage on Ross's elements explicitly, threaded on **ring structures**, one ring per type, for processing with no searching. Sketchpad is famous as an ancestor of object orientation; the quieter reading matters here. Its constraints were not behavior attached to objects, they were *data* — rows of a typed table, swept by a relaxation procedure that adjusted the drawing until they held. A homogeneous table of typed components iterated by an engine that owns the rule is a system, in the component-system sense, running in 1963. Two futures were latent in one thesis: the loud one became the boundary around the object; the quiet one — tables plus engines that sweep them — waited thirty-five years.

The third foundation is the tag. C. A. R. Hoare's *Record Handling* [3] proposed dynamically created records, grouped into declared classes, reachable only through references typed by class, with the discrimination of cases: a value belongs to one variant, the variant tag is part of the value, and access under the wrong tag is a checked error. This is the discipline the fat struct lacked — `kind` was a discriminant living outside the type system. Hold the sentence: *the tag belongs inside the value, and the type system guards access by it.* BCS compiles it into the first three bytes of every identifier.

## Looking Glass, 1998: the boundary moves

Thief: The Dark Project shipped in December 1998 [4] on the Dark Object System — a general database over the simulation's objects, built on a generic notion of *properties* an object may possess and *relations* that may hold between two objects, mature enough that designers specified most behavior without a programmer. Read structurally, every load-bearing piece of the inherited model moved. State left the entity for per-property tables, each owned by the one system that understands it. Behavior left the methods for systems that sweep those tables — Sketchpad's loop grown up. Kind left the inheritance tree and became composition of data: an archetype is a bundle of property values a concrete object inherits by reference. Encapsulation did not weaken; it *relocated*. The boundary OO drew around each object, the Dark Engine drew around each system. Strip away properties held in other systems' tables and relations held in other systems' graphs, and what remains of an object is its identifier. The entity became a join key.

## The remainder is identity

That remainder is where the lineage stopped. The identifier the industry settled on was a process-local integer — an index into an array, sometimes dressed with a generation counter. It means nothing after the process exits, so a save is a swizzling pass over every table; nothing on another machine, so replication invents a second identity scheme; nothing to a database, so persistence translates at every boundary; and it carries no type, so the wrong-table join fails at runtime if it is lucky. The component-system inversion gave systems a clean boundary and then handed them, as the only thing permitted to cross it, the weakest value in the program. BCS begins where that stops: if identity is the one thing that crosses every boundary, identity deserves the strongest contract in the system, not the weakest.

## What the branded id supplies

The branded snowflake contract — specified in this project's `contract/contract.md`, with vectors every runtime must pass — is the completed remainder, and each property answers one chapter of the history. The **namespace** is Hoare's discriminant carried in the value and in the type: the first three bytes of `CRS0KHTOWnGLuC` declare the domain class on the wire, and the compile-time brand declares it to the type system, so each system declares — in types and schemas, checked before the program runs — which parts of the vocabulary its boundary admits. The **order theorem** makes every property table a timeline: because the text form sorts as the mint instant sorts, a table keyed by branded id is chronological by construction, with no timestamp column and no sort. The **placement hash** makes storage addressable from identity alone: one 32-bit function routes a trie slot, a cache shard, and a hash partition alike. And the **canon** — one Rust source, one C reference, one vector file, conformant across the BEAM, Node, Go, PostgreSQL, and WebAssembly — makes "system" a deployment decision rather than a language commitment.

## The law, stated

The Branded Component System is three clauses. Systems own their state and their behavior, encapsulated behind a boundary no other system reaches through. The only values that cross boundaries are identities and messages about identities. And identity is a contract — typed by namespace, ordered by mint time, placed by hash, conformant across runtimes — so the type system can verify, before anything runs, that the flow of identities between systems matches the domain model. The shift from the inherited models is the relocation of one line: the boundary thirty years of practice drew around the object, BCS draws around the system, and it spends everything saved on making the object's last possession — its name — strong enough to be the architecture's foundation.

## The floor beneath the boundary

A law about what crosses boundaries leaves one question open: where does the state inside a boundary live once the process exits? For most of this series the answer was a fast, volatile one — ETS for a system's own tables, and EchoMQ on Valkey for the messages about identities that travel between systems. Volatile is the right default for a bus, and the wrong one for a ledger. **Echo Persistence** is the floor that closes the gap: a durable substrate beneath the bus, built so that durability is a dial a system turns, not a rewrite it undertakes.

The substrate is a ladder of four tiers, each with one job. ETS is the in-process head cache, derived and safe to drop. Valkey is the bus and the warm L2 — fast, volatile. Beneath them is a durable local page tier built, deliberately, twice: a native Elixir engine (`EchoStore.Graft.*`) on CubDB [5] — an append-only, immutable B-tree with zero-cost MVCC snapshots — and a Rust twin (`echo_graft`) on Fjall [6], a log-structured merge engine, for raw-page and replica-recovery work, driven over the bus under a non-colliding name. Both fold their segments down to Tigris [7], remote object storage, behind a single conditional-write fence — a create-only `If-None-Match` precondition that lets exactly one writer claim a commit slot, the same kind of atomic first-claim the leaderboard uses one tier up.

Two properties make this a floor and not a bolt-on. First, durability is one knob: a queue is placed on a dial — hold nothing, hold a bounded in-heap window with a checkpoint per K, or commit per record and replicate off-box — and the only durability the enqueue hot path ever touches is a small, mostly-idle outbox standing beside the bus, never a database on the path of every dequeue and ack. Telemetry rides ETS and Valkey alone; ordinary work adds the local page tier; a payment engages all four. Second, the substrate is addressed the way the rest of the architecture is addressed. `EchoStore.Table` is an ETS read cache over the durable tier, and `EchoStore.Coherence` keeps it correct with a newer-wins rule whose version *is the 14-byte branded id* — because a branded id sorts in mint order, "newer" is "greater id," and a cache update is, once more, a message about a name. Set durable, replicated, coherent state, addressed by identity, read without locks, side by side, and they describe not a queue but a place to keep any system's state. The bus was the first thing the platform built on it — the load-bearing proof the floor holds — and EchoMQ now dogfoods the page engine as its own durable spine, with the planned Stream Tier archiving there.

That reframes the whole series. The durable floor is the substrate BCS was shaped for: entities are identities, components are data keyed by those identities, and the floor already stores state by branded id and already speaks in messages about a name. Which member of the loop should lead the road ahead — the bus finishing its Stream Tier frontier, or the engine deepening toward its durability north star — is a live fork the platform holds open and the Operator owns; this preface records it rather than resolves it. The comparison that matters is named where it belongs: Oban [8] keeps jobs in the same PostgreSQL as the application's data, so a job and a row commit in one transaction, and that coupling is a real advantage Echo gives up; Echo separates the bus from the store and buys, in return, an in-memory hot path and a durability dial that the database-on-the-hot-path design cannot offer. The series records the trade beside the win.

## The series ahead

The substrate exists and is measured: the contract and its vector file, the conformant implementations in Elixir, Rust, TypeScript, Go, and SQL, and the articles of benchmarks behind them. The opening parts read the identity contract as the foundation stone and the per-property storage question on the BEAM — ETS tables and CHAMP forests as property databases owned by supervised systems. The middle crosses process boundaries: EchoMQ as the bus on which identities and messages about identities travel between BEAM, Node, and Go systems, the namespace gates standing at every consumer, and the near-cache whose coherence message carries a mint-time version. The new part stands beneath them — the persistence floor: the durability dial, the page engine built twice, the commit-LSN loop where the engine's commit drives the bus and the archive fold drives the engine's commit, and the door where the durable substrate is read, plainly, as the place BCS keeps its state. The later parts treat relations as their own systems, queries and feeds as consequences of the order theorem, PostgreSQL as a system whose boundary is a database, and the trading capstone assembled on the as-built tree. The image-generation pipeline built earlier is re-read, by then, as what it quietly already is: a first Branded Component System, with identities minted at the edge and the bus carrying messages about them, every table along the way keyed by a name that means the same thing everywhere.

The course teaches this book and doors out where another course owns the depth: `/echomq` for the bus protocol rung by rung, `/redis-patterns` for the substrate patterns under it, `/echo-persistence` for the durable floor in full — its fourteen modules from the durability spectrum to the door back to BCS — and `/elixir` for the umbrella where the production identity library lives.
