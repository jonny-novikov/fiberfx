A9 · EchoMesh in Depth — the second half of the heart

EchoMesh in depth.
The heart, made precise.

The heart deserves two chapters, and this is the second. A8 introduced EchoMesh; A9 takes the same substrate from introduced to understood — the design choices behind partition-survival made precise, and the architecture that carries them drawn in full. This is where the proposal earns its detail.

The depth half of the heart. A8 set out what EchoMesh is and why a partition is survivable; here the per-subsystem PACELC design and the architecture under the hood are taken to their foundations. Two dives carry it — each with an interactive emulator of failure and recovery on its page. The chapter after, A10 · The Whole Picture (soon), stands the whole course back beside what it declined.

Proposed · not shipped

EchoMesh remains a forward concept this course introduces; it does not yet exist in code, and every claim about the mesh here is proposed. Its constituent pieces are real and shipped — distributed Erlang, the canon’s audited hash, the lanes, the claim-check bus, the journal, and the investex venue client — but their composition into a partition-surviving, locality-aware mesh is the proposed design this chapter draws in detail. The interactive figures are teaching models of that design, not a console onto running infrastructure.

§1

Why a depth chapter

art9.md · why depth

The introduction could state that EchoMesh chooses consistency for matching and availability for market data; it could not show why those are the only defensible choices, what they cost on a healthy network as well as a partitioned one, or how a book moves from a lost node to a live one without ever having two writers. Those are the questions a reader who accepts the thesis asks next, and they are the substance of an architecture rather than a pitch.

A9 answers them in two dives: one on the consistency model, one on the mechanism. Each dive’s page carries an interactive emulator, so the reader can inject a partition or drop a node and watch the design respond rather than take its behavior on the page’s word. The emulators are teaching models of the proposed design, not a console onto running infrastructure.

§2

The PACELC design

art9.md · the consistency model

The first dive sets EchoMesh’s place in the framework that governs every distributed system. The CAP theorem is the established constraint: under a network partition, a system chooses between consistency and availability, and no design escapes that — a point the dive treats as given rather than something the mesh re-derives. PACELC is the fuller framework the design is built against: it adds that even on a healthy network, a system that replicates trades latency against consistency on every request, which is the cost most users feel, since partitions are rare and requests are constant.

A distinction is load-bearing here. The consistency PACELC reasons about is CAP-consistency — linearizability, one current value across replicas — and that is a different property from the ACID-consistency a regulated ledger keeps within a transaction. EchoMesh’s design is a per-subsystem assignment within PACELC: matching and the regulated ledger consistency-first, market data availability-and-latency-first. The dive makes each choice precise, including why the single-writer principle lets the consistency-first choice avoid a quorum, and how an ownership epoch fences a stranded writer so that re-placement never produces a split brain.

The single-writer principle is what makes the consistency-first column affordable: because one process is the sole mutator of each book, the consistency-first choice needs no quorum vote — it needs only that the one writer be unique. The ownership epoch is the fence that keeps it unique across re-placement: when a book is re-homed onto a survivor, the epoch rises, and a stranded former owner that returns finds its epoch stale and steps down rather than writing. Pick two corners below to see why a partition forces the choice the assignment above makes.

§3

The architecture, under the hood

art9.md · the mechanism

The second dive draws the mechanism. As designed, EchoMesh is one distributed-Erlang cluster whose nodes sit in different regions and on different providers; books are placed on owning nodes by a consistent-hashing ring over the canon’s audited hash, so any node computes a book’s owner with arithmetic and a lost node re-homes only its own share of books rather than the whole cluster. An order crosses at most one node boundary; results leave their owner as small claims rather than copied objects.

A failed node’s books resume elsewhere by replaying the journal under a fresh ownership epoch; and a partition splits the cluster into a side that keeps each book consistent-and-available and a side that refuses it, while market data stays available on both. The second dive shows each of these as a worked path, and its page lets the reader drive failover and recovery directly. Three results compose here; each is one face of the architecture, and the second dive takes each to depth.

Mechanism one

Placement by the consistent-hashing ring

Nodes and books map onto one circular hash space over the audited hash; a book is owned by the first node clockwise, so a membership change remaps only about one node’s share, and any node computes an owner with arithmetic alone — no coordinator asked.

Mechanism two

Failover by journal-replay under a rising epoch

A lost node’s books resume on a survivor by replaying the journal to its last state; the ownership epoch rises with the re-placement, so a stranded former owner that returns finds its epoch stale and steps down rather than writing — no split brain.

Mechanism three

Locality as the latency budget

A book is placed where its instrument’s flow already is, and an order crosses at most one node boundary, so a soft-real-time budget is kept across a spread mesh by where a process lives rather than by tuning after the fact.

Sources

  • Gilbert, S. & Lynch, N. (2002) — Brewer’s Conjecture and the Feasibility of CAPthe formal statement and proof of the CAP theorem: of consistency (linearizability), availability, and partition tolerance a distributed data store guarantees at most two, so under a partition it chooses between consistency and availability — the established constraint A9.1 designs within rather than re-proves
  • Abadi, D. — The PACELC design principle (2010 / 2012)the extension of CAP this chapter’s design is framed by: in a Partition choose Availability or Consistency; Else, on a healthy network, choose between Latency and Consistency — because keeping replicas consistent costs coordination, and coordination costs latency on every request
  • Karger, D. et al. (1997) — Consistent Hashing and Random Treesthe placement scheme behind the mesh’s ring: nodes and keys mapped onto one circular hash space, a key owned by the first node clockwise, so a membership change remaps only about one node’s share; “consistent” denotes stable placement under change, not replica or ACID consistency
  • Erlang Solutions — BEAM and JVM Virtual Machinesthe BEAM’s built-in distribution model: nodes forming a cluster and addressing each other’s processes by message-passing, with monitors and links for failure detection — the substrate the mesh’s membership, placement, and ownership fence run over
