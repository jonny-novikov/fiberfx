A0.1 · The Credo — The Art of BCS

Own the runtime.
The arithmetic says why.

The credo is one sentence reached after two decades of building distributed systems in finance, after enough postmortems whose root cause was a dependency the team did not write, could not inspect, and could not fix while it was down. This dive takes its three load-bearing claims — that the patterns are overpriced, that borrowed availability fails, and that owning the runtime changes the ceiling — and gives each the argument that turns a slogan into engineering.

The first dive of A0 · Orientation, taught from the manuscript chapter art01.md. The arithmetic is the spine; the cloud’s own published SLAs are the evidence, stated fairly before they are declined. The claim is narrow on purpose, never anti-cloud zealotry. EchoMesh, named at the end as the destination, is a forward concept the course introduces — not yet shipped.

§1

Overpriced — the tax of the constellation

art01.md · art.prelude.md

The enterprise pattern, concretely, is the constellation the course’s runtime dive maps: a coordinator for membership and election, a log broker for the event stream, a message broker for queues, and an orchestrator to run them all — each a separate distributed system acquired to supply a property. Calling it overpriced is not a remark about licensing, though that is real; it is the observation that each piece is paid for three times — in the dollars of running it, the latency of crossing it, and the availability it subtracts when it fails — to obtain properties the BEAM already provides for the cost of writing ordinary code.

The patterns earn their keep when a system spans many teams and many organizations and outgrows any single runtime. For a single-organization, soft-real-time exchange they are a tax on properties the runtime holds for free — and the heaviest part of the bill is paid in the currency that matters most for real time, which is availability. The credo’s three claims trace that bill from price to ceiling.

Claim one

The patterns are overpriced

Each member of the constellation is a separate distributed system acquired to supply a property the runtime already has. The bill is paid three times over — dollars, latency, and the availability subtracted on failure — for what the BEAM provides as ordinary code.

the bill is paid in · availability

Claim two

Borrowed availability fails

Managed does not mean never-down, and an SLA is a credit schedule, not a guarantee. Availability composes by multiplication, so the arithmetic is why — even in the months every piece meets its SLA — the composed product can sit below the line.

and when the dependency is down · you wait

Claim three

Owning the runtime changes the ceiling

Owning the engine removes the third-party terms whose product capped you, so achievable availability becomes a function of one’s own design rather than of others’ SLAs multiplied together. The nine-nines BEAM is read as a statement about design.

the ceiling is a function of · your own design

§2

Borrowed availability, derived

art01.md · AWS Compute SLA

State the cloud’s case fairly first, because an argument is only as strong as the fairest version of what it declines. The highest commitment AWS makes for compute is a monthly uptime of 99.99% for instances spread across availability zones, with a single instance committed to 99.5%, and no service offered at 100%. Four nines is roughly fifty-three minutes of permitted unavailability a month; it is a serious number, and the providers largely meet it.

The trouble is not any single number; it is how they compose. A real system depends on a coordinator, a log, a broker, a managed database, a load balancer, a secrets store — and availability composes by multiplication. A system on N independent third-party services, each available with probability a, has a ceiling of aN — the same function the calculator above computes. Put the published numbers through it:

The ceiling sits below the weakest single SLA, and it falls as the stack grows. Two candid additions complete the picture. An SLA is a credit schedule, not a guarantee — the provider’s commercially reasonable effort backed by a refund, not a promise the system stays up. And when a managed dependency is the one that is down, you wait: there is no way to open its source, attach a debugger, or ship the fix, which is the operational meaning of maintained by a third party. That a managed service can go down is uncontroversial; the arithmetic is why, even in the months every piece meets its SLA, the whole can still sit below the line a real-time system needs.

§3

Why that does not fit a real-time system

art01.md · the stalls on the path

A soft-real-time system meets its deadlines predictably for the work that matters, and predictability is exactly what borrowed availability destroys. The constellation’s failure modes are not graceful slowdowns; they are stalls on the path — unbounded waits the system did not choose and cannot end. Select one to read which member of the constellation it belongs to, the moment it blocks, and why it cannot be inspected while it happens.

readout · Select a stall above. Each of the three cards below is the static map: a different member of the constellation, blocking at a different moment, landing on the hot path as an unbounded wait that cannot be inspected while it happens.

A quorum re-electing

→ the coordinator

A coordinator round trip blocks while a quorum re-elects a leader. The wait is however long agreement takes, not a budget the venue set — and the matching engine on the other side cannot proceed until it returns.

A group rebalancing

→ the message broker

A broker pauses delivery while a consumer group rebalances partitions across its members. Work in flight waits for the reassignment to settle — a pause injected by the broker’s own coordination, not the load.

A partition unavailable

→ the log broker

A log partition goes unavailable mid-write, so an append blocks until the partition has a leader again. The write does not fail fast; it hangs on the path, and the deadline it was meant to meet passes while it does.

Where the missing time lands

It is not only that the nines are too few; it is that the missing time arrives as a spike in the place a matching engine can least absorb it, injected by a system that cannot be inspected while it is happening. A graceful slowdown can be planned around; an unbounded wait on the hot path cannot. That is the difference between a number that is occasionally low and a property a real-time venue cannot rely on.

§4

What owning the runtime changes

art01.md · Armstrong, thesis 2003

Against that, the BEAM was built at Ericsson for systems that were not allowed to stop, with supervision and process isolation as the basis of recovery. The figure most often attached to that lineage is the AXD301 switch’s nine nines — which, candor in advance, Armstrong’s own thesis reports with reservations, noting the long-term evidence was not collected systematically. So the credo’s nine-nines BEAM is read here as a statement about design, not a guarantee of outcome.

What can be claimed without overreach is the ceiling: owning the engine removes the third-party terms whose product capped the achievable availability, so achievable availability becomes a function of one’s own design rather than of others’ SLAs multiplied together. The runtime was built so that very high availability is reachable by construction and by the removal of borrowed availability — not bought as a dependency, and not promised as a number.

The destination where the last third-party term finally disappears is EchoMesh, the distributed substrate the course builds toward: a mesh spread across machines, regions, and providers, where no single provider owns the system’s coordination, log, or state, so the loss of one — even Fly — is a reduction in capacity rather than an outage. EchoMesh is a forward concept the course introduces, not yet shipped; it is named here as the point at which the arithmetic of borrowed availability is escaped, because there is no borrowed term left to multiply the ceiling down. The next dive is the runtime that makes the conviction buildable.
