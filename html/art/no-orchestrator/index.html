A5 · No Orchestrator — The Art of BCS

Why you don’t need an
orchestrator. Five jobs, each smaller.

Part V is the fourth and last of the four arguments. The previous chapter retired the message broker; this one takes the orchestrator — the control plane a containerized system runs to schedule, scale, heal, discover, and roll out its parts. The claim is the most carefully bounded of the four, because orchestration does not vanish so much as shrink.

State the tool fairly first. Kubernetes is a capable system, and at the scale of many heterogeneous services across many teams it is the right tool. The chapter takes its five jobs one at a time and answers each at a smaller grain, then draws the line and names the exit. Each figure here is verbatim from the manuscript or its cited sources. The senior continuation of /bcs.

§1

The orchestrator, stated fairly

art5.md · kubernetes.io/concepts/overview

State the tool fairly first. Kubernetes automates the deployment, scaling, self-healing, service discovery, and rollout of containerized applications — scheduling containers across a cluster, restarting failed ones, giving groups of them stable names and load balancing, and rolling out new versions. It is a capable system, and at the scale of many heterogeneous services across many teams it is the right tool. The course does not dispute that scale; it observes that a single-organization BEAM system has not reached it.

The system

A general-purpose container orchestrator

A control plane that schedules containers across a cluster, restarts failed ones, gives groups of them stable names and load balancing, and rolls out new versions — run as its own distributed system.

the five jobs a conventional architecture hands off

The grain

The container and the node

Healing at the level of the container and the node, on the timescale of a reschedule and a cold start — the wrong instrument for a single-writer book that must fail over in the time it takes to restart a process.

a grain matched to many heterogeneous services

The far end

Where it is the right tool

Many heterogeneous services across many teams and languages, with complex placement and policy. A single-organization BEAM system on Fly, with one identity contract across its stores, is not at it.

§2

The five jobs, answered job by job

art5.md · the runtime’s answer

Take the five jobs one at a time. Each has a smaller, closer answer for a single-organization BEAM system, and the grain shrinks: from the container to the process, from the cluster to a single machine, from a separate component to the runtime’s own distribution layer. The selector in the hero walks the schematic; the cards below state the three the runtime carries directly, with deployment and scaling drawn out in the next section.

Self-healing

→ supervision

A failed process is restarted to a known state at the grain of the process, one instrument, the rest of the node undisturbed — where the orchestrator would reschedule a whole container.

Service discovery

→ distributed Erlang

Nodes name each other in the runtime’s own distribution layer rather than a separate service-discovery component — the same property the coordinator chapter leaned on.

Rollout

→ the release

A new version deployed onto fresh machines, with the runtime’s ability to load code into a running system available but not required.

The grain the chapter keeps in view

The orchestrator heals at the level of the container and the node, on the timescale of a reschedule and a cold start. Supervision heals at the grain of the process, in the time it takes to restart one. For a single-writer book that must fail over in that time, the smaller grain is the right instrument, and the runtime already runs it.

§3

Deployment, scaling, and the Fly machine

art5.md · fly machines · FLAME

The remaining two jobs — deployment and scaling — move to the platform’s thin machine layer and an elastic-compute library. The deployment grain is the Fly machine: a fast-launching virtual machine that starts and stops at subsecond speed with per-region placement, onto which a release is deployed. Scaling, and elastic compute for bursty work, is FLAME: wrapping a function to run the whole application on short-lived machines, a pool that grows under load and scales to zero, with no horizontal pod autoscaler to operate — and a Fly machine scales to zero by exiting its process when idle, intact to start again from a clean slate.

This is elastic compute by a library and a thin machine layer rather than a control plane the team runs. The Phoenix engine, the umbrella, and the Fly deployment chapter are taught in depth on /elixir; the GPU-accelerated money-math worker fleet drained on those machines is the third dive.

§4

The line: orchestration shrinks, it does not vanish

art5.md · the line drawn

The candor here is the most important of the four chapters, because the claim invites overstatement. Orchestration does not disappear: a system runs on Fly machines, and the platform’s own machine layer is, in its makers’ words, the lowest level of orchestration, with Fly Launch the orchestrator that deploys and scales releases on top of it. What the chapter retires is the self-operated, general-purpose container orchestrator — a Kubernetes control plane run as its own distributed system, with operators and autoscalers encoding what should happen. The five jobs move to the runtime’s process supervision, the platform’s thin machine layer, and an elastic-compute library, none of which the team operates as a separate consensus system. Orchestration shrinks rather than vanishes.

§5

The line, and the named exit

art5.md · the named exit

The exit is named as ever: a fleet of many heterogeneous services across many teams and languages, with complex placement and policy, is where a general-purpose orchestrator earns its place — which a single-organization BEAM system on Fly has not reached. The refusal is bounded in advance rather than discovered late: a refusal that cannot say where it would be reversed is a slogan, not an argument. Mark which conditions hold to read the verdict.
