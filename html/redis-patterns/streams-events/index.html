R5 · Streams & Events

The durable,
replayable log.

A row says what is true now; a Redis Stream says everything that ever happened, in order — append every event, replay to rebuild state, and let many readers fold the same log at their own pace.

A stream is not a work queue but an append-only log: a read moves a cursor instead of removing the entry, so the entry stays until each reader acknowledges it. The grounding is EchoMQ's real shipped Stream Tier (echo/apps/echo_mq) — EchoMQ.Stream.append/4 issues XADD emq:{q}:stream:<name> and returns the branded EVT id, EchoMQ.StreamConsumer carries the consumer-group reader law, and EchoMQ.StreamRetention trims the log under a named policy — worked through codemojex's activity feed. This chapter records the log that R6–R8 lean on.

§1

Why & when

echo/apps/echo_mq

A plain key holds the latest value and forgets the rest. The moment the history matters as much as the present — an audit trail, an activity feed, a value that must be reconstructable as of a past instant, a state machine other readers also consume — a single overwritten value is not enough. Reach for streams whenever the record of what happened must outlive the latest value, and many independent readers each need to consume that record at their own pace. Each demand below has one matching technique, and the chapter is the set of those answers.

The history is the truth

Keep the immutable log and derive the present by folding it — nothing is overwritten, every transition is appended, the state is recomputed from the start.

Many readers, one log, different speeds

A read moves a cursor, not the entry — one reader can sit at the tail while another replays from the beginning, and the entry stays until each acknowledges it.

A reader must wait, not busy-poll

A blocking read parks until the next entry arrives, so a caught-up consumer costs the engine nothing and wakes the instant there is work.

The log must stay bounded

Retention is a policy, not a default — trim the live log by length or age, and fold what is trimmed to a durable floor so deep history survives without resident memory.

Every technique in this chapter answers one demand the log makes good on: the history as the truth, many readers at their own pace, a reader that waits without polling, and a log kept bounded with its deep history folded to a durable floor.

§3

How to apply

name the demand · the technique follows

The hard part is matching the stream technique to the demand you have. A stream is read non-destructively, and the four moves below are how a reader stays reliable while many readers share the one log. Name the demand, and the technique — and the real EchoMQ surface that implements it — follows.

There is no single streams trick — only the technique that answers the demand you have, each one a real move in EchoMQ's Stream Tier.

§4

The workshop

echo/apps/codemojex · EchoMQ.Stream

The chapter closes with R5.05: a live codemojex activity feed built off the :events stream lifecycle. Each event the game produces — a guess submitted, a round opened, a settlement posted — is appended to emq:{q}:stream:events with EchoMQ.Stream.append/4, minting a branded EVT id as the receipt. The feed is a fold of that log: a reader replays the stream in mint order to render what happened, and a consumer group lets the notification side resume after a restart without re-sending what it already delivered. Retention trims the live log under EchoMQ.StreamRetention, and EchoStore.StreamArchive folds older segments to the durable floor, so the deep feed survives without holding memory.

The durable floor

A trimmed stream segment, or a stream archived for deep history, reaches the durable tier — EchoStore.StreamArchive folding into the Graft engine and out to Tigris, the durability dial taught end to end.

Notes on Valkey. A stream key carries the queue name inside the braces (emq:{q}:stream:<name>), so every key of one queue hashes to one of the 16384 cluster slots and a multi-key script stays legal — no CROSSSLOT. valkey.io/topics/cluster-spec
