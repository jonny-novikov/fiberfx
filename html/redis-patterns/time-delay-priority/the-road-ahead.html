The arc — one score reading at a time

R3 built a queue that is correct under a crash: a job moves from emq:{queue}:wait into emq:{queue}:active and on to completion, recoverable rather than lost. R4 keeps that floor and adds two senses to it — when a job runs, and in what order — from one structure, the sorted set, read two ways. The member is always the job id; only the score changes meaning. The granular ladder takes that score apart one reading at a time, and each module is grounded in one real EchoMQ surface.

R4.01 · The delayed queue scores a job by its fire-time and sweeps the due head by score. EchoMQ scores the :delayed ZSET as (timestamp + delay) × 0x1000, and promoteDelayedJobs runs a ZRANGEBYSCORE of the due head followed by ZREM to the wait list. The queue learns when.

R4.02 · Schedulers & repeatable jobs registers recurring jobs by cron or interval. EchoMQ keeps the :repeat ZSET, upserted with ZADD nextMillis customKey behind a ZSCORE check, so a reboot re-registers the schedule rather than duplicating it.

R4.03 · Priority with composite scores packs a tier and an arrival counter into one number. getPriorityScore computes priority × 0x100000000 + (INCR pc) % 0x100000000 — the tier in the high 32 bits, a monotonic arrival counter in the low 32, so jobs in a tier stay FIFO. The queue learns in what order.

R4.04 · Backoff & retry reschedules a failed job with exponential backoff and jitter. EchoMQ.Backoff.calculate/4 owns the delay (base × 2^(n-1), then jitter), and retryJob-11.lua re-adds the job onto the :delayed set with that delay.

R4.05 · Leaderboards turns the same ZSET machinery into real-time rankings. This module grounds in Portal progress rankings rather than EchoMQ, using the same ZADD / ZRANK / ZREVRANGE commands the rest of the chapter teaches.

R4.06 · Workshop puts the whole chapter to work: scheduling Portal's notification and digest jobs over the :delayed and :repeat sets on one real surface.

Read the arc as one sorted set gaining one reading at a time: a fire-time, a recurring registry, a composite priority, a backoff reschedule, a ranking. The structure is the constant; the score is the chapter.

The door beyond — the living EchoMQ course

R4 teaches the transferable sorted-set patterns. The engine that runs them — the scheduler subsystem — is the subject of the dedicated EchoMQ course at /echomq. It is a living, agile-spec course, built in two movements. Movement I (E1–E2) teaches the as-built core library: the immutable wire protocol and data layer, then the lifecycle, components, and three runtimes. Movement II (E3–E8) is a living spine that tracks the EMQ extension ladder — emq.1 through emq.6 — rung by rung.

The patterns prove each score reading with one real excerpt and stop there. The scheduler subsystem owns the depth beyond the patterns: cron schedules across a timezone and a daylight-saving boundary; the busy-slot guard that holds a recurring job until its previous run finishes; the migration of legacy repeat keys onto the current registry; and parent-child flow scheduling, where a delayed or prioritized job gates a child flow. Pick a depth topic on the right to see which EchoMQ chapter owns it and which R4 pattern it extends.

  R4.02 schedulers     → E6   # timezone & DST, the busy-slot guard, legacy migration
  R4.03 priority       → E4   # intra-group priority & parent-child flow scheduling
  the scheduler depth  → E6 / E4 # lifecycle controls & groups own the engine

The course-level map pairs R4/R5 with E6/E7, but E7 is EchoStore, the near-cache — unrelated to time, delay, or priority. The honest, content-grounded doors are E6 · Lifecycle controls (the delay / promote / retry / TTL scheduler depth) and E4 · Groups (intra-group priority and the control plane). Pick a topic on the right to read what waits behind each door.

A note on honesty. The EchoMQ course is designed — its spec system and TOC are authored — and the EMQ ladder is drafted (the emq.1–emq.6 specs exist). The served /echomq pages ship as the build lands. It is the living companion course, not a course produced all at once.

A door, not a depth

This is where the patterns hand off. Redis Patterns Applied has shown the sorted-set techniques — the fire-time score, the composite priority, the recurring registry, the backoff reschedule — and grounded each in how EchoMQ-in-Portal really uses it, one excerpt at a time. What it has deliberately not done is teach the scheduler subsystem in full: cron arithmetic across timezones and DST, the busy-slot guard, the legacy-repeatable migration, and parent-child flow scheduling. That depth is a course of its own.
