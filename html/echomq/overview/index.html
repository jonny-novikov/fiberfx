Overview

One system,
three pillars

Start here: what EchoMQ is, the one wire it owns, and the three surfaces that fall out of it.

EchoMQ is one Valkey-native job system you own end to end. Its keys and its Lua scripts are the protocol; draw the line there and three surfaces fall out of one wire — the Queue that distributes work, the Bus that broadcasts signals and keeps a replayable log, and the Cache that serves reads. This chapter sets the frame: the three pillars, the protocol below the line that makes them polyglot and coherent, the door from Redis Patterns Applied, and what EchoMQ means for the Branded Component System it is the bus of. Three short dives carry it.

The protocol below the line

What makes one wire serve three surfaces, in any language? A line. EchoMQ records its design as a stack: the bottom layers are the protocol — fixed and shared by everything that speaks it; the top layers are the runtime's own. L0 is Valkey itself. L1 is the data layer: which key holds which structure, and the field names. L2 is the set of atomic Lua scripts that move a record between states. L3 is the script executor — load-once, run-by-SHA. L4 is the language API a caller invokes. Pick a layer to read its scope and whether it is shared.

The shared line falls between L2 and L3: below it the protocol is fixed and shared; above it, every runtime writes its own code. That single placement is what lets one wire serve three pillars — and what lets any runtime speak it.
