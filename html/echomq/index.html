EchoMQ In Depth

One Valkey, one wire you own — a queue, a bus, and a cache, from a single protocol.

EchoMQ is one Valkey-native job system you own end to end. Below the language line sit the keys and the Lua scripts — the protocol; above it stand the three surfaces you reach for every day: the Queue that distributes work, the Bus that broadcasts signals and keeps a replayable event log, and the Cache that serves reads.
This course teaches their internals in depth, and Elixir-canonical — the real keys, the real Lua, and the real module functions, lifted onto the page and annotated — and what they mean for every system in the BCS family that rides them. Because the protocol lives below the language line, any runtime that speaks the keys and the scripts is a peer, so EchoMQ is supported in and ported to others. It is the course on the far side of every “→ EchoMQ” door in Redis Patterns Applied.

One system, three pillars

EchoMQ is not a library you bolt onto an app — it is a protocol you own. The keys and the Lua scripts that run against Valkey are the system; everything a caller touches sits above them. Draw the line where the agreement lives: below it, the keys and the atomic scripts, fixed and shared; above it, the language you happen to write in. Push the agreement below that line and three surfaces fall out of one wire — the Queue (distribute work: enqueue, claim, retry, complete), the Bus (broadcast signals: pub/sub events and a retained, replayable log), and the Cache (serve reads: an L1 ETS / L2 Valkey near-cache kept coherent on the Bus).

Why Valkey. EchoMQ is backed by Valkey — the BSD-licensed, foundation-governed fork with Redis-semantics scripting, AOF durability, and hash-field TTL. It is the neutral substrate of record, the store every durability and coherence claim is phrased against, and it outlives any one vendor's licensing mood.

One protocol below the line; the Queue, the Bus, and the Cache above it — open the chapter you need.

Canonical in Elixir, polyglot by design

This course teaches EchoMQ through its canonical Elixir implementation — the real modules and the real Lua, lifted onto the page and annotated. That is where the depth is. But the depth is portable: because the protocol is the keys and the scripts, not the library, any runtime that speaks them is a peer. EchoMQ is supported in and ported to other runtimes; what you learn here — the keyspace, the record, the scripts, the lifecycle — is the same wire they speak. The pillars are taught once, in Elixir, and they hold wherever the protocol runs.

Learn the wire once, in Elixir; it is the same wire every peer runtime speaks.

Why the BCS architecture builds on EchoMQ

EchoMQ is not a library bolted onto an app — it is the queue, the bus, and the cache of the Branded Component System. The BCS course teaches the architecture: the 14-byte branded snowflake, the grammar-total keyspace law, fair lanes, and a coherent cache-aside near-cache. EchoMQ is that architecture, built — the bus (jobs, lanes, the consumer) and the cache (the L1/L2 near-cache, kept coherent on the bus) are the same modules this course opens up.

So a system built on the BCS architecture inherits its queue, its cache, and its coherence from EchoMQ for free. The worked example is codemojex, the BCS worked project: an emoji-guessing game whose scoring jobs, fair-lane guess claims, and round cache all ride EchoMQ. Read this course to learn the bus; read the BCS course to see the architecture it completes.
