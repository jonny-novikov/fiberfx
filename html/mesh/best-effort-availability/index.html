M2 · Best Effort Availability

Consistency first.

The first strategy on the menu: guarantee correctness regardless of the network, and provide availability as best the network allows — the right choice where partitions are rare and a wrong answer is worse than a late one.

The classic example is a coordination service kept consistent by a replicated-state-machine vote (Burrows' Chubby). EchoMesh takes the same consistency-first stance on its order book and regulated ledger but reaches consistency a different way — a single owning writer per instrument rather than a quorum, so the common case pays no round-trip — and lets the ledger block rather than diverge when it cannot reach its store. The strategy and its example are settled (Gilbert & Lynch 2012); the proof these surfaces answer to is M1, and the heart of the course, segmentation, is M5.

Proposed · not shipped

EchoMesh is a forward concept this course introduces; its pieces are real and shipped, and their composition into the mesh taught here is the proposed design.

§1

The strategy

mesh.2.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012 · Burrows 2006

Best-effort availability guarantees consistency no matter what the network does and optimizes availability for the common case — the right stance inside a single data center, where partitions are rare and the data must never diverge. Its classic example is Google's Chubby lock service: a cell of a few replicas that use a consensus protocol to elect a master, which handles reads and writes and replicates them to the others, so the service stays consistent and is available whenever it can maintain a master and a quorum.

Chubby serves coarse-grained locks deliberately, so that the occasional unavailability of the lock server does not stall its clients, and it names reliability and availability as its primary goals with throughput secondary. The lesson is that consistency-first is not a refusal to be available — it is a decision about which to give up when the two collide, and a system that picks consistency can still be available almost all the time.

§2

Consistency by ownership, not by vote

mesh.2.md · Thompson 2011 · Erlang Solutions

Chubby reaches consistency through a vote; EchoMesh reaches it through ownership. The single-writer principle holds that any item of data should be owned by one execution context for all its mutations, because the largest limit on scalability is multiple writers contending for the same item. EchoMesh applies this per instrument: one owning process is the sole writer of an instrument's book, so there is no contention to arbitrate and no agreement to reach.

The BEAM makes that owner a process with a single mailbox, addressable by message from anywhere in the cluster with the same semantics whether the caller is local or remote, so a single logical writer is a practical one at scale. The trade is named plainly: a single owner is a single point of unavailability for its instrument under partition, and one instrument's throughput is bounded by one process. EchoMesh answers those losses by giving each of many instruments its own owner, so the system scales horizontally, and by leaning on supervision and placement to restore an owner that fails.

The second dive, M2.2, builds the single writer and contrasts it with the quorum — one owning process per instrument, consistency by there being one writer rather than a vote, and the unreachable book that refuses under partition.

§3

The ledger that blocks rather than diverges

mesh.2.md · Elixir · Ecto.Multi

Settled money is the other consistency-first surface, and it is where blocking over diverging is sharpest. EchoMesh's regulated ledger runs on Postgres, with Ecto.Multi composing several writes — a debit, a credit, an entry — into one transaction that either commits as a whole or rolls back as a whole, returning a labelled result that names which step failed.

When the store cannot be reached or a step fails, nothing is committed; the surface refuses rather than recording half a transfer, which is the consistency-first choice made concrete — block, do not diverge.

The third dive, M2.3, builds the ledger and reads Ecto.Multi against the all-or-nothing guarantee the database provides beneath it — the line that blocks rather than diverges.
