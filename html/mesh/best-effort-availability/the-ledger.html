M2.3 · Best Effort Availability

The ledger on Ecto.Multi.

The other consistency-first surface, where blocking over diverging is sharpest. A ledger of settled money must never record half a transfer, so it composes a debit, a credit, and a journal entry into one all-or-nothing transaction — and when it cannot do the whole thing correctly, it does none of it.

No measurement here — this dive reads a mechanism, not a benchmark. Three primary sources carry it (Ecto.Multi, the PostgreSQL transaction-isolation documentation, and Gilbert & Lynch 2012): the composition is Ecto.Multi, the all-or-nothing guarantee is the database's, and the consistency-over-availability choice is the best-effort-availability strategy. The single writer that holds the other consistency-first surface is the previous dive, M2.2.

Proposed · not shipped

The mechanisms here are external and settled — Ecto.Multi over Postgres is shipped technology. That the regulated ledger is EchoMesh's consistency-first surface, built as one transaction per settlement that blocks rather than diverges, is the course's proposal; the shipped-versus-proposed line is drawn in full at M8.2.

§1

Settled money is consistency-first

mesh.2.3.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

A ledger records what has settled, and its correctness is not a matter of degree: a transfer is either fully recorded — the debit, the credit, and the entry all present — or it is not recorded at all. A half-recorded transfer, where money left one account but never reached another, is a worse outcome than a transfer that was refused and can be retried, because the refused one leaves the ledger correct and the half-recorded one leaves it wrong.

So the ledger is a best-effort-availability surface in the precise sense of the strategy: it guarantees correctness regardless of the network and gives up availability when it must, choosing consistency over availability whenever the two collide. The proof in M1.2 is what says the collision is real and unavoidable under a partition; the ledger's job is to make the safe side of it the one it lands on — refusing rather than recording something wrong.

§2

Ecto.Multi — one all-or-nothing transaction

mesh.2.3.md · Ecto.Multi

EchoMesh composes a settlement out of several operations — debit one account, credit another, write the journal entry — and runs them as one transaction with Ecto.Multi. A multi is built by naming each operation and chaining it onto the previous, then handed to Repo.transaction, which starts a database transaction, applies every operation, and commits the whole only if all of them succeed.

On success it returns the changes keyed by the names given to the steps; on any failure it rolls the whole transaction back and returns a labelled result naming the operation that failed, the value it failed with, and the changes made before it — so the caller learns not only that the settlement failed but exactly where. The naming is what makes a multi-step settlement legible: a failure is attributed to its step rather than to the transaction as a whole, and nothing partial survives. Ecto relies on the database's transaction for this guarantee rather than reimplementing it, which ties the surface's all-or-nothing behaviour to the store beneath it.

The shape to keep: a settlement is one named, chained Ecto.Multi handed to Repo.transaction. Success returns the changes keyed by step; failure rolls the whole back and names the step — nothing partial survives either way.

§3

What the database guarantees beneath it

mesh.2.3.md · PostgreSQL transaction isolation

The guarantee Ecto.Multi leans on is the database's atomicity: every operation inside a transaction completes, or none does — a commit makes the whole set permanent, and a rollback cancels all of it, leaving the database unchanged. This is block-over-diverge at the storage layer.

When the store cannot be reached, or a constraint is violated, or any step errors, the transaction rolls back and the ledger is exactly as it was before the attempt; there is no state in which the debit landed and the credit did not, because the database does not expose one. The surface's refusal to diverge is therefore not something EchoMesh enforces in application code and hopes holds — it is the atomicity the database provides, surfaced through one transaction per settlement.

§4

Serializable isolation — abort rather than anomaly

mesh.2.3.md · PostgreSQL transaction isolation

Atomicity handles a single transaction; concurrency is handled by isolation, and the strict end of it is where refusing over diverging shows again. PostgreSQL's serializable isolation emulates running transactions one at a time: it monitors for the read/write dependencies that could let a concurrent set behave in a way no serial order would, and when it detects such a condition it does not permit the anomaly — it aborts one of the transactions with a serialization failure, which the application is expected to catch and retry from the beginning.

So under contention the ledger does not produce an inconsistent interleaving; one transaction commits and the other is told to start over, and the database stays consistent throughout. The cost is named beside the guarantee: serializable carries monitoring overhead and forces retries that a looser isolation level would avoid, so it is the right choice where correctness outranks throughput — which is exactly the ledger's priority, and the same priority Chubby names when it puts reliability ahead of throughput in M2.1.

§5

The line that blocks rather than diverges

mesh.2.3.md · the consistency-first contract

Put together, the ledger draws one clear line: when it cannot do the whole settlement correctly, it does none of it. A store it cannot reach, a step that fails, a conflict it cannot serialize — each ends the same way, with the transaction rolled back and the ledger unchanged, available again the moment the obstacle clears. It blocks; it does not diverge.

This is the consistency-first contract made concrete on the surface where it matters most, and it is the deliberate opposite of the availability-first periphery, which answers from local state during a partition and logs enough to reconcile on heal rather than refusing — the choice the next chapter, M3, builds. The two surfaces are not in disagreement about the theorem; they have made opposite, deliberate choices about which side of it to land on.

Consistency-first · the ledger

Block when it cannot be correct

When the store is unreachable or a step fails, nothing commits and the ledger is unchanged. The settlement is refused and can be retried; the surface is unavailable for the moment, never wrong.

under failure: roll back, refuse, retry · never: half a transfer

Availability-first · the periphery

Answer, then reconcile on heal

The opposite choice EchoMesh's periphery makes: answer from local state during a partition and log enough to reconcile once the network heals, rather than refusing. The choice the next chapter, M3, builds.

under partition: answer locally, reconcile later · builds at: M3

Who may write. The single writer of M2.2 gives an instrument one ordered history cheaply, with no quorum, because there is one author. Correctness comes from ownership rather than a vote — there is one history because there is one writer.
What counts as a complete write. The atomic transaction gives a settlement all-or-nothing finality, with no partial state, because the database commits or rolls back as a unit. Several writes must stand or fall together; the multi is how they do.
The two mechanisms of the consistency-first side. Together they are how EchoMesh keeps the surfaces that must be correct correct — by ownership where a single history suffices, and by transaction where several writes must stand or fall together.

Hover or focus a panel to read it; click to pin. Without scripting, all three read in sequence. One answers who may write; the other answers what counts as a complete write.

Real and proposed, candidly: the mechanisms are external and settled — the atomic transaction is Ecto.Multi, the all-or-nothing commit and serializable abort-over-anomaly are PostgreSQL's, and the consistency-over-availability choice is the best-effort-availability strategy. EchoMesh's proposal is the use: a regulated ledger built as one transaction per settlement that blocks rather than diverges, sitting beside the single-writer book as the consistency-first side of the mesh — separated from the proposed composition at M8.2.

§6

What this dive reads, and does not

mesh.2.3.md · boundaries

This dive reads the transaction mechanism; it does not specify the ledger's schema or its reconciliation with upstream surfaces. It treats serializable isolation as the strict option without claiming every ledger operation runs at that level; the isolation a given settlement needs is a design choice the schema chapter settles. The identity that names an entry across surfaces is summarized, not specified — the specification is the /bcs course — and the availability-first alternative to blocking is M3.
