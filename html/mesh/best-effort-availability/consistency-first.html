M2.1 · Best Effort Availability

Consistency first.

The first strategy, read closely. Best-effort availability makes one promise without qualification — the system is consistent no matter what the network does — and treats availability as the thing to optimize rather than to guarantee. It is the stance for data where partitions are rare and a divergent answer is worse than a delayed one.

No measurement here: this dive reads a strategy and an example, not a benchmark. Two primary sources carry it (Gilbert & Lynch 2012, Burrows 2006) — the catalogue that names the strategy and gives its example, and the Chubby lock service, that example's canonical instance. The strategy and the system are external and settled; where EchoMesh places its surfaces is named as design. The single writer that replaces the vote is the next dive, M2.2.

Proposed · not shipped

The strategy and the example are settled external results. §1

The strategy stated

mesh.2.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

Best-effort availability makes one commitment without qualification — the system is consistent no matter what the network does — and treats availability as the thing to optimize rather than to guarantee. It fits where two conditions hold: partitions are rare, so the cost of being unavailable during one is paid seldom, and the data must never diverge, so a stale or conflicting answer is not an acceptable substitute for a slow one. Inside a single data center both conditions usually hold, which is why this is the stance for data that must be correct above all.

Read against the theorem, the strategy is the explicit choice of consistency over availability when a partition forces the two apart: under a partition the surface refuses rather than answers, because answering risks being wrong, and the proof in M1.2 is what says there is no third option. The strategy does not pretend the refusal never happens; it arranges for it to happen rarely and to be the safe outcome when it does. The strategy is named here; the proof it answers to is the dive linked from the doors below.

The unqualified promise. The system is consistent regardless of the network — the data never diverges. Correctness is the thing held fixed; nothing the network does is allowed to produce a wrong answer.
Availability is optimized, not guaranteed. Availability is treated as the quantity to make as high as the network allows, not as a property promised in every execution. The aim is to be available almost all the time, not all of the time.
Two conditions for the fit. Partitions are rare, so the seldom-paid unavailability is affordable; and the data must never diverge, so a delayed answer is preferable to a divergent one. Inside a single data center both usually hold.
The forced choice resolved. When a partition forces consistency and availability apart, the surface chooses consistency: it refuses rather than answers, because answering from the wrong side risks being wrong. The refusal is the safe outcome, arranged to be rare.

§2

The classic example — a coordination service

mesh.2.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012 · Burrows 2006

The example Gilbert and Lynch give is a coordination and lock service held consistent by a replicated-state-machine protocol, and Chubby is its canonical realization. A Chubby cell is a small set of replicas — typically five — that run a consensus protocol to elect a master; the master handles every read and write and replicates the result to the other replicas, so all of them agree on a single ordered history. Clients find the replicas through DNS and are forwarded to the current master.

The service stays available as long as it can maintain a master and a quorum of replicas behind it; when it cannot — a partition that isolates the master, or the loss of too many replicas — it stops serving rather than risk divergence. Two design choices keep that refusal cheap. Chubby serves coarse-grained locks, held for hours or days rather than seconds, so lock-acquisition is rare relative to client work and the brief unavailability of the lock server does not stall its callers; and the paper is candid that reliability and availability are the primary goals, with throughput and storage secondary. The service is consistency-first, and it is available almost all the time — the two are not in tension except when the network forces the choice.

§3

What “fast in the common case” buys, and costs

mesh.2.1.md · Burrows 2006

The shape of the strategy is a fast common path guarded by an expensive rare one. While a stable master is present, requests take the direct path and the consensus machinery is idle; the vote matters only at election time and when the network misbehaves. That is what lets a consistency-first service feel responsive most of the time.

The cost is the one the proof's corollary names: keeping replicas in agreement requires coordination, and coordination is a price paid on the common, healthy network as well as in the rare partition, because the master must replicate to a quorum before it can be sure a write will survive. So consistency-first is not free even when nothing breaks — it carries a standing coordination cost, examined as the everyday price of the trade in M1.2. The strategy is worth it precisely where correctness is non-negotiable and the coordination cost is affordable, which is the case for the surfaces EchoMesh assigns to it.

What EchoMesh carries from the cost: the everyday price of strong consistency is the coordination round-trip on the healthy network, not only the rare partition. That standing cost is the reason EchoMesh's consistency-first surfaces are built to be correct cheaply rather than by waiting — the mechanism M2.2 takes up next.

§4

Where EchoMesh stands

mesh.2.1.md · proposed surfaces

EchoMesh's order book and regulated ledger are best-effort-availability surfaces: correct always, available almost always, refusing rather than diverging when the network forces the choice. Where EchoMesh departs from the example is the mechanism for reaching consistency. Chubby keeps a single ordered history by a replicated vote — a quorum of replicas agreeing under Paxos — and pays a coordination round-trip to do it.

EchoMesh keeps a single ordered history per instrument by ownership instead: one process owns the instrument and is its only writer, so there is one history because there is one writer, with no quorum to assemble and no per-request vote to pay. The two stances answer the theorem the same way — consistency over availability under partition — but reach consistency by different means, and the difference is the subject of the next dive. The mechanism EchoMesh uses is built and contrasted with the quorum in M2.2.

Set the two mechanisms side by side: both produce one ordered history, but one pays a vote per request and the other has no vote to pay because there is only one writer.

The example · Chubby

One history by a replicated vote

A consensus-elected master replicates each write to a quorum of about five replicas under Paxos. The history is single because the replicas agree on one order.

cost: a coordination round-trip per write, paid on the healthy network too

EchoMesh · proposed

One history by a single owning writer

One process owns the instrument and is its only writer. The history is single because there is one writer for it — no quorum to assemble, no per-request vote.

cost: none per request · the failure and recovery of that writer is built in M2.2

§5

Real and proposed, candidly

mesh.2.1.md · shipped vs proposed

The strategy and the example are external and settled — best-effort availability is Gilbert and Lynch's catalogue entry, and Chubby is Burrows' lock service, consistent by a Paxos-backed master and available when it can hold a quorum. EchoMesh's placement of its book and ledger on this side of the trade is design, and its choice to reach consistency by a single owning writer rather than a quorum is the proposal this chapter develops; both run on shipped technology and are separated from the proposed composition at M8.2.

This dive reads the strategy and one example; it does not build EchoMesh's surfaces. It treats Chubby as the canonical instance of the example, not as the only one — the replicated-state-machine pattern appears in many coordination services. The single writer is M2.2 and the ledger is M2.3; the standing coordination cost named here is examined as the everyday price of consistency in M1.2.
