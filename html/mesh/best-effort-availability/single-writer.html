M2.2 · Best Effort Availability

The single writer, not the quorum.

How EchoMesh reaches consistency without the vote most consistency-first systems pay for. One owning process per instrument removes contention rather than coordinating it: the book has a single author, and therefore a single history, with no quorum to assemble and no agreement to reach.

No measurement here — this dive is a design argument. Four primary sources carry it (Thompson 2011, Erlang Solutions on the BEAM, Fischer–Lynch–Paterson 1985, Burrows 2006 on Chubby). The single-writer principle and the BEAM process model are settled and shipped; one owning process per instrument is the course's proposal. The proof this choice answers to is the previous chapter, M1.2; the availability-first counterpart is the next chapter, M3.

Proposed · not shipped

The single-writer principle, the BEAM, the consensus impossibility, and Chubby's quorum are settled external results. EchoMesh's proposal is the application — one owning process per instrument as the consistency-first mechanism, reached over the branded identity, restored by supervision and placement rather than a failover vote; the substrate-versus-proposal line is drawn in full at M8.2.

§1

The single-writer principle

mesh.2.2.md · Thompson 2011

The principle is narrow and consequential: for any item of data, a single execution context should own all of its mutations. Its motivation is that the chief limit on a scalable system is multiple writers contending for the same item, and the standard ways to manage that contention — mutual exclusion through a lock, or an optimistic compare-and-swap that retries on conflict — are both overhead incurred only because more than one writer exists.

Remove the second writer and the overhead has nothing to do: there is no lock to arbitrate and no retry to lose, because there is no contention. The principle does not make writes faster by clever coordination; it removes the coordination by removing the contention, which is a different and sturdier kind of win. EchoMesh takes the principle as the design rule for its consistency-first surfaces.

§2

One owner per instrument

mesh.2.2.md · proposed application

EchoMesh's unit of ownership is the instrument. One process owns an instrument's book and is its only writer, so every change to that book passes through one execution context, and the book has a single ordered history because it has a single author. Readers do not contend with that writer for the right to mutate, because they do not mutate; they observe.

The instrument's branded identity is what routes a request to the one process that owns it — the identity places the owner on a consistent-hashing ring so that the same instrument resolves to the same owner from anywhere — and the identity contract itself is the /bcs course, with the placement that uses it built at M7. The result is that consistency on a book is not something EchoMesh computes and defends; it is a property of there being one writer, which is the cheapest correctness there is.

§3

The BEAM makes the owner real

mesh.2.2.md · Erlang Solutions

A single logical writer is only useful if it can be a single practical writer at scale, and the BEAM is what makes that hold. A process on the BEAM owns its state and is reached only by sending it a message; it handles its messages one at a time, so its state is mutated serially without any lock.

Crucially, that addressing is location-transparent: a process is reached by the same message-send whether the caller runs on the same machine or a different node of the cluster, so an instrument's owner can sit anywhere in the fleet and still be the one writer every caller reaches. When an owning process fails, the runtime's supervision restarts it, and placement re-establishes which node holds it — so the single writer survives crashes not by a failover vote but by being restarted and re-placed.

This is the substrate that lets ownership scale: many instruments mean many owners, spread across the cluster, each the sole writer of its own book. The runtime, the umbrella where the identity library lives, and the deployment chapter are the /elixir course.

§4

Why removing the vote matters

mesh.2.2.md · Fischer–Lynch–Paterson 1985

The reason ownership is worth choosing over a quorum is the consensus impossibility. A quorum protocol is a way of solving consensus, and consensus cannot be guaranteed to make progress in an asynchronous system where even one process may crash; what real quorum systems do is rely on partial synchrony, failure detectors, or randomization to make progress in practice, and pay a coordination round-trip to a majority on the common path.

A single owner has no consensus to run, because there is nothing to agree on — one writer is the whole authority over its instrument — so it sidesteps the impossibility's progress problem rather than working around it, and it pays no per-write quorum round-trip in the common case. The win is not that EchoMesh solves consensus more cleverly; it is that on these surfaces it has no consensus to solve.

The proof that there is no third option, that there is no way to be both correct and available under a partition, is the previous chapter, M1.2; this dive is the consistency-first answer that proof forces. The single-writer claim is asserted here against the impossibility and built on the BEAM above, carried under a Proposed · not shipped note until the substrate-versus-proposal line is drawn at M8.2.

§5

The contrast with Chubby, and the loss

mesh.2.2.md · Burrows 2006

Chubby and EchoMesh make the same CAP choice and reach it by opposite mechanisms. Chubby keeps a single ordered history by a quorum: a few replicas elect a master under a consensus protocol, and the master replicates each write to a majority before it counts. EchoMesh keeps a single ordered history by ownership: one process is the only writer, and there is no replication to a quorum on the write path.

The difference is where the coordination lives — Chubby pays it per write to keep replicas in agreement; EchoMesh pays none in the common case because there is only one writer to be in agreement with. The loss is the mirror image of the win and must be named: Chubby's quorum survives the master's failure by electing a new master from replicas that already hold the state, whereas a single owner is a single point of unavailability for its instrument while it is unreachable, and one instrument's write throughput is bounded by one process.

EchoMesh answers the first with supervision and placement that restart and re-home a failed owner, and the second by spreading many instruments across many owners so the system scales horizontally even though each instrument does not — but it does not pretend a single owner has a quorum's built-in failover.

The win. No per-write coordination round-trip in the common case, because there is only one writer to be in agreement with. Correctness is a property of there being one author, not something computed and defended — the cheapest correctness there is.
The loss · availability. A single owner is a single point of unavailability for its instrument while it is unreachable. Chubby's quorum, by contrast, survives the master's failure by electing a new master from replicas that already hold the state — built-in failover the single owner does not have.
The loss · throughput. One instrument's write throughput is bounded by one process. A single owner does not scale a single instrument; it scales the system, by there being many instruments and therefore many owners.
The answers, candidly. The unavailability is answered by supervision and placement that restart and re-home a failed owner (M6, M7); the throughput bound is answered by spreading many instruments across many owners, so the system scales horizontally. EchoMesh does not pretend a single owner has a quorum's built-in failover.

Hover or focus a panel to read it; click to pin. Without scripting, all four read in sequence. The point is candour: the loss is named, not hidden.

Under a partition the book refuses. When a partition makes an owner unreachable, EchoMesh does not serve the book from a stale replica on the other side; the instrument is unavailable until its owner is reachable or re-placed — consistency over availability, refusing rather than diverging, the same choice Chubby makes when it cannot hold a quorum. The availability-first surfaces that make the opposite choice, answering from local state and bounding staleness, are the next chapter, M3.
