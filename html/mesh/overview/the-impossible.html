M0.1 · Overview

The impossible result,
read precisely.

The CAP theorem is not a slogan about picking two of three. It is one case of a deeper impossibility — you cannot guarantee both safety and liveness in an unreliable system — with a harder sibling in the consensus literature, and a cost that lands even when the network is healthy. This dive states the theorem at orientation depth, so the menu of responses and the mesh that follow both have a wall to stand against.

A dive of M0 · Overview, the orientation chapter of EchoMesh, In Depth. No measurement here: this is a conceptual orientation, resting on four primary sources. The line-by-line proof is M1.2; here is the shape of it.

Proposed · not shipped

EchoMesh's answer to this wall — a single owning writer on its consistency-first surfaces — is a forward concept this course introduces, deferred to M2; its pieces are real and shipped, and their composition into the mesh is the proposed design.

§1

The register that cannot be both

mesh.0.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2002

The 2002 result fixes the smallest interesting case. A service is a set of servers, p1 through pn, with clients that read and write a single emulated register. Partition the servers into the set holding p1 and the set holding the rest, then send a read to p2 while every message from p1 is lost. The proof shows p2 cannot distinguish a world in which p1 acknowledged a write of one value from a world in which it acknowledged a different value; no matter how long it waits, it must either return a response that risks being wrong or never return at all.

So in a network that can drop messages, no service implements an always-responding atomic register that stays correct. That is the wall, stated once: under a partition, a correct shared register must sometimes refuse to answer. The figure above walks the two indistinguishable worlds, the cut, and the forced choice.

§2

Safety and liveness — the frame that generalizes

mesh.0.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

The 2012 paper sets that wall inside a larger one. Consistency is a safety property: it says nothing bad ever happens — every response is correct with respect to the writes before it. Availability is a liveness property: it says something good eventually happens — every request eventually receives a response. The CAP theorem is then one instance of the general fact that you cannot guarantee both safety and liveness in an unreliable system, and seeing it that way is what makes the result transfer to settings far from a web register.

The sharper edge follows from asynchrony: a server has no bound on message delay, so it cannot tell a partition from a slow link, and therefore cannot promise to be correct only during partitions and fast the rest of the time. The bad case is latent in every execution, even one in which no message is ever lost. And still, working systems run the world: practitioners, in Gilbert and Lynch's phrase, must “do the impossible” — the subject of the next dive. Hover the framings below to read each in turn.

Consistency is a safety property. A safety property says nothing bad ever happens. Consistency is one, because every response must be correct with respect to the writes that preceded it. Under a partition, holding it means a surface that cannot confirm it is current stops answering rather than risk an incorrect reply.
Availability is a liveness property. A liveness property says something good eventually happens. Availability is one, because every request must eventually receive a response. Under a partition, holding it means a surface answers from what it has rather than wait for confirmation — it may serve a stale value, but it never stops.
Asynchrony makes the bad case latent. A server has no bound on message delay, so it cannot tell a partition from a slow link. It therefore cannot promise to be correct only when the network misbehaves and fast the rest of the time — the impossible case is present in every execution, even one in which no message is ever lost.
CAP is one instance of a general impossibility. Naming the two halves for what they are — safety and liveness — is what lets the result transfer past a web register: you cannot guarantee both in any unreliable system. Practitioners cope rather than escape; in Gilbert and Lynch's phrase, they must “do the impossible.”

§3

The harder sibling — consensus

mesh.0.1.md · Fischer–Lynch–Paterson 1985

The safety-versus-liveness wall has a steeper face. In 1985 Fischer, Lynch, and Paterson studied consensus — every process starts with a value, and all must agree on one — under three requirements: agreement and validity, which are safety, and termination, which is liveness. Their result is that in an asynchronous system where even one process may crash, no deterministic protocol guarantees all three; for any purported protocol there is an execution, with no actual failure, in which it never terminates.

Agreeing is strictly harder than reading a register, so the CAP wall implies the consensus wall: you cannot reach agreement under partitions either. This is why reaching for a quorum protocol does not escape the trade — it relocates it. EchoMesh's answer on its consistency-first surfaces is to avoid the vote entirely with a single owning writer, which M2 builds.

Reading a register

One value, one reader

The CAP case: a single emulated register, read at p2 under a partition. The reader must risk a wrong answer or never return. This is the wall §1 states.

Consensus asks every process to agree on one value, with agreement and validity as safety and termination as liveness. FLP: unsolvable by any deterministic protocol with a single crash.

§4

The cost on a healthy network

mesh.0.1.md · Abadi, PACELC

The theorem bites in the rare partition; a companion observation bites every day. Keeping replicas consistent forces them to coordinate, and coordination costs latency on every request — so a consistency-first design pays not only when the network breaks but on the common Tuesday. Abadi's PACELC states this as the else-clause CAP omits: if there is a partition, trade availability against consistency; else, trade latency against consistency.

The lesson EchoMesh takes from it is that the price of strong consistency is real and continuous, which is the reason a single writer — paying no per-request quorum round-trip — is the cheaper way to be correct than a vote.

§5

“Two of three” is the wrong reading

mesh.0.1.md · the per-subsystem question

The popular gloss invites a system to drop partition tolerance and keep the other two. That is not a choice on offer: partitions are a property of the network, not a dial in the design, and a long-enough delay is indistinguishable from one. The real decision is narrower and more useful — for each subsystem, what does it do during a partition, and how does it recover after.

Read that way, the theorem stops being a wall to scale and becomes a design question to answer surface by surface. The menu of answers is M0.2; the segmented architecture that assigns a different answer to each surface is M0.3, and in full M5.

The boundary of this dive. It sketches the result; it does not prove it line by line — that is M1.2. It works in the asynchronous model, where the impossibility is sharpest; real networks are partially synchronous, and the strategies in the next dive live in the gap between the two models. Weaker consistency conditions — sequential, causal, bounded-stale — are named here only as the territory the availability-first surfaces will occupy later.
