M0 · Overview

Engineers must still
do the impossible.

The CAP theorem proves a service cannot hold consistency and availability together under a partition. Yet distributed systems plainly work — practitioners cope with the theorem rather than defeat it. This overview states the result as the safety-versus-liveness impossibility it belongs to, names the four strategies that cope, and sets out EchoMesh's stance: segment the trade across an owned stack, and make the infrastructure beneath transparent.

The orientation chapter of EchoMesh, In Depth. It states the whole argument once, then opens into three dives. Every figure on the page is quoted from a cited source; the theorem is stated precisely, as a constraint to design within.

Proposed · not shipped

EchoMesh is a forward concept this course introduces; its pieces are real and shipped, and their composition into the mesh described here is the proposed design.

§1

The impossible result

mesh.0.md · Gilbert & Lynch

Eric Brewer's conjecture, proved by Gilbert and Lynch, is that in a network that can drop messages between servers, a service cannot implement a correct, always-responding shared register: under a partition it must give up consistency or availability. Their later perspective places the result where it belongs — as one instance of a deeper trade between safety and liveness in any unreliable system. This is the constraint every distributed design answers to, and EchoMesh does not pretend to escape it.

The PACELC principle sharpens the cost further: even on a healthy network, keeping replicas consistent forces coordination, and coordination costs latency on every request — so the trade is paid not only in the rare partition but on the common Tuesday. A design's job is to decide, deliberately, where to pay it.

The bleak reading would end the conversation, except that distributed systems plainly work. Practitioners build and deploy services that stay responsive under bad networks and stay correct where correctness is required, coping with the theorem rather than defeating it. The resolution is that a real system is not a single trade-off applied globally; it is many trade-offs, assigned subsystem by subsystem, so that each part does what it owes its users while the whole does something no single label describes. EchoMesh is the architecture built around that resolution, and this course is its study.

§2

The menu of defensible answers

mesh.0.md · the four strategies

Gilbert and Lynch catalogue four ways systems cope with CAP, and the catalogue is the spine of the course. Each maps to a surface of EchoMesh, and each is a chapter. Hover a strategy to read its trade, its canonical example, and the EchoMesh surface it becomes.

Best Effort Availability · consistency-first → M2. Guarantee consistency regardless of the network and optimize availability for the common case — the right choice inside one data center, where partitions are rare. The paper's example is a coordination and lock service kept consistent by a replicated-state-machine protocol, fast with a stable leader and unavailable only when the cluster splits. EchoMesh's matching book and regulated ledger are these surfaces: correct always, and the consistency comes from a single owning writer per instrument rather than a quorum, so the common case pays no vote.
Best Effort Consistency · availability-first → M3. Guarantee a response always and provide consistency as best it can — the right choice for content served to users worldwide, where a slightly stale answer is tolerable and a stopped one is not. The paper's example is a content delivery cache, and naming through caching, trading strong consistency for proximity and speed. EchoMesh's market-data feed and globally replicated retention are these surfaces: always answering, with staleness bounded rather than forbidden.
Trading Consistency for Availability · the dial → M4. Set the trade continuously, by how out of date data may be: recent good connectivity buys consistency cheaply, while a long partition forces a choice. The paper's example is a toolkit for continuous consistency in which an application tightens its requirement as the stakes rise — a reservation system that tolerates stale seat counts when the plane is empty and demands accuracy as it fills. EchoMesh exposes this as a staleness budget per surface.
Segmenting Consistency and Availability · the dominant strategy, the heart → M5. Split one system into subsystems, each with its own trade, so the whole guarantees neither strong consistency nor full availability while each part guarantees exactly what it should. The paper names five dimensions to segment along — data, operation, function, user, and hierarchy. EchoMesh is a segmented system by construction, and this is the heart of the course.

Hover or focus a strategy to switch; click to pin. Without scripting, all four read in sequence. The signature spectrum that plots them on the trade lives on the course landing.

Why the fourth is the spine. The first three strategies pick one trade for a whole service; the fourth admits that a real service has many requirements at once and segments accordingly. It is the strategy most large systems use in practice, and it is the one EchoMesh is built on. The reason it works without dissolving into chaos is identity: a segmented system coheres only if a single entity stays addressable as it crosses surfaces that have chosen different trades.

§3

The mesh — segment, own the stack, make it transparent

mesh.0.md · EchoMesh's stance

EchoMesh is a segmented system held whole by one identity, placed on an owned stack, on infrastructure that disappears into the design. Three claims carry the course.

Claim one

The branded identity is the seam

Segmentation coheres only if a single entity stays addressable across surfaces that chose different trades. The 14-byte branded id makes a fill matched on a consistency-first book the same entity in the cache that serves it, the stream that logs it, the object store that retains it, and the worker that prices it.

it names state rather than carrying it · one entity, many trades

Claim two

Segment the trade across an owned stack

Each surface sits where its trade belongs — the BEAM and a single writer for consistency, EchoCache and the EchoMQ bus and streams for availability, Tigris for globally replicated retention, Postgres with Ecto.Multi for the regulated ledger that blocks rather than diverges.

the whole guarantees neither · each part guarantees what it should

Claim three

The infrastructure becomes transparent

The BEAM and the FLAME pattern let one program treat its whole self as elastic — the same code on a laptop and across a fleet of subsecond-launch machines, with placement by the branded id over a consistent-hashing ring that computes the same owner on one node or a thousand.

scale is a property of the design · not a target to operate

The stack, placed on the trade. EchoMesh assigns its surfaces across an owned stack, each piece sitting where its trade belongs: the engine on Elixir and the BEAM, whose built-in distribution, supervision, and process model hold the coordinator, log, broker, and orchestrator as language properties; the bus and the per-instrument stream log on EchoMQ; the near-cache, the ring, and the journal on EchoCache; deep retention on Tigris, which writes close to a region and replicates close to requesters; the regulated ledger on Postgres, with Ecto.Multi composing several writes into one all-or-nothing transaction. The stack is M6, the transparent runtime is M7, and the synthesis is M8.
