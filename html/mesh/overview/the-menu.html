M0.2 · Overview

The menu of
defensible answers.

The result said a service cannot have both consistency and availability under a partition. This dive lays out what working systems do instead. Gilbert and Lynch catalogue four coping strategies — three that pick a single trade for a whole service and one that refuses to — and that catalogue is the spine of the course. Each maps to a surface of EchoMesh and earns its own chapter.

A dive of M0 · Overview, the orientation chapter of EchoMesh, In Depth. No measurement here: the menu is a set of design stances, not a benchmark. The full treatment of the dominant strategy is M5; this dive walks the menu so the choice each later chapter defends is legible in advance.

Proposed · not shipped

Each strategy below names the EchoMesh surface it becomes. Those placements are a forward concept this course introduces; the pieces are real and shipped, and their composition into the mesh is the proposed design.

§1

Reading the menu

mesh.0.2.md · Gilbert & Lynch

Begin with the two options the theorem seems to leave. Under a partition a correct register must either stop answering or risk a wrong answer, so the crude menu is “stay consistent, lose availability” or “stay available, lose consistency.” Read only that far, the result is a binary — one switch, thrown once, for the whole system.

The 2012 paper's contribution is to show that binary is too coarse for real design. A service can choose differently for different operations, vary the strength of its guarantee with conditions, and split itself so the question is asked many times rather than once. The four strategies are the named points on that wider field: the first two take the binary at face value and commit a whole service to one side; the third makes the commitment adjustable; the fourth makes it local.

§2

The three that pick a side

mesh.0.2.md · strategies one through three

The first three strategies share an assumption: one trade for the whole service. They differ in which trade, and in whether it is fixed in advance or set by conditions. Each has a canonical example from the paper, and each becomes a chapter and an EchoMesh surface.

Strategy one · M2

Consistency first

Guarantee consistency regardless of the network; optimize availability for the common case. The paper's example is a coordination and lock service on a replicated-state-machine protocol. EchoMesh's order book and ledger are these surfaces — a single owning writer, not a vote.

the cost PACELC names · correctness buys latency, every request

Strategy two · M3

Availability first

Answer always; provide consistency as best it can without ever blocking. The paper's example is a content-delivery cache and naming through caching. EchoMesh's market-data feed and globally replicated retention are these surfaces — staleness bounded, not denied.

the loss is explicit · bound the staleness, do not forbid it

Strategy three · M4

Trading the trade

Set the trade continuously, by how stale data may be: good connectivity buys consistency cheaply, a long partition forces a choice. The paper's example is a reservation system, loose while the cabin is empty, exact as it fills. EchoMesh exposes a per-surface staleness budget.

a dial, not a switch · the owner sets the number

One commitment apiece. Strategies one and two fix the dial in advance, at opposite ends; strategy three keeps a hand on it and turns it as conditions change. All three still answer the question once, for the whole service. The fourth strategy is the one that stops doing that.

§3

The continuous dial, read precisely

mesh.0.2.md · trading consistency for availability

The third strategy is worth a second look, because it is the bridge to the fourth. It does not pick a point on the consistency–availability line and stay there; it makes the point move. The lever is how out of date data is permitted to be. While connectivity has been good, a surface can stay consistent at little cost; as a partition lengthens, it spends a staleness allowance, and when that allowance runs out it must finally choose between answering stale and not answering at all.

The paper's example makes the lever concrete: a toolkit for continuous consistency in which an application tightens its requirement as the stakes rise — a reservation system tolerant of approximate seat counts while the cabin is empty and demanding of precision as it fills. EchoMesh carries the same idea as a per-surface staleness budget: a number a surface owner sets, rather than a property baked into the engine. That a single surface can hold a different budget from its neighbour is already a step toward segmentation.

§4

Segmenting — why the fourth is the spine

mesh.0.2.md · the dominant strategy, M5

The fourth strategy is different in kind. The first three each pick one trade for an entire service; the fourth admits that a real service holds many requirements at once and segments itself accordingly, so that the whole guarantees neither strong consistency nor full availability while each part guarantees exactly what it owes. It is the strategy most large systems use in practice, and the one EchoMesh is built on.

Gilbert and Lynch name five dimensions to segment along. Hover each to read what it splits and the shape it gives the system.

By data. Different records get different guarantees. The records that must never diverge are held consistency-first; the records where a stale read is tolerable are held availability-first. The split runs through the data itself, not the code path that touches it.
By operation. Reads and writes are typed apart and given different trades. A write that must be durable and ordered takes the consistency-first path; a read that can be served from what is on hand takes the available one, even against the same record.
By function. One service joins a consistent core to an available periphery. The core holds the state that must be correct; the periphery answers from caches and projections of it, staying responsive while the core stays right.
By user. Separated populations are served under separate trades. One class of caller is given strong guarantees and the latency they cost; another is given fast, eventually-consistent answers — the same service, two contracts.
By hierarchy. The split nests inside itself. A segment chosen by one dimension is itself segmented by another, so the design is a tree of trades rather than a single cut — which is how a real system reaches many guarantees at once.

Hover or focus a dimension to switch; click to pin. Without scripting, all five read in sequence.

Why the fourth is the spine. The first three strategies pick one trade for a whole service; the fourth makes the choice local, so a single design can keep an order book correct and a market-data feed responsive without contradiction. EchoMesh is segmented by construction, which is why segmentation is the heart of the course — M5. The reason it does not dissolve into chaos is identity: a segmented system coheres only if one entity stays addressable as it crosses surfaces that have chosen different trades, which is the subject of the next dive.
