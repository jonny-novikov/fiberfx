M1.1 · Doing the Impossible

Safety and liveness.

CAP is cleanest when its two halves are named for what they are. Consistency is a safety property — nothing bad ever happens — and availability is a liveness property — something good eventually does. The CAP trade is one instance of the general impossibility of guaranteeing both in an unreliable system.

This dive makes the two properties precise, shows why the asynchronous model — where a server cannot distinguish a partition from a slow link — is where the trade bites hardest, and places EchoMesh's surfaces on the two sides. The framing is settled theory (Gilbert & Lynch 2012, 2002); the proof that the trade is forced is the next dive, M1.2. Nothing here is benchmarked: it is a conceptual dive.

Proposed · not shipped

The safety/liveness framing is established theory and none of it is EchoMesh's invention; what this course proposes is the placement — book and ledger on the safety side, feed and retention on the liveness side. Those pieces are shipped; their composition into one mesh is the proposal.

§1

Nothing bad, and something good

mesh.1.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

The two words have exact meanings, and the exactness is what makes the trade legible. A safety property says a bad thing never happens; its defining feature is that any violation is witnessed by a finite prefix of an execution — there is a first moment at which the bad thing has occurred, and you can point to it. A liveness property says a good thing eventually happens; its defining feature is the opposite — no finite prefix can witness a violation, because at any finite moment the good thing might still be coming, and only an infinite execution in which it never arrives is a counterexample.

Consistency is of the first kind and availability of the second. The asymmetry matters: a safety violation is a concrete event you can catch, while a liveness violation is the absence of an event you can only ever be still waiting for — which is precisely the situation a partitioned server is in when it cannot tell whether a response is late or never coming.

§2

Consistency is a safety property

mesh.1.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2002

The consistency CAP refers to is atomic, or linearizable, behavior on a shared register: the system behaves as if there were a single copy of the data and every operation took effect at one instant between its call and its return, so every read returns the value of the most recent write in that single order. Read as safety, the bad thing it forbids is a read that returns a value inconsistent with the writes ordered before it — a stale or out-of-order answer presented as current.

The property is violated the moment such a response is emitted, and that moment is finite and identifiable, which is exactly why the 2002 proof can construct an execution and point to the offending read. The next dive, M1.2, walks that construction; here it is enough that the failure is a definite event with a first occurrence.

§3

Availability is a liveness property

mesh.1.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2002

The availability CAP refers to is the guarantee that every request received by a non-failing node eventually returns a response — not necessarily fast, but not never. Read as liveness, the good thing it promises is termination: the request does not hang forever. Its violation cannot be caught at any finite instant, because a response that has not yet arrived may still arrive; only the infinite execution in which it never does is the counterexample.

This is why availability cannot be salvaged by waiting longer: waiting is the failure mode, not the remedy. A surface that must always answer is committing to liveness, and it pays for that commitment by answering from whatever state it holds — which, under a partition, may be stale.

§4

EchoMesh on the two sides

mesh.1.1.md · proposed placement

Each surface commits to the property its users really need. The placement below is the course's proposal, not a shipped claim — the pieces run on shipped technology, and their assignment to the two sides is what M5 composes into one mesh.

Safety side — the bad thing must never happen

Order book & regulated ledger

A fill matched against state that has moved on, or a ledger entry that disagrees with the balance before it, is not a slow answer but a wrong one. The design forbids it rather than bounding it.

commits to: correctness with respect to prior writes · refuse rather than risk a stale answer

Liveness side — something good must always eventually happen

Market-data feed & replicated retention

A reader is better served a slightly stale quote now than a correct one withheld indefinitely. These surfaces answer always, and bound how stale the answer may be rather than risk never answering.

commits to: termination · answer always, bound the staleness rather than block

Why the split is the design: under a partition no single surface can hold both, so EchoMesh assigns each the property it actually needs and segments the trade across them — the architecture the heart of the course, M5, builds. The boundary between a safety-side and a liveness-side surface is held by the branded identity contract of /bcs.

§5

The asynchronous model, and why it bites

mesh.1.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2002 / 2012

The model the impossibility lives in is asynchronous message passing: nodes communicate only by messages, and there is no upper bound on how long a message may take to arrive, or whether it arrives at all. In that model a single hard fact does the damage — a node cannot distinguish a partition from a slow link. A message that has not come may have been dropped by a partition or merely delayed by congestion, and nothing the node can observe separates the two.

The consequence is that a service cannot promise to be correct only while partitioned and responsive otherwise, because it can never know it is partitioned; the bad case is latent in every execution, including ones in which no message is ultimately lost. This is the asymmetry of the two properties made operational: safety can be protected by refusing to answer until certain, but that refusal is itself the liveness violation; and liveness can be protected by answering from local state, but that answer is itself the safety risk. The model leaves no third move.

A slow link. The message was sent and will arrive — eventually. Congestion delayed it. There is no upper bound on the delay, so “eventually” carries no deadline the server can hold against.
A partition. The message was dropped and will never arrive. The link is severed. But severed and merely slow produce the same thing at the server: a message that has not come.
What the server observes: a message that has not come. Identical under both causes. Nothing the server can observe separates them, so it cannot act on which one is true — it must choose its property in advance, and the bad case is latent even when no message is ultimately lost.

Hover or focus a cause to read it; click to pin. Without scripting, all three read in sequence. The point is the third panel: the two causes are one observation.

§6

The general trade

mesh.1.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

Naming the two properties for their class is what lifts CAP out of the web-services setting it was born in. The impossibility of guaranteeing both safety and liveness in an unreliable system is general, and CAP is one case of it. The same shape recurs wherever a component must both never act wrongly and always eventually act: a lock service that must never grant two holders yet should always eventually grant one, a commit protocol that must never disagree yet should always eventually decide.

Seeing the trade at this altitude is what lets the rest of the course treat each EchoMesh surface as a fresh instance of one well-understood problem rather than a new puzzle, and it is why the harder consensus impossibility in the next dive is recognizably the same wall with a steeper face.
