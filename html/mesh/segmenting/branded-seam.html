M5.3 · Segmenting Consistency and Availability

The branded identity as the seam.

Five cuts leave one fill as five representations on surfaces with five different trades, and something has to keep them the same fill. That something is a single branded identity — minted once at the consistent core and carried verbatim to the available periphery — whose properties are exactly what a seam across differently-traded surfaces needs.

No measurement here: this dive reads a role, not a benchmark. Two primary sources carry it (Gilbert & Lynch's 2012 treatment of segmentation, which creates the need for a seam, and the Snowflake lineage of the branded identity, which supplies the properties the seam requires). The strategy the seam completes is M5.1; the dimensions it spans are M5.2. The identity contract itself is the /bcs course, pointed to here rather than restated.

Proposed · not shipped

The Snowflake-lineage identity — time-ordered, coordination-free, location-independent — is a settled external result, and the branded contract is the shipped /bcs course. EchoMesh's proposal is the use: one identity minted at the consistent core and carried to the available periphery, serving as the seam across five differently-traded surfaces. The shipped-versus-proposed line is drawn at M8.2.

§1

The problem segmentation leaves

mesh.5.3.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

Segmentation solves one problem and creates another in the same motion. Cutting the system so each surface takes its own trade is what makes each surface right, as M5.2 showed, but it means a single fill now exists as five things at once: a row in the consistent ledger, a value in the available cache, an entry in the ordered stream, an object in the global store, and a job in the worker tier.

If each surface held its own private notion of which fill it was dealing with, those five things would be disconnected, and the system could not reconcile them — it could not answer whether the ledger's fill and the stream's fill were the same event. A segmentation that fragments the entity is worse than no segmentation, because it trades a coherent slow system for a fast incoherent one. The parts must stay reconcilable into one entity, and the question is what makes them so.

§2

What a seam must be

mesh.5.3.md · the requirements

A seam is a name for the entity that holds across every surface, and the requirements it must meet follow directly from the surfaces' different trades. It must mean the same thing on every surface, so the name on the ledger and the name on the stream denote one fill. It must be mintable without coordination, so the available periphery never has to wait on a round-trip to name something — the property that would re-introduce exactly the coordination the availability-first surfaces exist to avoid.

It must be stable, never reassigned, so a name means one entity for all time. It must be unique, so no two fills ever collide on a name. It must be time-ordered, so that orderings taken on different surfaces — the stream's append order, the ledger's commit order — agree rather than conflict. And it must be location-independent, a self-contained value any surface can interpret without consulting a registry, so that no surface depends on a lookup that a partition could sever. A name with these properties can hold a segmented system together; a name without them cannot.

§3

The branded identity meets them — the lineage

mesh.5.3.md · Snowflake

The branded identity is built to meet exactly those requirements, and it meets them because of its lineage. It derives from the Snowflake design for distributed identifiers, in which an identifier is a time-ordered value composed of a timestamp, a generating-node identifier, and a per-node sequence, so that any node mints unique, sortable identifiers independently with no central authority and no coordination.

Those are the seam's requirements line for line: the timestamp in the high bits makes the identifier time-ordered, the node and sequence make it unique without coordination, and the value is self-contained, so it is location-independent by construction. The branding the /bcs course adds — a wider, fourteen-byte identifier carrying registered namespaces and a compact text encoding — sharpens the identifier for this system without changing what makes it a seam; the role the identity plays here is the time-ordered, coordination-free, location-independent name, and the byte layout that realizes it is described in /bcs, not repeated here.

Time-ordered. The timestamp lives in the high bits of the identifier, so ids sort by the moment they were minted. This is what makes the stream's append order and the ledger's commit order agree rather than conflict — orderings taken on different surfaces concur because they are orderings of the same time-stamped names.
Generated without coordination. The node identifier and per-node sequence let any node mint a unique id independently — no central authority, no round-trip. This is what lets the consistent core mint the name and the available periphery carry it without waiting, never re-introducing the coordination the availability-first surfaces exist to avoid.
Location-independent. The id is a self-contained value — every surface can interpret which entity it names with no registry lookup. This is what lets a surface depend on nothing a partition could sever: the object store keys on it, the worker references it, and none of them consult a central table to learn what fill the name denotes.

Hover or focus a property to read which seam requirement it satisfies; click to pin. Without scripting, all three read in sequence. The properties are the Snowflake lineage's, line for line the seam's requirements.

§4

Minted at the core, carried to the periphery

mesh.5.3.md · M2 & M2.3

The seam is anchored at the consistent end. A fill's branded identity is minted once, at the moment the consistency-first book owner of M2 commits the fill, and the identity is stamped as part of the very same all-or-nothing transaction that records the fill, the transaction of M2.3 — so the name is decided exactly once, by the same authority and in the same atomic step that decides the value.

From there the identity propagates outward to the available surfaces, which carry it rather than inventing their own: the cache stores the resulting value under the identity, the stream appends an entry bearing it, the object store retains the record keyed by it, and the worker processes a job that references it. Identity flows from the consistent core to the available periphery, never the reverse. This direction matters: because the name is born with the value, in the one place that blocks rather than diverges, every downstream surface inherits a name it can trust without having had to agree to it.

§5

One fill across five surfaces

mesh.5.3.md · M2 & M3

Follow a single fill's branded identity across the system. First, the ledger records the fill and its effect on a balance under the identity, strongly consistent, allowed to block rather than diverge, because a wrong or half-applied balance is the error the system refuses. Second, the feed caches the value the fill produced under the identity, available, served from local memory and marked stale under a partition rather than withheld. Third, the per-instrument stream appends the fill as an ordered entry carrying the identity, available, read by consumers at their own pace. Fourth, the global object store retains the fill's record keyed by the identity, globally available and eventually coherent across regions. Fifth, the worker tier runs a job for the fill that references the identity, on its own trade.

Five surfaces, five trades, and one identity on all five — and because the identity is identical everywhere, the system answers “is this the same fill” with a comparison of names, never a coordination across surfaces. The seam turns five representations into one entity by giving them one name.

§6

Why the seam holds where coordination would not

mesh.5.3.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

The alternative to a shared identity is to have the surfaces agree on identity at runtime — to reconcile, when they meet, whether their respective records refer to the same fill. That agreement is a coordination, and coordination is precisely what the CAP limit makes unavailable under partition: the surfaces that need to agree might be on opposite sides of a split and unable to reach each other.

A minted-once, coordination-free identity sidesteps the problem entirely, because the surfaces never need to agree — they were each handed the same name at the source, so they already concur without communicating. This is why the seam survives a partition exactly where a consensus-on-identity would stall, and it is the reason the identity must be Snowflake-style rather than a value assigned by a central database. A central sequence would be a single point that every surface had to consult to learn a name, which is the bottleneck and the partition-vulnerable dependency the limit punishes. The seam works by removing the need to coordinate on identity, not by coordinating it faster.
