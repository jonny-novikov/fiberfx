M5.1 · Segmenting Consistency and Availability

The dominant strategy.

The fourth coping strategy, and the one real systems are really built with: stop choosing a single trade for the whole system, and instead segment it into parts, each with the trade its stakes call for. This dive reads segmentation as the dominant strategy of the menu — the design that yields the right whole from differently-traded parts.

No measurement here: this dive reads a strategy and makes an argument, not a benchmark. Two primary sources carry it (Gilbert & Lynch's 2012 treatment of segmenting consistency and availability, and Brewer's 2012 retrospective that the choice is continuous and fine-grained); the strategy is external and settled, and EchoMesh's segmented structure is named as design built on it. The five dimensions are M5.2; the seam that holds a segmented system together is M5.3.

Proposed · not shipped

Segmenting a system into differently-guaranteed components, and the candor that its whole-system guarantees are application-specific, are Gilbert and Lynch's settled results; the fine granularity that justifies it is Brewer's. EchoMesh's segmented structure is the course's proposal, built on shipped surfaces and separated from the proposed composition at M8.2; the shipped-versus-proposed line is drawn there. All magnitudes named below are qualitative — strong, high, low, partial, bounded, neither — carrying no measured number.

§1

Segmentation is redesign, not compromise

mesh.5.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

The strategy begins from an observation about real systems: many do not have a single uniform requirement, because some aspects need strong consistency and others need high availability. Faced with the CAP limit, the natural move is not to pick one property for everything and live with the loss, but to redesign the system — to segment it into components, each of which provides a different type of guarantee.

The shift is structural rather than a weakening. No part is made worse: the parts that need consistency are built fully consistent, the parts that need availability are built fully available, and the design's work is drawing the boundaries between them so each part faces only the stakes it was built for. Segmentation is the act of cutting the system along those boundaries so that the CAP trade is made many times, locally and correctly, instead of once, globally and badly.

§2

Why it is the dominant strategy

mesh.5.1.md · mesh.1.3.md

Gilbert and Lynch present segmentation as the approach that shows up frequently in practice, and the reason is that it is what building a real system in fact requires. The three earlier strategies each describe one surface's trade — consistency-first, availability-first, or tuned — but a system of any size is not one surface; it is many, with different stakes. So the question is never what trade the system as a whole should make, but what trade each of its surfaces should make.

The menu of M1.3 framed exactly that as the per-subsystem question, and segmentation is the systematic answer: ask the question once per surface and build each surface to its own answer. Every system that serves both a settlement path and a price feed, both a coordination service and a content cache, is already segmented whether or not its designers named it so. The strategy is dominant because the alternative — one trade for everything — does not survive contact with a system that does more than one thing.

Consistency-first. Under a partition the surface gives up availability to stay correct — it blocks rather than diverges, the M2 ledger. One surface's trade: it describes how a single surface behaves, not how a whole system of many surfaces should.
Availability-first. Under a partition the surface gives up consistency to stay up — it diverges within a bound and marks its lag, the M3 feed. Again one surface's trade, correct for the surfaces whose stakes are liveness rather than safety.
Tuned. The surface picks a stated point between the ends — a staleness budget, the M4 dial. Still one surface's trade: a precise middle, but a single setting for one surface, not an answer for a many-surface system.
Segmenting · the dominant strategy. Ask the per-surface question once for each surface and build each to its own answer — the other three strategies applied where each fits. It is dominant because a real system is many surfaces with different stakes, and one trade for everything does not survive contact with a system that does more than one thing.

§3

Why guaranteeing neither is a feature

mesh.5.1.md · Brewer 2012

Seen from the outside, a segmented system guarantees neither strong consistency nor full availability, and under the two-of-three reading that looks like the worst outcome — a system that lost both. The reading is wrong, and seeing why is the point of the chapter. This is the argument of M4.3 raised from one surface to the whole system. The whole guarantees neither property uniformly precisely because each part guarantees exactly the right thing: the consistent parts are fully consistent, the available parts are fully available, and the whole is only the union of those parts, which has neither property uniformly because it was never meant to.

Brewer's retrospective is the warrant — the choice is fine-grained, made per subsystem, per operation, even per datum, so a system that is consistent in one place and available in another is not half-failed, it is correctly built. The system-level neither is not a loss; it is the shadow that a set of correct per-part choices casts when one insists on describing the whole with a single label. The feature is that each part is right; the system-level neither is what right looks like from a vantage point that ignores the parts.

One surface, raised to the whole: the case that a tuned surface guaranteeing neither property fully is a deliberate choice is M4.3, made for a single surface. This dive raises that same argument from one surface to the whole segmented system, where the parts are not three settings of one dial but many surfaces, each at its own point.

§4

The candor segmentation requires

mesh.5.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

Segmentation buys its correctness at the price of a tidy whole-system label, and the candor it requires is to admit that. Gilbert and Lynch are explicit that the precise guarantees a segmented system provides are specific to the application and the particular partitioning, and that understanding these schemes in general remains an open question. So a segmented system cannot wear a clean system-wide property — calling it the consistent system, or the available system, erases the boundaries that make it work.

The candid move is to state each part's guarantee and the boundaries between the parts, and to let a consumer reason about the specific surface it touches rather than the system in the abstract. This is why EchoMesh states a trade per surface — the ledger's, the feed's, each budgeted surface's — rather than advertising a single label for the system. A whole-system CP or AP badge is the category error the menu warned against, applied to a thing that is deliberately neither.

The category error

One label for the whole

Calling a segmented system the consistent system, or the available system — a whole-system CP or AP badge. It erases the boundaries that make the system work and tells a consumer nothing true about the surface it actually touches.

claim: the system is consistent · cost: erases the boundaries

The candid move

A trade per surface

State each part's guarantee and the boundaries between the parts, and let a consumer reason about the specific surface it touches. The ledger's trade, the feed's trade, each budgeted surface's trade — stated, not summarized into one label.

claim: this surface guarantees X · cost: no tidy whole-system label

§5

Where EchoMesh stands

mesh.5.1.md · proposed application

EchoMesh is segmented by construction, not by accident. Its ledger is consistency-first, its feed and streams are availability-first, its global store is available-and-eventually-coherent, and its worker tier carries its own trade; no single setting governs the system, and none was meant to. This is the spectrum the emulator above draws: four parts at four points, and a whole that is their union.

What the next dive does is show that this segmentation runs along every one of Gilbert and Lynch's five dimensions at once, mapping each to an EchoMesh surface. What the dive after it does is answer the question this structure raises — how surfaces with different trades stay one system — with the branded identity that is the seam. The strategy is the chapter's spine, and the rest of the chapter is its anatomy.
