M5.2 · Segmenting Consistency and Availability

The five dimensions.

Segmentation runs along five dimensions, and EchoMesh is cut along every one of them at once. This dive takes Gilbert and Lynch's five — data, operation, functional, user, and hierarchical partitioning — in turn, reads the example the paper gives for each, and maps each to an EchoMesh surface.

No measurement here: this dive reads five dimensions and maps each to a surface, not a benchmark. Two primary sources carry it (Gilbert & Lynch's 2012 enumeration of the dimensions, and Brewer's 2012 fine-grained choice); the dimensions and their examples are external and settled, and the EchoMesh mappings are named as design built on them. The strategy these dimensions serve is M5.1; the seam that holds them together is the next dive, M5.3.

Proposed · not shipped

The five dimensions and their examples — shopping cart, PNUTS, Chubby-and-DNS, Craigslist, the geographic hierarchy — are Gilbert and Lynch's settled result. EchoMesh's mapping, which surface takes which trade along each dimension, is the course's proposal, built on shipped surfaces and composed into the mesh at M8.2; the shipped-versus-proposed line is drawn there. Magnitudes below are qualitative words, not measurements.

§1

Data partitioning

mesh.5.2.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

The first dimension splits by what the data is: different data may require different levels of consistency and availability. Gilbert and Lynch's example is an e-commerce site, where a shopping cart can be highly available and occasionally inconsistent — losing a recent update in a rare anomaly is tolerable — and product inventory can be somewhat out of date, but the checkout, billing, and shipping records must be strongly consistent, because a finalized order has to reflect what the customer intended.

EchoMesh makes the same cut. The ledger's data — balances, positions, settlement records — is strongly consistent, held by the single owner of M2, because a wrong balance is the one error the system cannot tolerate. The feed's data — quotes, ticks, recent prices — is availability-first, served from the near-cache of M3, because a quote a moment stale is useful while a withheld quote is not. One system holds both, traded differently, because the data differs in what it can tolerate.

§2

Operation partitioning

mesh.5.2.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

The second dimension splits by what the operation does: different operations may require different levels of consistency and availability. Gilbert and Lynch's example is a system that keeps read-only operations highly available while letting operations that modify the database block during a partition, and that guarantees consistency for a purchase while letting a query return out-of-date data — the design Yahoo's PNUTS provides, with different trade-offs for different kinds of read and write.

EchoMesh routes operations the same way. A commit operation — recording a fill, moving a balance — runs through the consistency-first path of M2.3, an all-or-nothing transaction that is allowed to block rather than diverge, because a half-applied fill is unacceptable. A query operation — reading a recent price, tailing a stream — runs through the availability-first path, always answered, because a slightly stale read is acceptable and a stopped one is not. The same datum can be touched by both kinds of operation; the operation's stakes, not only the data's, decide the trade.

§3

Functional partitioning

mesh.5.2.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

The third dimension splits by subservice: many systems divide into subservices with different requirements. Gilbert and Lynch's example composes three — a coordination service like Chubby for coarse-grained locks and strong consistency, a naming service like DNS that caches for weak consistency but high availability, and a third subservice with yet another trade for content distribution.

EchoMesh's architecture is this dimension made concrete: a set of functional subservices, each with its own trade, composed into one system. The ledger is the strongly consistent coordination core. The cache and feed are the available read tier. The per-instrument streams are the available, ordered record. The global object store is the globally available, eventually coherent retention. The worker tier is its own trade again, executing jobs off the bus. This is the cleanest of the five mappings, because EchoMesh is, in its structure, a functional segmentation — the chapters of this course are largely a walk through its functions, each chosen for its stakes.

§4

User partitioning

mesh.5.2.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

The fourth dimension splits by who and where: network partitions and poor performance correlate with geographic distance, so a system can divide its users by region. Gilbert and Lynch's example is a service like Craigslist that splits its servers between an east-coast and a west-coast data center, so that California users rely on the west-coast center and get high availability, with consistency readily achieved among the co-located west-coast servers, and the same for east-coast users.

EchoMesh applies this through regional placement: a user's reads are served from a nearby region, and within that region the relevant surfaces are co-located and well-connected, so they can be both available and consistent locally. The trade bites only across regions, where the network is the constraint the CAP limit describes. The placement mechanics that put a surface in the right region are M7; the dimension here is the recognition that who and where is a seam along which to cut.

§5

Hierarchical partitioning

mesh.5.2.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

The fifth dimension nests the others: some systems are organized hierarchically and partitioned along these dimensions multiple times. Gilbert and Lynch describe a hierarchy whose top level spans the whole world or the whole database and whose lower levels partition into geographically smaller, better-connected pieces, so that as you descend toward the leaves the CAP limit becomes less onerous — the relevant servers are better connected, allowing both stronger consistency and better availability — while the global root must relax.

EchoMesh layers the same way. At the leaf is a per-instrument book owner, a single writer co-located with the readers of that instrument, well-connected enough to be both consistent and available. Above it are regional streams that aggregate instruments within a region. At the root is the global object store, spanning regions, globally available and only eventually coherent, because global strong consistency is the expensive thing the limit forbids cheaply. Descending EchoMesh's hierarchy eases the trade exactly as the paper describes; the hierarchy is the frame that nests the data, operation, functional, and user cuts within each other.

§6

Five cuts, one system

mesh.5.2.md · Brewer 2012

The five dimensions are not five choices among which EchoMesh picks one; they are five cuts it makes at once, all crossing the same entities. A single fill is subject to a data cut — it lives in the consistent ledger and the available feed — an operation cut — it is written by a commit and read by a query — a functional cut — it passes through ledger, cache, stream, store, and worker — a user cut — it is read from whichever region asks — and a hierarchy that nests all of these from its instrument up to the global store.

Brewer's fine granularity is the same idea stated as a principle: the choice is made per subsystem, per operation, per datum, which is exactly per-function, per-operation, per-data. The consequence is the chapter's final problem. Five overlapping cuts on one fill mean five representations of it on surfaces with different trades, and something has to keep them the same fill. That something is the seam, and it is the next dive.

The data cut on one fill. The fill lives in the consistent ledger as a settlement record and in the available feed as a recent price — the same event, held to two trades because what each surface tolerates differs.
The operation cut on one fill. The fill is written by a commit through the consistency-first path and read by a query through the availability-first path — the same event, touched by two kinds of operation with different stakes.
The functional cut on one fill. The fill passes through ledger, cache, stream, store, and worker — five subservices, each with its own trade, all carrying a representation of the one event.
The user cut on one fill. The fill is read from whichever region asks for it, served nearby and consistent in-region, with the trade biting only across regions.
The hierarchy on one fill. The fill nests from its per-instrument leaf up through regional streams to the global store — the frame that holds the other four cuts within each other.

Hover or focus a cut to read how it lands on one fill; click to pin. Without scripting, all five read in sequence. Brewer's principle: the choice is per subsystem, per operation, per datum — exactly per-function, per-operation, per-data.

The chapter's final problem: five overlapping cuts on one fill mean five representations of it on surfaces with different trades, and something has to keep them the same fill. That something is the seam — the branded identity of the next dive, M5.3, carried under a Proposed · not shipped note until the substrate-versus-proposal line is drawn at M8.2.
