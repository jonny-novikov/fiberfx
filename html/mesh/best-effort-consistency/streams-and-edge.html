M3.3 · Best Effort Consistency

Streams and the global edge.

The availability-first retention and delivery tiers, in two layers. Each instrument has its own append-only stream — the ordered, replicated record readers consume without contending with the writer — and deep retention runs on a globally distributed object store that keeps data close to where it is written and replicates it close to readers.

No measurement here: this dive reads two mechanisms, not a benchmark. Three primary sources carry it (a Valkey-class store's stream type, Tigris global object storage, and Gilbert & Lynch 2012); the mechanisms are external and settled, and EchoMesh's per-instrument streams and its retention are named as design built on them. Both tiers answer from local or nearby state and accept bounded staleness as the price of always answering — the availability-first posture of M3.

Proposed · not shipped

The append-only stream with its consumer groups and trimming, and the globally replicated S3-compatible edge, are shipped technology. EchoMesh's use — a per-instrument stream as the hot ordered record and Tigris as the global retention tier, both keyed by the branded identity — is the course's proposal, composed into the mesh at M2 and separated from the substrate at M8.2.

§1

Per-instrument streams

mesh.3.3.md · Valkey · Streams

EchoMesh gives each instrument its own append-only stream. A stream is a log with auto-generated, time-ordered entry IDs, so ordering is a property of the structure rather than something the application maintains; a producer appends an entry with one command, and the store assigns it an ID composed of a timestamp and a sequence. Entries are immutable once written.

Readers consume them in two modes — tailing the stream forward from a cursor to follow it in real time, or joining a consumer group that hands each entry to one member, tracks a per-consumer pending list, and lets an unacknowledged entry be reclaimed if a consumer fails. Memory stays bounded because the stream is trimmed, by length or by oldest entry, as it rolls forward, and the whole structure — including consumer-group state — is replicated to replicas and persisted, so it survives restarts.

Per instrument, this is an ordered, durable record of everything that happened to that instrument, readable by many consumers at their own pace. The shape of the commands — append, tail, group, trim — is the Valkey-class store's stream data type, lifted here with teaching comments rather than invented.

§2

Why a stream is availability-first

mesh.3.3.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

The stream is an availability-first surface because of how it is read. Consumers read from the log — from a replica or from the persisted record — in order, without taking any lock the writer holds and without waiting on a quorum; the writer appends and the readers follow, decoupled. That decoupling is what lets the delivery path keep answering: a consumer can read the most recent entries it has, in order, regardless of whether it is momentarily behind the producer.

The staleness is the replication and propagation delay — a replica may trail the authoritative log by the time it takes an entry to reach it — and it is bounded by that delay rather than open-ended. So the stream answers now, in order, from the state it holds, and reconciles toward the producer's latest as new entries arrive, which is the availability-first shape of the strategy: a response always, and consistency as best it can without blocking.

Tailing follows the log in real time. A reader reads forward from a cursor, taking the most recent entries it has in order, at its own pace. Many readers tail the same stream independently — none contends with the writer, which only appends.
A consumer group shares the work. Each entry goes to exactly one member of the group; a per-consumer pending list tracks what is in flight, and an unacknowledged entry can be reclaimed if a consumer fails, so a failed reader does not lose its share of the log.
Trimming bounds memory. The stream is trimmed by length or by oldest entry as it rolls forward, so the hot tier stays bounded. The records that age out are not discarded by EchoMesh — they are retained in the cold tier, Tigris.
The structure is replicated and persisted. The whole stream, including consumer-group state, is replicated to replicas and persisted, so it survives a restart. A reader can read from a replica — the source of the bounded staleness and the reason the delivery path keeps answering.

Hover or focus a mode to read it; click to pin. Without scripting, all four read in sequence.

§3

The global edge — Tigris

mesh.3.3.md · Fly.io · Tigris

Deep retention and global delivery run on Tigris, a globally distributed, S3-compatible object store. Tigris keeps an object close to the region where it is written, and when the object is requested it replicates it close to the requesting user, with the cross-region replication managed by the service according to global traffic patterns; the effect is low-latency reads anywhere and a replacement for a CDN with no configuration to manage.

Underneath, redundant regional clusters track where objects live, and the platform resolves writes from multiple regions into a coherent global view rather than leaving conflicts unhandled, with stronger read-after-write consistency available where a caller needs it. For EchoMesh this is the retention tier: the ordered records that age out of the hot streams are kept durably in Tigris and served to readers from a nearby region, so retention and global delivery are the same surface — written close to the source, read close to the consumer.

§4

Two tiers, one availability-first posture

mesh.3.3.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

The two layers divide retention by temperature and share one posture. The per-instrument stream is the hot tier — recent, ordered, in-memory, replicated — where consumers follow an instrument in near-real time. Tigris is the cold tier — durable, global, served from the edge — where the older record is retained and delivered worldwide.

Both answer from local or nearby state, both accept bounded staleness as the price of always answering, and both are keyed by the instrument's branded identity, so the same instrument names its stream and its retained objects across the surfaces that touch it; the identity contract is the /bcs course, described there rather than restated here. The two tiers are not different strategies — they are the same best-effort-consistency choice applied to hot and cold data.

Hot tier

The per-instrument stream

Recent, ordered, in-memory, replicated. Consumers tail it or share a consumer group, following the instrument in near-real time. Trimmed to bound memory; the staleness is the replication delay.

answers from a replica · bounded by replication delay

Cold tier

The Tigris global edge

Durable, global, served from a nearby region. The record that ages out of the stream is retained here and delivered worldwide, written close to the source and read close to the consumer.

answers from a near region · bounded by propagation window

The same theorem, twice: both tiers make the availability-first choice — answer now from local or nearby state, accept a bounded lag — and both are keyed by the branded identity, the seam that lets one instrument name its stream and its retained objects across every surface. The budget that turns “bounded” into a specific number per surface is M4.

§5

The trade, named — and how it sits with the feed and the book

mesh.3.3.md · Fly.io · Tigris

The win is an ordered, decoupled read path with low-latency global delivery that keeps answering whether or not a reader is momentarily behind. The loss is the mirror image and must be named: a stream replica can trail the producer by its replication delay, and a globally replicated object can be read from a region before the latest write has propagated there, so a reader can observe a value that is behind.

What keeps the loss bounded is that the lag is a propagation window rather than an unbounded divergence, and that the cold tier offers a stronger-consistency read where a caller needs the authoritative value rather than the fast one. Each surface chooses its point on the trade — fast-and-slightly-behind by default, stronger-and-slower on demand — and the budget that fixes that point per surface is M4.

The three surfaces of this chapter and M2 compose into one path for a value. Each makes a deliberate choice about the theorem — the book blocks rather than diverges, the feed and the streams diverge within a bound rather than block — and together they are how EchoMesh both decides values correctly and delivers them everywhere, fast.

Consistency-first · M2

The book decides

A single owner per instrument decides the authoritative value — one writer, one ordered history. It blocks rather than diverges: correctness from ownership, not a vote.

chooses: safety over liveness · cost: blocks under partition

Availability-first · M3.2

The feed serves

A near-cache serves that value to readers at memory speed, kept coherent by invalidation. Under partition it marks a value stale rather than withholding it.

chooses: liveness over safety · cost: a marked, bounded staleness

Availability-first · M3.3

The streams record & deliver

The per-instrument stream records the ordered history; Tigris retains and delivers it globally. Both answer from nearby state, behind by a bounded propagation window.

chooses: liveness over safety · cost: a bounded replication lag
