M3.1 · Best Effort Consistency

Availability first.

The second strategy, and the deliberate inverse of the first. Best-effort consistency makes one commitment without qualification — every request receives a response — and treats consistency as the thing to provide as well as it can, without ever blocking. The stance to take where a stale answer is useful and a withheld one is not.

No measurement here: this dive reads a strategy and its example, not a benchmark. Two primary sources carry it (Gilbert & Lynch 2012, which names the strategy and its examples, and Nygren, Sitaraman & Sun 2010, the description of the Akamai content delivery network). The strategy and the system are external and settled; where EchoMesh places its surfaces is named as design and built in the dives that follow.

Proposed · not shipped

The strategy and the content-delivery example are external and settled. That EchoMesh's market-data feed and globally replicated retention take the availability-first side is the course's proposal — built on shipped technology (the CDN, client-side caching, and the rest are settled), with the near-cache and global edge developed in M3.2 and M3.3 and the shipped-versus-proposed line drawn in full at M8.2.

§1

The strategy stated

mesh.3.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

Best-effort consistency makes one commitment without qualification — every request receives a response — and treats consistency as the thing to provide as well as possible rather than to guarantee. It fits where two conditions hold: a stale answer is tolerable for a bounded time, so serving slightly behind is acceptable, and a stopped answer is not, so withholding a response is the worse failure. Content served to a wide, geographically spread audience usually meets both.

Read against the theorem, the strategy is the explicit choice of availability over consistency when a partition forces the two apart: the surface answers from whatever local state it has rather than refusing, accepting that the answer may be behind. The proof that there is no way to be both correct and available under the partition is M1.2. The strategy does not pretend the staleness never happens; it bounds it and makes it the tolerable outcome when it does.

It is the deliberate inverse of the consistency-first strategy of M2, which blocks rather than diverges. Set the same partition in front of each surface and they answer in opposite ways — one keeps serving and reconciles, the other withholds until it is certain.

§2

The classic example — a content delivery network

mesh.3.1.md · Nygren, Sitaraman & Sun 2010

The example Gilbert and Lynch give is a content delivery cache, and Akamai is its canonical realization. A content delivery network improves performance by caching content at edge servers close to end users, so a request is served from the nearest edge rather than from a distant origin, avoiding the middle-mile bottlenecks that make the open Internet slow and variable.

The architecture is deliberately, heavily distributed — tens of thousands of servers across hundreds of networks worldwide — because proximity to the reader is what buys both speed and the ability to keep answering when any one origin or path is unreachable. Freshness is managed by a time-to-live: an edge serves a cached object until its TTL expires and then refreshes from origin, so a reader may see a value that is behind by at most roughly its TTL, and in practice low TTLs keep that window short while resolver caching downstream can widen it.

The delivery path is availability-first by construction: it answers from the edge, now, and reconciles toward the origin's current value on the next refresh. The emulator above walks exactly this path, and the one move that defines the strategy is the partition step — the edge keeps answering bounded-stale rather than withholding.

§3

Naming served through caching

mesh.3.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

The second example Gilbert and Lynch name is naming served through caching, and it is the same pattern in a different domain. A name resolver answers a lookup from a cached record until that record's lifetime expires, then refreshes; between refreshes it may hand back a value that has since changed at the authority, trading strong consistency for the ability to answer immediately and locally.

The trade is identical to the content delivery network's — proximity and a guaranteed answer in exchange for bounded staleness — and naming it twice, in content and in naming, is how the 2012 paper shows the strategy is general rather than a quirk of one system. Both are surfaces that answer from a cache and let correctness catch up. Set the two side by side to see the one strategy under two domains.

Content · the CDN

A cached object near the reader

An edge serves a cached object from close to the user, behind by at most roughly its TTL, and refreshes from origin when the TTL expires. Proximity buys speed and the ability to keep answering.

traded: strong consistency · for: proximity, speed, an answer always

Naming · through caching

A cached record at a resolver

A resolver answers a lookup from a cached record until its lifetime expires, then refreshes; between refreshes it may hand back a value that has since changed at the authority.

traded: strong consistency · for: an immediate, local answer

Content delivery is the first reading. An edge serves a cached object from close to the reader, behind by at most roughly its TTL, refreshing from origin on expiry. It answers now and reconciles later — availability over consistency, made acceptable by a bounded staleness window.
Naming is the same pattern in a different domain. A resolver answers a lookup from a cached record until its lifetime expires, then refreshes; between refreshes it may hand back a value that has since changed at the authority. The trade is identical to the CDN's: proximity and a guaranteed answer for bounded staleness.
Naming it twice shows it is general. Gilbert and Lynch give content and naming as two examples of the one strategy — best-effort consistency is not a quirk of one system but a coping strategy that recurs wherever a stale answer is useful and a withheld one is not.
M2 is the deliberate inverse. The consistency-first surfaces of M2 block rather than diverge: under the same partition they withhold until they are certain, paying latency to never be wrong. Best-effort consistency answers and may be behind. Two opposite answers to the one forced choice — placed on purpose, per surface.

§4

What “answer now, reconcile later” buys, and costs

mesh.3.1.md · the trade, named fairly

The shape of the strategy is an immediate local answer backed by an eventual refresh. The win is that the surface is always responsive and fast, because it serves from nearby state and never waits on a distant authority or a quorum. The cost is the mirror image and must be named: a reader can observe a stale value, one that the authority has already moved past, for as long as the refresh interval allows.

The discipline that makes this acceptable is that the staleness is bounded, not unbounded — a TTL, an invalidation, or a reconciliation on heal caps how far behind a reader can be — and bounded staleness is a contract a reader can reason about, where unbounded staleness is not. So availability-first is not a license to be arbitrarily wrong; it is a commitment to answer now and to be no more than a stated amount behind, with the amount tunable per surface, which is the subject of M4.

The win

Always responsive

The surface answers from nearby state and never waits on a distant authority or a quorum. Fast, and available even when an origin or a path is unreachable.

serve from local state · no wait, no quorum

The cost

An observable stale value

A reader can see a value the authority has already moved past, for as long as the refresh interval allows. The mirror image of the win, named plainly rather than hidden.

behind by a refresh interval · named, not hidden

The discipline

Bounded, not unbounded

A TTL, an invalidation, or a reconciliation on heal caps how far behind a reader can be. Bounded staleness is a contract; the bound is tunable per surface.

the bound is the contract · M4 tunes it

The line that keeps the strategy honest: availability-first is a commitment to answer now and to be no more than a stated amount behind, not a license to be arbitrarily wrong. The amount is the staleness budget, set per surface — the subject of M4, where the trade stops being a binary and becomes a dial.

§5

Where EchoMesh stands

mesh.3.1.md · proposed placement

EchoMesh's market-data feed and its globally replicated retention are best-effort-consistency surfaces: always answering, bounded in staleness, reconciling toward the authoritative value rather than blocking. The feed serves recent values from a near-cache kept fresh by a coherence broadcast, and marks a value stale rather than withholding it when a partition cuts the refresh — built in M3.2.

Retention and delivery run on per-instrument streams and a globally replicated object store that answer from local or nearby state — built in M3.3. Each makes the availability-first choice the strategy describes, and each makes it deliberately, as the counterpart to the consistency-first surfaces of M2 that block rather than diverge.

The strategy and the example are external and settled. EchoMesh's placement of its feed and retention on this side of the trade is design; the near-cache, coherence broadcast, and global edge that implement it are the proposal the next two dives develop — all run on shipped technology and are separated from the proposed composition at M8.2.
