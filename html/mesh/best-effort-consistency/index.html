M3 · Best Effort Consistency

Answer now, reconcile toward correct.

The second strategy on the menu, and the deliberate opposite of the first: guarantee a response always, and provide consistency as best the network allows — the right choice for content served to readers worldwide, where a slightly stale answer is useful and a withheld one is not.

The classic example is a content delivery network caching close to users; EchoMesh takes the same stance on its market-data feed and its globally replicated retention — answering from local state and bounding staleness rather than forbidding it, marking a value stale under partition instead of refusing it. Where the consistency-first ledger of M2 blocks rather than diverges, these surfaces diverge within a bound rather than block. The strategy and its examples are settled and external (Gilbert & Lynch, Akamai, Valkey, Tigris); EchoMesh's composition is what this course proposes.

Proposed · not shipped

The near-cache, the coherence broadcast, and the per-instrument streams are EchoMesh components built on shipped mechanisms; their assembly into the mesh taught here is the proposed design.

§1

The strategy

mesh.3.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

Best-effort consistency makes the opposite commitment to best-effort availability: a response is guaranteed, and consistency is provided as well as it can be without ever blocking. It fits where two conditions hold — a stale answer is tolerable for a while, and a stopped answer is not — which is the case for content served to a wide, geographically spread audience.

Its classic example is a content delivery network: a CDN caches content at edge servers close to end users to avoid middle-mile bottlenecks, serving from the nearest cache and refreshing on a time-to-live, so a reader may see a value bounded in staleness by the TTL. Naming served through caching, where a resolver answers from a cached record until it expires, is the same pattern. The lesson mirrors the first chapter: availability-first is not a refusal to be correct — it is a decision to answer now and reconcile toward correctness, choosing availability over consistency when the two collide.

§2

Market data on the near-cache

mesh.3.md · Redis · client-side caching

A market-data feed must always answer and may answer slightly behind, which is exactly the availability-first shape. EchoMesh serves it from a near-cache: readers hold a local cache of recent values, and the authoritative value — computed by the consistency-first book's single owner of M2 — is kept fresh by a coherence broadcast.

The mechanism is the client-side-caching protocol of a Valkey-class store: when a client caches a key, the server remembers it and pushes an invalidation message the moment that key changes, so the cache flushes the stale entry and the next read fetches the new value. In the common case the reader serves the most recent value from local memory with no round-trip; when the invalidation channel is cut by a partition, the reader cannot be sure its cached value is current, so it serves it marked stale rather than withholding it — availability over consistency, with the staleness made visible rather than hidden.

The second dive, M3.2, builds the near-cache and the coherence broadcast, and makes the stale-marking under partition concrete.

§3

Streams and the global edge

mesh.3.md · Valkey Streams · Tigris

Retention and delivery are the other availability-first surfaces, and EchoMesh splits them across two tiers. Each instrument has its own append-only stream — a log with auto-generated, time-ordered IDs, appended with one command and tailed by readers or shared across a consumer group, with old entries trimmed to keep memory bounded. The stream is the ordered, replicated record that readers consume without contending with the writer.

Deep retention runs on Tigris, a globally distributed, S3-compatible object store that keeps an object close to the region where it was written and replicates it close to the readers who request it, giving low-latency global reads and replacing a CDN with no configuration to manage. Both tiers answer from local or nearby state and accept bounded staleness as the price of always answering.

The per-instrument stream. An append-only log with auto-generated, time-ordered entry IDs: producers append with one command, consumers tail it or share work via a consumer group, and trimming bounds the stream's size. The ordered, replicated record readers consume without contending with the writer.
The global edge · Tigris. Deep retention on a globally distributed, S3-compatible object store: an object is kept close to the region where it was written and replicated close to the readers who request it, giving low-latency global reads and replacing a CDN with no configuration to manage.
Both, the same stance. The stream and the global edge each answer from local or nearby state and accept bounded staleness as the price of always answering — the availability-first commitment, applied to retention and delivery rather than to a live read.
