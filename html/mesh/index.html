EchoMesh, In Depth — the course

The CAP theorem is a menu,
not a wall.

Practitioners building distributed services must still do the impossible.

A distributed system cannot hold consistency and availability together under a partition — that is proved and final. Yet working systems ship anyway, by coping with the trade rather than defeating it. EchoMesh segments that trade across a Branded Component System stack on the BEAM — and makes the infrastructure beneath transparent, the same code from a laptop to a fleet of ephemeral machines.

The senior successor to the EchoMesh chapters of The Art of BCS. Every figure on every page is quoted from a cited source. The course rests on the /bcs identity contract for the seam that holds a segmented system together.

Proposed · not shipped

EchoMesh is a forward concept this course introduces; its pieces are real and shipped, and their composition into the mesh taught here is the proposed design.

§1

The thesis — do the impossible, by segmenting it

mesh.landing.md · mesh.0.md

Gilbert and Lynch put the paradox plainly: practitioners must, in their words, “do the impossible.” The resolution is that they do not defeat CAP — they choose where to spend it. A real system is not one trade-off but many, assigned subsystem by subsystem to fit what each part owes its users. Three claims carry the course.

Claim one

The theorem is a design instrument

Under a partition a service gives up consistency or availability — and on a healthy network, keeping replicas consistent still costs coordination latency on every request (PACELC). The design's job is to decide, deliberately, where to pay it.

read as a menu, not a wall · per subsystem

Claim two

Segment the trade across an owned stack

Each surface is assigned its own trade and placed where it belongs — the BEAM and a single writer for consistency, EchoCache and streams for availability, Tigris for globally replicated retention, Postgres with Ecto.Multi for the regulated ledger.

the whole guarantees neither · each part guarantees what it should

Claim three

The branded identity is the seam

Segmentation coheres only if a single entity stays addressable as it crosses surfaces that chose different trades. The 14-byte branded id makes a fill the same entity in the book, the cache, the stream, the object store, and the worker.

it names state rather than carrying it · one entity, many trades

And the second move: transparent infrastructure. The BEAM and the FLAME pattern let one program treat its whole self as elastic — the same code on a laptop and across a fleet of subsecond-launch machines, placement by the branded id over a consistent-hashing ring that computes the same owner on one node or a thousand. Scale becomes a property of the design rather than a deployment target to operate.

§2

The stack, placed on the trade

mesh.landing.md · the owned stack

EchoMesh assigns its surfaces across an owned stack, each piece sitting where its trade belongs. Hover a surface to read its place on the spectrum and the role it plays.

BEAM · the owned runtime. Elixir and the BEAM, whose built-in distribution, supervision, and process model hold the coordinator, log, broker, and orchestrator as language properties. The single owning writer per instrument is the consistency-first surface that needs no quorum. This is M6.1.
EchoCache · availability-first. The near-cache, the ring, and the journal — serving the most recent value, kept coherent by an invalidation broadcast, marked stale under partition rather than withheld. This is M6.2.
EchoMQ · the bus and the log. The claim-check bus and the per-instrument stream log — the three messaging shapes, each in its primitive; the availability-first delivery and the durable spine. This is M6.2, with the protocol in depth at /echomq.
Tigris · globally replicated retention. A globally distributed, S3-compatible object store that writes close to a region and replicates close to requesters — availability-first deep retention with no configuration to manage. This is M6.3.
Postgres · the regulated ledger. Ecto.Multi composes several writes into one all-or-nothing transaction that rolls back as a unit — the consistency-first surface that blocks rather than diverges. This is M6.3, the line drawn at the edge.

Hover or focus a surface to switch; click to pin. Three availability-first surfaces, two consistency-first — segmentation made concrete.
