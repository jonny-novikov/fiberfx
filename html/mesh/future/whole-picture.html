M8.1 · The Whole Picture

The architecture, seen whole.

The segmented mesh of M5 runs on the owned stack of M6 with the transparent infrastructure of M7, and this dive lays the surfaces out, gives each its trade in Elixir, and traces one fill across the weave. The thread that makes it a single system is the branded identity, minted at the consistent core and carried to the available periphery.

No measurement here: the Elixir illustrates the design — the proposed composition — rather than benchmarking it. Two primary sources frame the assembly: the segmentation strategy [1] and the fine-grained, per-subsystem nature of the choice [2]. The picture is not layers stacked to hold something up; it is surfaces woven by one name.

Proposed · not shipped

The architecture is built from shipped substrate; what EchoMesh proposes is the assignment of trades across those surfaces and their binding by one branded identity. The whole-picture view here is the proposed composition; the piece-by-piece accounting is M8.2.

§1

The thread — one branded identity

mesh.8.1.md · /bcs

The thread woven through every surface is a branded identity — time-ordered so identities sort by when they were created, and coordination-free so any node can mint one without consulting a registry. Its full contract is the /bcs course; here it is the thread, shown only as much as the weave needs. Because the value is fixed when it is minted, every surface that later receives it agrees on which entity it names without agreeing with each other at runtime.

§2

The strands — each surface's trade

mesh.8.1.md

The consistency-first strand is the ledger: one process owns an instrument's book — a single writer, addressed through a registry, processing messages in order — recording a fill all-or-nothing and blocking or aborting rather than diverge. The availability-first strands answer from state held close and reconcile toward correct; the elastic strand runs on an ephemeral machine and is gone when done. All carry the same identity.

§3

The weave — one fill across the surfaces

mesh.8.1.md

The surfaces become one system in the function that weaves them. A fill is recorded at the ledger, which mints the identity on the consistent side; that identity is carried outward to the cache, the streams, the store, and a worker, each of which made its own trade. There is no surface beneath another — they are peers, joined by the thread.

§4

What the whole guarantees

mesh.5.1.md [1]

The candid system-level statement is the one the heart of the course reached: the whole of EchoMesh guarantees neither consistency nor availability as a global property, and that is a feature rather than an omission. A surface that needs correctness has it; a surface that needs a response under partition has that; the whole is the sum of those local guarantees and makes no single global one, because that is exactly what the proof of M1 denies.

The framework is careful here, and so is this dive: what a segmented whole guarantees is application-specific, assembled from the parts rather than proven once for the system, and treated in the literature as an open, design-dependent question [1].

§5

Why a mesh and not a stack

mesh.8.1.md [2]

It is worth being precise about why the organizing image is a weave and not a stack of layers or a row of supports, because the wrong image hides the architecture. In a layered picture, each layer rests on the one below and a request passes down through all of them; the bottom layer is load-bearing for everything above it. EchoMesh has no such order. The ledger does not sit under the cache, and the cache does not sit under the streams; they are peer surfaces, each with its own trade, and none depends on another being reachable to do its job — which is the entire point of segmenting under the CAP limit.

What joins them is the thread that runs through all of them at once, the branded identity, not a foundation they all stand on. Describe EchoMesh as supports holding up a runtime and you have described a single node and lost the mesh; the mesh is the crossing of independent surfaces, and the identity is what crosses them. The choice at fine granularity is what makes the weave legitimate rather than contradictory — the decision is made per surface and per operation [2].
