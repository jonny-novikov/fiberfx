M8.2 · Real and Proposed

The candid ledger.

Every chapter ended by promising that the line between what is shipped and what is proposed would be drawn in full here, and this dive draws it, piece by piece. The substrate is real and in production use; what is proposed is the module that weaves those calls together under one branded identity. In code, the line is sharp: the inner calls are real, the weave is the proposal.

No measurement here: this dive is an accounting, not a benchmark. Each real piece is cited to its primary source [1]–[8]; the proposed composition, EchoMesh, is named rather than cited, because it is the proposal this course argues for.

Proposed · not shipped

Each piece below is shipped and cited; the assembly into one named system is the proposal, separated piece by piece here. EchoMesh is the proposed composition — not a product already on a shelf.

§1

What is real

mesh.8.2.md · cited [1]–[8]

Seven kinds of shipped technology carry the architecture, each with a documented home: the BEAM [1]; a Valkey-class store's client-side caching behind EchoCache [2]; the claim-check bus [3]; the per-instrument streams [4]; Tigris for global retention [5]; PostgreSQL's serializable isolation [6] expressed through Ecto.Multi [7]; and the FLAME pattern for elastic work [8]. Shown as the calls they are, none is exotic.

§2

What is proposed

mesh.8.2.md

What this course proposes is not any of those pieces but their arrangement. EchoMesh is the claim that this specific set, assigned the trades the earlier chapters chose and bound together by one branded identity, composes into a single coherent system. The proposal is thin where the substrate is thick: it is mostly the weave. The names that mark it — EchoMesh, EchoCache — denote that proposed assembly, not a separate catalogue of shipped products predating the course.

§3

The line, in code

mesh.8.2.md

Set the two blocks side by side and the line is unmistakable. The first is calls into other people's libraries, each behaving as its documentation says. The second introduces no new capability — no storage engine, no protocol, no runtime — it only decides which call serves which surface and threads one identity through all of them. Everything load-bearing in the first block is external and cited; everything new in the second is composition.

§4

Why the line matters

mesh.8.2.md [6][2]

Keeping the line visible is a matter of candor about where each claim's authority comes from. That the ledger blocks rather than diverges rests on PostgreSQL's documented behavior [6]; that EchoCache stays coherent rests on the client-side-caching protocol [2]. Those stand on their sources. That EchoMesh guarantees what it guarantees is a claim about the composition, resting on the argument across these chapters — the segmented-whole guarantee, which the framework treats as application-specific. A reader deserves to know which sentences lean on mature technology and which on this course's design.

§5

What would make the proposal real

mesh.7.3.md

Candor cuts both ways, so it is worth naming what stands between the proposal and a shipped EchoMesh. The implementation, as M7.3 argued, is small — a supervised process that owns a book, a function wrapped to run on an ephemeral machine, a hash of an identity onto a ring — but small is not the same as written. Beyond the code, the course's own discipline would demand a conformance suite that holds the composition to its stated trades, and measurement under load this conceptual course did not perform. The gap is not a missing capability — the substrate supplies the capabilities — but the work of assembling, conforming, and measuring.
