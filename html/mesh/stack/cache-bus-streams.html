M6.2 · The Stack

EchoCache, the bus, and streams.

Three different jobs — keep a value fresh near readers, move work between surfaces, keep an ordered record per instrument — take three distinct shapes on one Valkey-class store: an invalidating near-cache, a reference-passing bus, and an append-only stream log. This dive builds each in the primitive that fits it, and marks all three on the available side of the line.

No measurement here: this dive maps three shapes to three primitives, not a benchmark. Three primary sources carry it (a Valkey-class store's client-side caching, the claim-check pattern, and the stream data type); the primitives are external and settled, and EchoCache, the bus, and the streams are named as the EchoMesh components built on them. The cache and streams come from M3; the line between them and the consistent ledger is the next dive, M6.3.

Proposed · not shipped

The primitives are external and settled — the near-cache's invalidation push is a Valkey-class store's client-side-caching protocol, the bus's reference-passing is the claim-check enterprise integration pattern, and the stream log is that store's data type. EchoCache, the EchoMQ bus, and the per-instrument streams are the EchoMesh components built on those primitives; their composition is the course's proposal, separated from the shipped substrate at M8.2. Command names below (CLIENT TRACKING, XADD, XREAD) name the primitives illustratively, not a measured deployment.

§1

Three shapes, one store

mesh.6.2.md · the design choice

The availability-first surfaces do not all need the same messaging behavior. One needs to hold a recent value close to many readers and keep it from going stale; one needs to hand work from one surface to another without choking on size; one needs to keep an ordered, durable record that many consumers replay at their own pace. These are three different shapes, and the design choice that runs through this dive is to match each to the primitive that fits it rather than stretch one mechanism across all three.

A Valkey-class store offers all three primitives, so the three shapes can share one store — which keeps the operational surface small — while each gets a mechanism built for its job. Three shapes, three primitives, one store. The diagram above lights one shape at a time; the three sections below build each.

§2

The near-cache: EchoCache

mesh.6.2.md · Redis client-side caching

The first shape is the near-cache, and its job is to answer reads from local memory while staying coherent with the authority. EchoCache is built on the client-side-caching protocol: when a reader caches a key, the server records that the reader holds it, and when that key is later modified the server pushes an invalidation to the reader, which flushes its stale copy so the next read refetches. This is the coherence broadcast of M3.2 pinned to its primitive — the broadcast is the protocol's invalidation push, not a mechanism EchoMesh invents on top.

The cached value is keyed by the instrument's branded identity, so a write to one instrument invalidates only the readers caching that instrument rather than the whole feed, and the identity that does this keying is the /bcs course's contract. The near-cache is the shape that keeps the available read path both fast and current-to-within-one-invalidation.

§3

The bus: the claim-check pattern

mesh.6.2.md · Hohpe & Woolf

The second shape is the bus, and its job is to move work between surfaces — to hand a fill from the ledger to a worker, say — without dragging a large payload through the broker. The primitive is the claim-check pattern: rather than carry the payload in the message, the bus stores the payload in a store and passes a small reference, a claim check, through the queue, and the consumer redeems the claim check to retrieve the payload when it needs it.

The fit is exact, because a messaging system is built to move a high volume of small messages and degrades when made to carry large ones, so keeping the payload in a store and the broker carrying references plays each to its strength. The claim check is a unique key, and EchoMesh already has the ideal one: the branded identity that serves as the seam in M5.3 is a unique, location-independent key, which is precisely what a claim check must be. The bus — the EchoMQ tier — passes that identity and lets the consumer fetch the payload from the store by it.

§4

The streams: the per-instrument log

mesh.6.2.md · Valkey Streams

The third shape is the stream, and its job is to keep an ordered, durable record of everything that happened to an instrument, readable by many consumers at their own pace. The primitive is the stream data type: an append-only log with auto-generated, time-ordered IDs, where a producer appends an entry with one command (XADD) and consumers either tail the log forward (XREAD) or join a consumer group that shares the entries out with per-consumer acknowledgment, and trimming keeps the log bounded as it rolls forward.

This is the per-instrument stream of M3.3 pinned to its primitive — the ordering is the structure's time-ordered IDs, not something the application maintains. The log replicates and persists, so it survives restarts, and because consumers read from it without taking a lock the writer holds, the record is decoupled from its production exactly as an availability-first surface requires.

§5

Why three primitives, not one

mesh.6.2.md · the design

It is worth saying why this is three primitives rather than one stretched thin, because the temptation is to pick a single mechanism and use it everywhere. Each mechanism is excellent at its own shape and a poor fit for the other two, so matching shape to primitive is the design, and putting all three primitives on one store is what keeps that design from multiplying the things EchoMesh has to operate.

All three shapes share one trait that places them in this chapter: each answers from local or replicated state and accepts bounded staleness, which is the availability-first side of the trade. Hover a wrong single mechanism below to see what it would lose.

Pub/sub for all three. A publish-subscribe channel would broadcast changes but retain nothing, so the stream's replayable record would be lost. It can invalidate a cache, but it cannot keep a durable, ordered log a late consumer replays.
A queue for all three. A queue would move work but keep no cache coherent and replay no history. It can hand a claim check from producer to consumer, but it cannot push an invalidation to a reader or let many consumers tail the same ordered record.
A log for all three. A log would record everything but pass no reference efficiently and invalidate no cached value. It can keep the ordered, durable record, but it neither flushes a stale near-cache nor moves a large payload out of the broker's way.

Hover or focus a single mechanism to read what it loses; click to pin. Without scripting, all three read in sequence. Each mechanism is excellent at its own shape and a poor fit for the other two.

§6

Where the line is

mesh.6.2.md · M6.3

These three are the available substrate of the system — the surfaces that keep answering and reconcile toward correct. The consistency-first core, the ledger, and the deep global retention, Tigris, are the next dive, and the line between the available substrate here and the consistent ledger there is the CAP boundary of the whole system.

Under a partition, the cache marks its value stale and the streams serve what they have replicated rather than withholding, which is the deliberate availability-first choice; the ledger, on the other side of the line, does the opposite. The third dive, M6.3, draws that line.
