M6 · The Stack

The architecture, made of real pieces.

The segmented system of M5 grounded in the technology it runs on, surface by surface — the owned BEAM runtime, the near-cache and the claim-check bus and the per-instrument streams on a Valkey-class store, globally replicated Tigris retention, and the regulated Postgres ledger through Ecto.Multi. Each pinned to its place on the consistency/availability spectrum.

Nothing new is traded here — the trades were chosen in the earlier chapters — but each is pinned to the substrate that makes it real. The pieces are external and shipped (the BEAM, OTP supervision, client-side caching, the claim-check pattern, Valkey streams, Tigris, Ecto.Multi); the assignment — which surface runs on which piece, and where each sits on the spectrum — is what EchoMesh proposes. The placement that puts these surfaces in the right region is M7.

Proposed · not shipped

The stack is shipped, all of it. What EchoMesh proposes is the assignment of surfaces to pieces and the position each takes on the consistency/availability spectrum. EchoCache, the EchoMQ bus, the per-instrument streams, and the regulated ledger are EchoMesh components built on those shipped pieces; their composition is separated from the shipped substrate at M8.2.

§1

The owned runtime

mesh.6.md · Erlang Solutions · Chechina et al. 2017

Everything that coordinates in EchoMesh runs on the BEAM, the Erlang virtual machine, and the choice is load-bearing rather than incidental. On the BEAM a unit of work is a lightweight process that owns its own state with no shared memory and communicates only by message passing, so the single-writer owner of M2 — one process owning one instrument's book — is not a pattern bolted on top of the runtime but the runtime's native shape.

Failure is handled by supervision: a supervisor monitors a process and replaces it when it crashes, and supervisors nest into a tree that can span nodes, so a global registry of process names lets a crashed owner be restarted without restarting the clients that address it. Coordination, an ordered log, a message broker, and an orchestrator are, on this runtime, properties of the language rather than separate services to operate.

The first dive, M6.1, reads the owned runtime and what it gives for free — the runtime that holds coordination, log, broker, and orchestrator as language properties.

§2

The cache, the bus, and the streams

mesh.6.md · Redis CLIENT TRACKING · Claim Check · Valkey Streams

The availability-first surfaces ride a Valkey-class store, and they take three distinct messaging shapes, each in the primitive that fits it. The near-cache — EchoCache — keeps recent values close to readers and is kept coherent by the client-side-caching protocol, where the server tracks the keys a client holds and pushes an invalidation the moment one changes.

The bus — the EchoMQ tier — moves work between surfaces using the claim-check pattern: rather than carry a large payload through the broker, it stores the payload and passes a small reference, a claim check, that the consumer redeems to retrieve it. The per-instrument streams are append-only logs with auto-generated, time-ordered IDs, appended with XADD and read with XREAD or shared across a consumer group, trimmed to stay bounded. Three shapes — invalidating near-cache, reference-passing bus, ordered log — on one store.

The second dive, M6.2, builds each in its primitive — the near-cache, the claim-check bus, and the per-instrument stream log.

§3

The store and the ledger, and the line between

mesh.6.md · Fly.io Tigris · Ecto.Multi

The two ends of the spectrum are the two persistent stores, and the CAP line of the whole system runs between them. Deep retention is Tigris, a globally distributed, S3-compatible object store that keeps an object near the region where it was written and replicates it near the readers who ask, which makes it availability-first by construction — global reads stay fast, at the cost of bounded staleness across regions.

The ledger is Postgres, written through Ecto.Multi so that a fill and its effects commit all-or-nothing: the operations apply in order and commit together as {:ok, changes}, and on any error every operation is rolled back, returning {:error, failed_operation, failed_value, changes_so_far} — which makes it consistency-first by construction. The edge answers always and reconciles toward correct; the ledger insists on correct and is willing to block.
