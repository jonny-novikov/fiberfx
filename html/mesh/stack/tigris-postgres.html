M6.3 · The Stack

Tigris and Postgres.

The two persistent stores are the two ends of the spectrum, and the CAP line of the whole system runs between them. Tigris, the globally replicated object store, is availability-first by construction; Postgres written through Ecto.Multi is the consistency-first ledger that blocks rather than diverges. This dive builds both and draws the line.

No measurement here: this dive reads two stores, not a benchmark. Three primary sources carry it (Tigris global object storage, the Ecto.Multi transaction abstraction, and PostgreSQL's transaction isolation); the stores are external and settled, and the retention tier and the regulated ledger are named as the EchoMesh components built on them. The available substrate is M6.2; the runtime is M6.1; the placement that distributes these stores is M7.

Proposed · not shipped

The globally replicated, S3-compatible retention is Tigris; the all-or-nothing transaction is Ecto.Multi; the serializable isolation that blocks or aborts rather than diverges is PostgreSQL's. These are shipped, settled external results. EchoMesh's proposal is the placement on the spectrum — Tigris as the availability-first retention and the Postgres ledger as the consistency-first core, with the line between them drawn where the segmentation requires — composed into the mesh at M8.2, where the shipped-versus-proposed line is drawn.

§1

The two ends of the spectrum

mesh.6.3.md

The earlier dives placed the runtime and the messaging shapes; this one places the two stores, and they are the spectrum's extremes. Tigris holds the deep retention and global delivery — the cold, broadly-read data — and it is built to answer from anywhere, fast, accepting that a reader far from a write may be briefly behind. Postgres holds the ledger — the balances and settlement records — and it is built to be correct above all, accepting that it may sometimes have to block to stay so.

One store leans all the way toward availability and one all the way toward consistency, and the line between them is the CAP boundary of the whole system. Everything the chapter has assembled sits on one side of that line or the other — which is the dial above, with each store at its end and the line drawn between them.

§2

Tigris: availability-first retention

mesh.6.3.md · Tigris

Tigris is a globally distributed, S3-compatible object store, and its design is availability-first in the literal sense the strategy means. It keeps an object near the region where it was written and, when the object is requested, replicates it near the requesting user, so reads are served from a nearby region at low latency and the store stands in for a CDN with no configuration to manage. This is the deep-retention tier of M3.3 pinned to its store: the ordered records that age out of the hot streams are kept durably here and delivered worldwide from the edge.

The cost is the one availability-first surfaces always pay, and it belongs in view: a read taken in a region the latest write has not yet reached may be behind, bounded by the replication window rather than open-ended, with a stronger-consistency read available where a caller needs the authoritative version over the fast one. Tigris answers everywhere and reconciles toward the latest, which is the availability-first shape.

§3

Postgres on Ecto.Multi: the consistent ledger

mesh.6.3.md · Ecto.Multi

The ledger is Postgres, and it is written through Ecto.Multi so that recording a fill is all-or-nothing. A fill and its effects — the entry, the balance change, the audit record — are composed into a single multi whose operations apply in order and commit together as one unit, returning the changes on success as {:ok, changes}; if any operation fails, every operation is rolled back and the failure is reported with the operation that caused it as {:error, failed_operation, failed_value, changes_so_far}.

There is no state in which a fill is half-recorded: either the whole effect lands or none of it does. This is the ledger of M2.3 pinned to its store, and the word regulated is apt — the ledger is the auditable record of what happened to money, so partial or ambiguous writes are the one outcome it cannot permit. The transaction is the unit of correctness, and the multi is how EchoMesh expresses it — hover the two outcomes below to walk a commit and a rollback.

All operations apply — {:ok, changes}. The entry, the balance change, and the audit record apply in order and commit together as one unit. The multi returns {:ok, changes} with the result of every named operation, and the whole effect of the fill has landed. This is the only outcome in which any part of the fill is recorded.
One operation fails — {:error, failed_operation, failed_value, changes_so_far}. If any operation fails, every operation is rolled back: nothing is committed. The failure is reported with the operation that caused it and the value that failed it, alongside the changes made before the failure. There is no half-recorded fill — either the whole effect lands or none of it does.

Hover or focus an outcome to read it; click to pin. Without scripting, both outcomes read in sequence. The success tuple is {:ok, changes}; the failure tuple is {:error, failed_operation, failed_value, changes_so_far}.

§4

Why the ledger blocks rather than diverges

mesh.6.3.md · PostgreSQL isolation

Atomicity keeps a single write all-or-nothing; isolation is what keeps concurrent writes from diverging, and it is where the ledger's consistency-first stance becomes concrete. At the serializable level, PostgreSQL emulates serial execution: it allows a set of concurrent transactions to commit only if it can prove their effect matches some order in which they had run one at a time, and when it cannot, it rolls one back with a serialization failure carrying SQLSTATE 40001, which the application retries from the beginning.

The consequence is precisely the consistency-first choice of M1.2 made concrete. Faced with a conflict it cannot serialize, the ledger does not accept two divergent histories and reconcile later; it blocks or aborts one writer and has it retry, so the history stays single and correct. The cost is the one the consistency-first side always pays: under contention, or when the network keeps a transaction from completing, a write is delayed or refused rather than allowed through. The ledger would rather stop than be wrong.

§5

The line drawn at the edge

mesh.6.3.md · M5 & M5.3

With both stores built, the line is visible. On one side is the available substrate — the near-cache, the bus, and the streams of M6.2, and Tigris here — all answering from local or nearby state and marking staleness rather than withholding under partition. On the other side is the consistent core — the ledger here — blocking or aborting rather than diverging. The line runs at the edge of that core, and it is not an accident of the technology but the cut the segmentation of M5 made on purpose: the data that must be correct is on the consistent side, and the data that must be available is on the available side.

Identity is what crosses the line without erasing it. The branded identity of M5.3 is minted at the ledger, on the consistent side, and carried outward to the cache, the streams, and Tigris on the available side, so a fill decided once on the consistent side is delivered and retained everywhere on the available side under the same name. The line separates deciding a value from delivering it, and the seam carries the name across — the amber arrow on the dial above.

§6

Where this leaves the stack

mesh.6.3.md · M6.1, M6.2, M7
