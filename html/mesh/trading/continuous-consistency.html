M4.1 · Trading Consistency for Availability

Continuous consistency.

The third strategy turns the binary consistency switch into a knob. Instead of choosing strong consistency or none, a surface bounds how far it may diverge and picks that bound to fit its stakes — tolerating an approximate answer when the stakes are low and tightening toward exact as they rise.

No measurement here: this dive reads a model and an example, not a benchmark. Two primary sources carry it (Gilbert & Lynch 2012 and Yu & Vahdat 2000) — the catalogue that names the strategy and Yu and Vahdat's continuous-consistency model with its conit and three metrics. The per-surface budget EchoMesh derives is named as design and built in the next dive, M4.2.

Proposed · not shipped

The model and the reservation example are external and settled. EchoMesh's per-surface staleness budget is the proposal the chapter develops from them — built on shipped mechanisms and separated from the proposed composition at M8.2. The reservation figures here are the toolkit's evaluation, not EchoMesh measurements.

§1

Turning the switch into a knob

mesh.4.1.md · Yu & Vahdat 2000

The model starts from a complaint about the usual choice: designers of replicated systems were forced to pick either strong consistency or none at all, with nothing in between. Yu and Vahdat's first move is to turn that binary switch into a knob, and the carrier for the setting is the conit — a logical consistency unit.

The shift is subtle and consequential: applications impose their consistency requirement on conits rather than on physical data items, so the requirement is expressed in terms of how much the application cares about divergence rather than in terms of the storage layout. A conit is a unit the application defines — a quantity it wants kept close to consistent — and its allowed divergence is what the surface tunes. That is what lets a single mechanism serve requirements that differ widely: each surface declares its own conit and its own tolerance.

§2

The three metrics

mesh.4.1.md · Yu & Vahdat 2000

Divergence is measured along three application-independent metrics, and a conit's consistency level is a bound on each. Numerical error counts the writes applied somewhere but not yet reached a given replica, so it measures how far a replica's value is off in magnitude. Order error counts the writes a replica has accepted tentatively that may still be reordered before they commit, so it measures how unsettled the sequence is. Staleness bounds the real-time delay between a write and its propagation, so it measures how far behind in time a replica may be.

Together the three capture the dimensions along which a replicated value can drift from an ideal single-copy execution, and bounding them gives a precise, quantitative grip on consistency that a binary label does not. A surface that bounds all three tightly approaches strong consistency; one that bounds them loosely approaches eventual; the space between is the continuum the strategy occupies.

Numerical error — how far off in magnitude. The writes applied somewhere in the system but not yet reflected at this replica. On the reservation conit, it is the seats sold elsewhere that this copy's count has not yet absorbed.
Order error — how unsettled the sequence is. The writes a replica has accepted tentatively that may still be reordered before they commit. It bounds how far the not-yet-final ordering can move under the replica's feet.
Staleness — how far behind in time. The real-time delay between a write and its propagation. It is a duration a person can reason about, which is why EchoMesh takes it as the most legible dial of the three.
The space between is the continuum. Bound all three tightly and the surface approaches strong consistency; bound them loosely and it approaches eventual. The continuum the strategy occupies is everything in between — a precise, quantitative grip a binary label does not give.

§3

Bounded staleness as a unit of trade

mesh.4.1.md · Yu & Vahdat 2000

Of the three metrics, staleness is the most legible as a dial, because it is a duration a person can reason about — how far behind the authority a reader may be. Tuning it is tuning the trade directly. A tight staleness bound keeps readers close to current, but holding it requires the replicas to coordinate more often, which costs availability and latency when the network is slow or partitioned; a loose bound lets readers fall further behind, but it stays available through a longer disruption because it demands less coordination.

The bound is therefore not a quality setting that is always better when tighter — it is a position on a trade, and moving it tightens one property by loosening the other. EchoMesh takes staleness as the primary unit of its per-surface budget for exactly this reason: it is the metric whose cost is easiest to state and whose value is easiest for a surface owner to choose.

Why EchoMesh chooses this dimension: a per-surface staleness budget is a recency window a surface owner sets and the engine holds the surface within. Of the conit's three metrics it is the one whose cost a surface owner can state plainly and whose value is the easiest to choose — the design the next dive M4.2 builds.

§4

Tightening as the stakes rise

mesh.4.1.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012 · Yu & Vahdat 2000

The reservation system is the example that makes "continuous" concrete, and both Yu and Vahdat and Gilbert and Lynch use it. A conit is defined over the seats on a flight — in the toolkit's evaluation, a flight initialized at four hundred seats — and each reservation draws the count down. While the cabin is nearly empty, an approximate seat count is harmless: two replicas disagreeing by a few seats cannot oversell a flight with hundreds free, so the surface tolerates a loose bound and stays cheaply available.

As the flight fills, the same disagreement becomes dangerous, because a few seats of error can oversell the last rows, so the application tightens the bound toward exact, paying more coordination precisely when the stakes justify it. The requirement is not a constant the surface is configured with once; it is a function of conditions the surface moves as those conditions change. That is what the strategy means by continuous — the dial is set and reset against the stakes, not fixed. The emulator above walks exactly this progression.

§5

What the toolkit showed

mesh.4.1.md · Yu & Vahdat 2000

The model is not only a framing; the toolkit demonstrated that a precise middle is worth occupying. TACT was evaluated on three applications with differing requirements — an airline reservation system, a distributed bulletin board, and load-distribution front ends to a web server — and against strong consistency it improved throughput by up to a factor of ten, while against weak consistency it provided a stated guarantee on the maximum inconsistency a read or write could observe.

The lesson EchoMesh takes is that the continuum is not a vague compromise but a measurable one: a surface in the middle is not merely "somewhat consistent," it is consistent to a bounded, named degree, which is a property a system can reason about and a reader can rely on. A precise middle is the thing worth building.

Where EchoMesh stands: it adopts the conit idea narrowed to its most legible dimension — a per-surface staleness budget. A surface whose stakes are low takes a loose window and stays cheaply available; a surface whose stakes rise tightens the window and pays the coordination to stay fresh. How that budget is set and what it costs is the next dive, M4.2; the argument that such a surface guarantees neither property — and that this is the point — is M4.3.
