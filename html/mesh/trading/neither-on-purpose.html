M4.3 · Trading Consistency for Availability

Neither, on purpose.

A surface tuned with a staleness budget guarantees neither strong consistency nor full availability — and that is the design, not a failure of it. It was built to deliver a bounded, chosen amount of each, and delivering exactly that is success, not a shortfall.

This dive is an argument, not a benchmark, carried by three primary sources (Gilbert & Lynch 2012, Brewer 2012, Abadi's PACELC): the trading strategy occupies the middle, the consistency-and-availability choice is continuous and fine-grained, and the cost of strong consistency is a standing one. The framing is external and settled; EchoMesh's stance — state the bound, mark the staleness, never claim a property the surface lacks — is named as design. This closes M4 and opens onto segmentation, M5.

Proposed · not shipped

That the trading strategy occupies the middle, and that the choice is continuous and fine-grained, are settled external results. That a tuned EchoMesh surface sits at a chosen point on the dial and states its bound is the course's design, applied to the budgeted surfaces this chapter builds and composed into the mesh at M5; the shipped-versus-proposed line is drawn in full at M8.2.

§1

Neither property, by construction

mesh.4.3.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012

A surface tuned with a staleness budget bounds each property to a stated amount, which means it holds neither in full. It is not strongly consistent, because a reader may be behind the authority by up to the window. It is not fully available, because holding the window may require the surface to coordinate, throttle its source, or — at the tight extreme — refuse rather than serve something too stale.

Gilbert and Lynch say as much of the trading strategy directly: it guarantees neither strong consistency nor full availability, occupying the space between rather than either pole. The point to hold onto is that this is the construction working as intended. The surface was built to deliver a bounded, chosen amount of each property, and delivering exactly that — not more, not both in full — is success, not a shortfall.

§2

A destination, not a way station

mesh.4.3.md · Brewer 2012

The instinct to read the middle as failure comes from the “two of three” framing, in which a system that has neither C nor A in full looks like it has lost something. Brewer's retrospective dismantles that reading. The three properties are continuous rather than binary — availability ranges from none to full, consistency comes in many strengths — and the choice between consistency and availability is made many times over, at fine granularity, differing by subsystem, by operation, even by the specific datum.

Once the properties are continuous and the choice is local, an intermediate point is not a compromise between two destinations; it is itself a destination, chosen on purpose for a surface whose requirements are intermediate. A surface that is consistent to a bounded degree and available to a bounded degree has not failed to reach a pole — it has arrived where it meant to go.

§3

The cost of pretending otherwise

mesh.4.3.md · design

The danger in a middle surface is not that it is in the middle but that it might pretend not to be. If a surface that is bounded-stale is described to its consumers as “basically consistent,” or one that throttles to hold a window is described as “always available,” the consumers build on a guarantee that does not hold, and the gap surfaces under exactly the stress the budget was meant to manage.

The candid move is the opposite: state the bound, and let consumers reason against it. A reader that knows it may be a stated amount behind can decide for itself whether that is acceptable for what it is doing; a reader told it is current when it is not cannot. This is why EchoMesh marks staleness on the feed and states a window on a budgeted surface rather than hiding either — an unstated middle is a trap, while a stated middle is a contract.

The stated middle

Says what it bounds

The window is the statement and the staleness mark is the statement. A reader that knows it may be a stated amount behind decides for itself whether that is acceptable for what it is doing.

what the consumer gets: a bound it can reason against · a contract

Pretending hides the trade. A bounded-stale surface called “basically consistent,” or a throttling one called “always available,” hands its consumers a guarantee that does not hold. The gap is invisible until the network turns — the precise moment the budget was meant to manage — and then it surfaces all at once.
Stating it makes a contract. Name the bound, and a consumer can reason against it. A reader that knows it may be a stated amount behind can decide for itself whether that is acceptable for what it is doing; a reader told it is current when it is not cannot make that decision at all.
EchoMesh marks the staleness. The proposed design states a window on a budgeted surface and marks staleness on the feed rather than hiding either. The mark and the window are the statement — a consumer builds on a known quantity rather than a claim that quietly fails under load.
A stated neither beats an unstated both. Dependability is in the stating: a bound a consumer can see is a bound a consumer can plan around. A surface that says what it guarantees is more trustworthy than one that claims both properties and delivers neither when a partition or a load spike arrives.

Hover or focus a panel to read it; click to pin. Without scripting, all four read in sequence. The point is the contrast: an unstated both is less dependable than a stated neither.

§4

When neither is the right design

mesh.4.3.md · Abadi's PACELC

The tuned middle is the right design for the surfaces whose stakes are truly intermediate, and forcing them to either pole misserves them. A market-data feed tolerates staleness but not arbitrary staleness; an analytics rollup needs recent-enough data, not exact data; a cache should be fresh most of the time but must keep answering when it cannot confirm freshness.

Push these to the consistency-first end and they over-pay — they coordinate on every read for a freshness their consumers never required, and PACELC says that coordination is a standing latency cost on every request, not a rare one, so paying it where it is not needed is pure waste. Push them to the availability-first end with no bound and they under-serve — they may fall arbitrarily far behind, and unbounded staleness is something a consumer cannot reason about. The tuned middle fits them exactly: enough freshness for the stakes, enough availability to keep answering, and a stated bound so consumers know which is which.

§5

Neither, but stated

mesh.4.3.md · the chapter's candor

The chapter's candor comes down to a single contrast. A budgeted surface is neither strongly consistent nor fully available, on purpose, and it says so — the window is the statement, and the staleness mark is the statement, and a consumer builds on a known quantity. That is the opposite of a system that advertises both properties and quietly delivers neither when a partition or a load spike arrives.

A stated neither is more dependable than an unstated both, because the dependability is in the stating: a bound a consumer can see is a bound a consumer can plan around. The trading strategy's contribution is not that it splits the difference but that it splits it precisely and admits where it landed.

§6

Toward segmentation

mesh.4.3.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012 → M5

This dive leaves one question open, and it is the question the rest of the course turns on. If each surface picks its own point on the dial — the ledger at zero, the feed wide, a tuned surface somewhere between — what holds the system together when its surfaces have chosen differently. The whole guarantees neither strong consistency nor full availability, by the same logic that applies to a single tuned surface, while each part guarantees exactly what its stakes require.

The mechanism that lets those differently-tuned surfaces compose into one coherent system, rather than fragmenting into a pile of inconsistent parts, is segmentation, and it is the heart of the course. The seam that makes it cohere is the branded identity that keeps one entity addressable as it crosses surfaces with different settings. Segmentation is M5, and this chapter's dial becomes one of the per-surface choices it assigns.

The line drawn candidly: that the trading strategy occupies the middle is Gilbert and Lynch's, that the choice is continuous and fine-grained is Brewer's, and that the cost of consistency is continuous is Abadi's PACELC — settled and external. EchoMesh's stance — to state a surface's bound and mark its staleness rather than claim a property it lacks — is design, applied to the budgeted surfaces this chapter builds and separated from the proposed composition at M8.2.
