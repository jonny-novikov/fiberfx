M4.2 · Trading Consistency for Availability

The staleness budget.

EchoMesh's expression of the trading strategy is a per-surface recency window a surface owner sets and the engine holds the surface within. This dive builds the budget — how it is expressed, how the engine keeps a surface inside it, and what it costs — and shows that the cost is continuous, not confined to a partition.

No measurement here: this dive reads a mechanism, not a benchmark. Three primary sources carry it (Yu & Vahdat's conit model, Azure Cosmos DB's bounded-staleness level, and Abadi's PACELC); the mechanisms are external and settled, and EchoMesh's per-surface budget is named as design built on them. The model the budget narrows is M4.1; the argument that a budgeted surface guarantees neither property is the next dive, M4.3.

Proposed · not shipped

The conit's staleness metric, Cosmos DB's version-or-time bound with its throttling cost, and PACELC's continuity are settled external results. EchoMesh's per-surface staleness budget is the course's proposal, built on those shipped mechanisms and composed into the mesh at M8.2; the shipped-versus-proposed line is drawn there. Window settings named below are illustrative example settings, not measurements.

§1

A per-surface recency window

mesh.4.2.md · Yu & Vahdat 2000

A staleness budget is a recency window attached to a surface: a maximum amount a reader on that surface may lag the authoritative value, declared by the surface's owner and enforced by the engine. It is the staleness metric of the conit model of M4.1, narrowed to its most legible dimension and exposed as a knob a surface owner turns.

The budget is per-surface because the stakes are per-surface. An analytics rollup and a settlement check do not need the same freshness, so binding one window to a whole system would over-pay on the surfaces that tolerate staleness and under-serve the ones that do not. Each surface declares the window it needs, and the engine's job is to keep the surface inside it or to make the breach visible — the dial above is exactly this one surface's window, moved.

§2

How a budget is expressed

mesh.4.2.md · Azure Cosmos DB

A window has two natural forms, and the real precedent uses both at once. Azure Cosmos DB's bounded-staleness level bounds the lag between regions by a number of versions — updates of an item — or by an interval of time, whichever is reached first. The two forms answer different questions: a version bound caps how many writes a reader may be behind, which suits a surface where each update matters; a time bound caps how many seconds a reader may be behind, which suits a surface where recency in wall-clock terms is what a consumer reasons about.

EchoMesh expresses a budget the same way — a recency window stated as a duration, a version count, or the lesser of the two — so a surface owner picks the form that matches how that surface's consumers think about freshness. The window, once stated, is the contract: a reader on the surface is never more than the window behind, and can reason accordingly.

A version bound. The lag is capped at a number of updates of the item — a reader may be behind by at most that many writes. This suits a surface where each update matters and being a fixed count of changes behind is the meaningful limit, regardless of how fast or slow the writes arrive.
A time bound. The lag is capped at an interval of wall-clock time — a reader may be at most that many seconds behind. This suits a surface where a consumer reasons in seconds: a moment-old view is fine, a minute-old one is not, whatever the write rate.
Whichever comes first. Cosmos DB's bounded staleness applies both at once and holds to the tighter — the lag stays under the version count or the time interval, whichever is reached first. EchoMesh expresses a window the same way, letting a surface owner state a duration, a count, or the lesser of the two.

Hover or focus a form to read it; click to pin. Without scripting, all three read in sequence. Cosmos DB's bounded staleness uses both bounds at once, holding to whichever is reached first.

§3

How the engine holds the window

mesh.4.2.md · Azure Cosmos DB

Holding a window means acting before the lag exceeds it, and there are two moves. The first is to close the gap by coordinating — refreshing the reader from the authority, or pushing an invalidation that flushes a stale cache, the mechanism the market-data feed of M3.2 already uses, where the authority's write triggers an invalidation that the reader honors.

The second is to slow the source when the gap cannot be closed fast enough. Cosmos DB, when the lag would exceed the configured bound, throttles writes for the affected partition until staleness returns within the limit, trading write availability to keep the window. EchoMesh applies the same logic per surface: within budget, the surface answers from local state at full speed; as the lag approaches the window, the surface coordinates to stay inside it; and if it cannot coordinate fast enough, it either throttles the source or, at the tight extreme where the window is zero, refuses rather than serves stale — the consistency-first behavior of M2. The window is held by spending availability as needed to keep staleness inside it.

§4

What it costs — and why it is continuous

mesh.4.2.md · Abadi, PACELC

The budget makes the trade explicit, and the cost runs in both directions. A tighter window buys fresher reads and pays in availability and latency, because keeping the lag small forces coordination or throttling that a looser window would not; a looser window buys availability and lower latency and pays in staleness, because a reader may fall further behind before anything intervenes. There is no setting that escapes the trade — moving the window tightens one property by loosening the other, which is exactly what the dial above shows as its three bars move against each other.

PACELC is the reason this cost is not a partition-only phenomenon. Once data is replicated, consistency trades against latency on every request, even on a healthy network, because a fresher read requires the replicas to have coordinated first. So a tight budget is paid on the common Tuesday, not only in the rare partition, and a surface owner choosing a window is choosing a standing cost, not an emergency one.

§5

Setting a budget well

mesh.4.2.md · proposed application

A budget is set against the stakes, the way the reservation system of M4.1 sets its tolerance against how full the flight is. Low-stakes surfaces take loose windows: an analytics rollup, a view count, or a market-data tick that is superseded within milliseconds loses nothing by being a moment behind, so a generous window keeps it cheap and always available. High-stakes surfaces take tight windows or zero: a settlement check or a risk limit cannot act on stale state, so its window collapses toward the consistency-first end.

A budget can also be a function of conditions rather than a constant — tightening as a position grows, as the reservation conit tightens as seats fill — so the surface spends freshness only when the stakes call for it. The surface's branded identity is what scopes a budget to the right surface, so a window set for one surface does not bind another.

Where the identity comes from: a window is scoped to a surface by that surface's branded identity, so one surface's budget never binds another. The identity contract itself is the /bcs course, described there rather than restated here — carried under a Proposed · not shipped note until the substrate-versus-proposal line is drawn at M8.2.

§6

How it sits with the two ends

mesh.4.2.md · M2 & M3

The budget is the dial the whole chapter draws, and the two earlier chapters are its endpoints. At the tight extreme the window is zero: the surface refuses to serve anything behind the authority, which is the ledger of M2 blocking rather than diverging. At the loose extreme the window is wide: the surface serves whatever local state it has and marks how far behind it might be, which is the feed of M3 diverging within a bound rather than blocking.

A surface tuned with a staleness budget picks a point between those ends, and the budget is what makes “between” a specific, stated position rather than a vague one. The chapter's three strategies are, seen this way, three settings of one dial — and the next dive argues that a setting in the middle, guaranteeing neither property fully, is a deliberate and correct choice for the surfaces whose stakes call for it.
