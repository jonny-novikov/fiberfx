M4 · Trading Consistency for Availability

Setting the trade continuously.

The third strategy on the menu refuses to fix the dial once. It sets the choice by how out of date data may be — while the network is good a surface stays consistent with good availability; as a partition lengthens, it spends its allowance and must eventually choose.

This landing draws the dial between the consistency-first surfaces of M2 and the availability-first surfaces of M3, and is candid that a tuned surface guarantees neither strong consistency nor full availability. The strategy and its instances are external and settled (Gilbert & Lynch, Yu & Vahdat's TACT, Cosmos DB, Abadi's PACELC, Brewer); the per-surface staleness budget is what this course proposes. The heart of the course — segmentation — arrives at M5.

Proposed · not shipped

The staleness budget is the proposal of this chapter; the mechanisms it builds on — the conit model, bounded staleness, PACELC — are shipped and settled. Its place in the mesh is separated from the shipped substrate at M8.2.

§1

The strategy

mesh.4.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012 · Yu & Vahdat 2000

Trading consistency for availability sets the choice continuously rather than picking one side for a whole service. The dial is staleness — how far behind the authoritative value a reader may be — and the trade follows from it: a tight bound keeps data close to current but forces a refusal or a coordination cost when the network cannot keep up, while a loose bound stays available through a longer partition at the price of a more stale answer.

The canonical realization is Yu and Vahdat's TACT, a toolkit that turns the binary consistency switch into a knob: applications express their requirement as a consistency unit, a conit, whose divergence is bounded by three metrics — numerical error, order error, and staleness — so a surface can demand exactly as much consistency as it needs and no more. The example both the TACT paper and Gilbert and Lynch use is a reservation system: a conit defined over a flight's seats tolerates an approximate count while the cabin is empty and tightens toward exact as it fills, spending less consistency when the stakes are low and more when they rise.

The first dive, M4.1, reads continuous consistency and the reservation example closely — bounded staleness as a unit of trade, and tightening consistency as the stakes rise.

§2

The staleness budget

mesh.4.md · Cosmos DB · Abadi PACELC

EchoMesh exposes this strategy as a staleness budget: each surface owner sets a recency window — a maximum amount a reader on that surface may lag the authority — and the engine holds the surface within it. The mechanism has a direct real-world precedent in tunable stores. Azure Cosmos DB's bounded-staleness level keeps the lag between regions under a configured amount, expressed as a number of versions or an interval of time, whichever comes first, and when the lag would exceed that bound it throttles writes until staleness returns within the window.

The cost is legible in that behavior, and it runs in two directions. A tighter budget buys fresher reads and pays in availability and latency, because the surface must coordinate or throttle to stay inside the window; a looser budget buys availability and pays in staleness. PACELC names why this cost is continuous rather than partition-only: once data is replicated, consistency trades against latency on every request, even on a healthy network.

The second dive, M4.2, builds the per-surface budget and reads its cost — how a window is set and what it spends.

§3

Neither, on purpose

mesh.4.md · Gilbert & Lynch 2012 · Brewer 2012

A surface tuned this way is, by construction, neither strongly consistent nor fully available — it sits at a chosen point between the two, and that is the design rather than a failure of it. The candor the chapter insists on is that the trading strategy does not deliver both properties; it delivers a bounded, stated amount of each, picked to fit the surface's stakes.

Brewer's retrospective is the warrant for treating that as sound design rather than compromise: the consistency-and-availability choice is continuous and fine-grained, made differently per subsystem, per operation, even per datum — so an intermediate point is a legitimate destination, not a way station.

A reader may lag

Within the window, a read can trail the authority. The surface does not promise every answer is current — only that it is no more stale than the budget allows.

The chosen point

A bounded amount of each

The budget fixes the surface's position on the dial. It is not the consistency-first end and not the availability-first end — it is the point the stakes call for.

Not full availability

It may refuse under partition

To hold a tight window the surface must coordinate or throttle, and a long enough partition forces a refusal rather than a stale answer. Availability is bounded, not absolute.

coordinate or throttle · a stated amount

Why guaranteeing neither is sound: a surface whose stakes are low pays for consistency it does not need if it is forced to the consistency-first end; a surface whose answers must be current loses correctness if pushed to the availability-first end. The trading strategy lets each pick its point. The third dive, M4.3, argues this in full and sets up the chapter that assigns each surface its point — segmentation, M5.
