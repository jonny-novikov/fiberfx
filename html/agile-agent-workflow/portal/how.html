The loop, run once

A7 runs the Author/Operator loop once on the Portal, end to end. The roles do not change. The Operator owns decomposition, judgement, and acceptance; the Author is the fast, well-specified implementer. The loop ships one production-grade rung at a time, in dependency order, until the multi-surface Portal is deployed.

The seven steps are the ones the course taught one at a time: A7.01 decomposes the vision, A7.02 plans the roadmap, A7.03 specs the rung, A7.04 briefs the Author, A7.05 builds the increment, A7.06 hardens it to production, and A7.07 accepts and ships. The difference here is that none of them stops at a worked fragment. Each rung is shipped to production quality before the next begins, so the run never accumulates un-hardened work. The ledger above advances one rung at a time; the gate holds at every rung.

Who owns each step

The thesis the whole course argues holds across the whole run: a Claude agent is a fast, tireless implementer of well-specified thin slices; the human is the source of decomposition, judgement, and acceptance. Read the run step by step and the ownership is exact. The Operator owns five of the seven steps — the framing and the acceptance; the Author owns the two that turn a brief into production code. Select a step below to report its phase and its owner.

Ownership is fixed for the run: the Operator frames and accepts, the Author builds and hardens. The agent implements well-specified work; it does not decide what to build.

On the Portal

The Portal is what makes the run a real run. It is one multi-surface system — a branded store, an event-sourced engine behind one facade, a Phoenix web app, a Telegram bot, and a student dashboard — carried from an empty repository to a deployed platform. The surfaces come up in dependency order: the engine before the surfaces that read it, each shipped to production quality before the next is briefed.

The companion Functional Programming in Elixir course builds those surfaces for real, with the OTP internals it cites rather than re-teaches; its F6 Phoenix chapter ships the web surface end to end from a real roadmap. Inside the loop, the increment's boundary parses untrusted input into a typed value — Portal.ID.generate/1 mints a branded id, Portal.ID.decode/1 reads its .type and .timestamp back — and a supervisor isolates a crash so one surface failing does not take the platform down.
