Keep vs ceremony

Most Agile process exists to coordinate a team of people. A one-agent, one-reviewer pair is not a team, so a lot of that process is ceremony here.

A practice is kept if it drives a move in the loop, adapted if its intent survives in a one-pair form, and dropped if it coordinates people the pair does not have. Not wrong — only answering a problem the pair does not have. Switch the view on the right to classify each practice.

The single test

There is one test for every Agile practice in this workflow: does it add a move the Author/Operator loop already needs, or only process around the loop? The test sorts practices into three: keep the ones that drive a loop move; adapt the ones whose intent survives but whose team form is replaced by a one-pair form; drop the ones that exist to coordinate people the pair does not have.

None of this says the dropped practices are wrong. A daily standup keeps a six-person team aligned; velocity helps a manager staff the next quarter; planning poker surfaces disagreement between estimators. They answer real problems — problems a one-agent, one-reviewer pair does not have. Dropping them is not cutting corners; it is removing process that protects against a risk the pair has already designed out.

keep
The practice drives a move in the loop. Short iterations, acceptance criteria, continuous feedback — kept as-is.
adapt
The intent survives, but the team form is replaced by a one-pair form. The retro becomes a per-rung feedback note; pairing becomes the Author/Operator split.
drop
The practice coordinates people the pair does not have. The standup, velocity, and poker — dropped, because the risk they cover is already designed out.
the test
Does it add a move the loop needs, or only process around it? A practice earns its place by naming a move.

The verdict follows from the engine the previous dive drew: a practice is kept when it is part of inspect-and-adapt, and dropped when it only coordinates a team around it.

Kept practices cover the moves; dropped ones do not

The payoff of the sort is concrete. Select any set of practices below. The readout reports how many of them drive a loop move and which moves are covered, and how many are dropped ceremony that add count but no move. Turn on only the kept and adapted practices and every selection maps to a stage the loop runs; add a dropped one and the move-count does not rise — it is process the loop does not need.

This is the test made visible: a practice that does not raise the covered-move count is not earning its place. Trim it and nothing the loop needs is lost.

A practice earns its place by raising the covered-move count. The ones that do not are ceremony the pair can trim with nothing lost.

Keep vs ceremony on the Portal

On the Portal — the learning platform this course builds — the web chapter F6 shipped nine rungs with the kept set and none of the dropped one. It keeps short iterations (each rung is a PR-sized increment), acceptance criteria (each rung carries a Given/When/Then definition of done), and continuous feedback (every rung's iteration line names the feedback asked). It adapts the retro into the roadmap's per-rung feedback column, and pairing into the Author/Operator split — the Author writes the spec and builds; the Operator reviews and feeds back.

And it drops the rest. There is no standup, no velocity chart, and no planning poker in the F6 roadmap, because the second estimator, the staffing forecast, and the team sync those practices serve do not exist for a one-agent pair. Nine rungs shipped without them, and nothing the loop needs was missing.

# F6 — the Portal on the web — ran the kept set and dropped the team ceremony.

# KEEP: short iterations, acceptance criteria, continuous feedback — each names a loop move.

Recap — the sort

The keep-versus-ceremony sort runs on one test: does a practice add a move the loop needs, or only process around it? Keep what drives a move — short iterations, acceptance criteria, continuous feedback; adapt what survives in a one-pair form — the retro and pairing; drop what coordinates a team the pair does not have — standups, velocity, poker. Distilled, Agile is the handful of principles that name a loop move, and the workflow that runs them is the Author/Operator loop.
