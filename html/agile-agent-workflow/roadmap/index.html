The roadmap layer

Decomposition gave you a backlog. A roadmap plans its delivery — thin-but-robust increments, grouped into milestones, sequenced and re-sequenced by the inspect-and-adapt loop. The slice stays the unit; the question becomes the order and size it ships in.

A2 produced a value ladder of small, testable stories. A backlog says what is worth building; a roadmap says in what order, at what size, to what milestone it ships. This chapter is the layer between the two: how to plan delivery without touching the spec, and how feedback re-orders the plan between rungs — all practised on the Portal.

The journey so far

A0 — Foundations framed the method in three questions: why an agile agent workflow works, what we are building, and who does the work. It named the two roles the rest of the course runs on — the human Operator (judgement, decomposition, acceptance) and the Claude Author (fast, well-specified implementation).

A1 — Why an Agile Agent Workflow made the case. Neither no-plan vibe-coding nor an all-plan big-bang ships reliable software; the unit that does is a thin, provable slice. The Author/Operator loop is the cadence that runs one rung end to end, and A1.04 — Two layers drew the line this chapter inherits: the roadmap is how we deliver, the spec is what we build and prove, and they are kept apart on purpose.

A2 — Decomposition produced the work the loop runs on: a backlog. It taught value not tasks, the Connextra form, INVEST, Given/When/Then acceptance, splitting stories that are too big, and ordering the result into a value ladder. The A2.07 workshop ran the whole sequence on the Portal's real web surface to yield its nine-rung F6 ladder — the backlog A3 now plans to deliver.

The principle

A backlog is a set of slices ordered by dependency and value. It answers what is worth building, but not the sequence, the batch size, or the milestone each rung serves.

→

On the Portal

A roadmap.md sequences those slices into thin-but-robust increments and groups them into shippable milestones — pointing at each spec, defining no behaviour, re-ordered by feedback between rungs.

Decomposition answers what to build; the roadmap answers in what order, at what size, and to what end — and nothing in it edits the spec.

The roadmap layer, in five questions

The roadmap layer is a single artifact with a single job: plan delivery without redefining the work. Five questions fix what it is, why it exists, who owns it, when it changes, and how it operates — the deep overview the three orientation dives then unpack.

What
A roadmap.md — the coarse, re-orderable plan of how we deliver. An ordered list of thin-but-robust increments grouped into milestones, each pointing at a spec and defining no behaviour of its own.
Why
To separate delivery from definition, so the spec stays the single source of truth (A1.04) and the order can change under feedback without touching what “done” means.
Who
The Operator sequences and prioritises the roadmap; the Author builds each rung against its spec. The Operator owns order; the Author owns production.
When
After decomposition, before and between building. The roadmap is re-ordered by feedback between rungs, on a different cadence than the spec — which only feedback edits.
Where
It sits over the spec layer and points at it, never defining behaviour. The roadmap references specs; the specs reference nothing above them.
How
Thin-but-robust increments grouped into milestones, run through an inspect-and-adapt loop: ship a rung, demo it, take feedback, re-order the roadmap, build the next.
