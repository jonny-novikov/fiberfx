Chapter F3 · nine modules

The Elixir Language

F2 argued that programs are pure transformations of immutable data, composed into pipelines. This chapter is where that argument becomes a working language: the syntax, the data, and the runtime that make those ideas the path of least resistance.

Elixir is a young language on an old, battle-tested machine. Before the lessons begin, three short readings set the scene: who built it and why, how it has grown release by release, and what actually happens when your source becomes bytecode on the BEAM. Then the nine modules run from the values you type into the shell, through pattern matching and modules, to processes and OTP.

The arc of the chapter

Nine modules in three movements. Foundations cover the everyday syntax: values and the shell, the match operator, and how code is organised into modules. Data & shape are the tools for working with collections and giving data a form — enumerables and streams, structs, and protocols. Concurrency is what the BEAM is for: processes, OTP, and a playground where you watch messages arrive.

Select a stop on the path to see what it covers.

The chapter is one line of reasoning: learn the syntax and data, give that data shape, then put it to work across processes — the thing the BEAM was built to do.

Where this sits

F3 is the third of six chapters. It follows F1 · Algebra and F2 · Functional Programming, which built the ideas as mathematics and as a style; here those ideas become the concrete language. It precedes F4 · Algorithms & Data Structures, where the language does real work, and the later chapters on pragmatic engineering and Phoenix. Read in order, each chapter is a consequence of the one before.
